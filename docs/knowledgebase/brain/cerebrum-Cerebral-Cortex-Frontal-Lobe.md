# Region-Centric Communication Map: Cerebrum — Cerebral Cortex — Frontal Lobe

This document provides a complete communication map for every major region of the frontal lobe. For each region, it specifies what that area communicates with at every level: within the frontal lobe, with other cortical lobes, with subcortical structures, and with brainstem structures — all with layer-level connectivity where applicable.

The frontal lobe is the largest and most functionally diverse lobe, spanning from direct motor output posteriorly to abstract cognition anteriorly. This document is correspondingly comprehensive.

---

## Foundational Reference

**Before reading this document, ensure familiarity with the Canonical Cortical Microcircuit document** (`cerebrum-Cerebral-Cortex-Canonical-Microcircuit.md`), which describes:
- The six cortical layers and their cell types
- Feedforward connections: Layer III → Layer IV of target
- Feedback connections: Layer V/VI → Layer I of target (avoiding Layer IV)
- Layer IV as the input layer; Layer V as subcortical output; Layer VI as thalamic feedback
- Granular vs agranular cortex types

**Critical note for the frontal lobe:** The posterior frontal cortex (primary motor cortex, premotor areas) is **agranular cortex** — Layer IV is thin or absent. This means the standard feedforward rule (Layer III → Layer IV) does not apply in the usual way for inputs to motor cortex. This document addresses these laminar variations explicitly.

## Part 1: Overview of the Frontal Lobe

### Anatomical Location

The frontal lobe occupies the anterior portion of each cerebral hemisphere. Key boundaries:

**Posterior:** Central sulcus (separates from parietal lobe)
**Inferior:** Lateral (Sylvian) fissure (separates from temporal lobe)
**Medial:** Extends from frontal pole to paracentral lobule on medial surface
**Anterior:** Frontal pole (most anterior point of the brain)

The frontal lobe comprises approximately **one-third of the total cortical surface area** — the largest of the six lobes.

### Major Gyri and Sulci

**Lateral Surface:**
- Precentral gyrus (primary motor cortex)
- Superior frontal gyrus
- Middle frontal gyrus
- Inferior frontal gyrus (contains Broca's area in left hemisphere)
- Precentral sulcus (anterior to precentral gyrus)
- Superior frontal sulcus
- Inferior frontal sulcus

**Medial Surface:**
- Superior frontal gyrus (extends onto medial surface)
- Cingulate gyrus (anterior cingulate cortex)
- Paracentral lobule (straddles central sulcus — contains leg motor and sensory areas)

**Ventral (Orbital) Surface:**
- Gyrus rectus (most medial)
- Orbital gyri (lateral to gyrus rectus)
- Olfactory sulcus (contains olfactory tract)

### The Posterior-to-Anterior Gradient

The frontal lobe is organized along a fundamental **posterior-to-anterior gradient** from concrete action to abstract cognition:

**Posterior (Motor):** Primary motor cortex — direct control of movement
**Middle (Premotor):** Premotor and supplementary motor areas — movement planning and preparation
**Anterior (Prefrontal):** Prefrontal cortex — goals, rules, values, abstract reasoning

This gradient is reflected in multiple properties:

**Cytoarchitecture:** Motor cortex is agranular (output-focused); prefrontal cortex is granular/dysgranular (input-integrating)

**Temporal abstraction:** Posterior regions operate on immediate timescales; anterior regions represent extended temporal contingencies

**Connectivity:** Posterior regions connect more with sensory and motor structures; anterior regions connect more with limbic and association areas

**Evolution:** Prefrontal cortex is disproportionately expanded in primates, especially humans; motor cortex is more conserved

### Functional Overview

The frontal lobe serves four major functional domains:

**Motor Control:** Generation and execution of voluntary movement (M1, premotor, SMA)

**Executive Function:** Working memory, cognitive control, planning, rule representation (DLPFC, VLPFC)

**Value and Decision-Making:** Computing expected outcomes, reward-based decisions (OFC, vmPFC)

**Social Cognition and Self:** Self-referential processing, emotion regulation, social behavior (vmPFC, ACC)

## Part 2: Laminar Organization in Frontal Cortex

### The Agranular Motor Cortex Problem

Primary motor cortex (M1) and premotor areas are **agranular** — they lack a prominent Layer IV. This creates a departure from the canonical cortical microcircuit:

**In granular cortex (e.g., sensory areas):**
- Thalamic input → Layer IV
- Feedforward cortical input → Layer IV
- Layer IV distributes to other layers

**In agranular motor cortex:**
- Layer IV is thin or absent
- Thalamic input arrives in Layers III and V directly
- Feedforward cortical input arrives in Layers III and deep layers
- The "input layer" function is distributed across multiple layers

### Laminar Variation Across Frontal Lobe

**Primary Motor Cortex (M1, BA 4):**
- Agranular — Layer IV essentially absent
- Very thick Layer V — contains giant Betz cells (up to 100 μm diameter)
- Layer V neurons give rise to corticospinal tract
- Inputs arrive in Layers I, II/III, and V

**Premotor Cortex (BA 6):**
- Agranular to dysgranular — thin Layer IV
- Thick Layer V but without Betz cells
- Transitional between motor and prefrontal

**Dorsolateral Prefrontal Cortex (BA 9, 46):**
- Dysgranular — moderate Layer IV
- More balanced across layers
- Follows more standard connectivity rules

**Orbitofrontal and Medial Prefrontal:**
- Variable — ranges from agranular (posterior orbital) to granular (anterior)
- Reflects transitional position between allocortex and neocortex

**Anterior Cingulate Cortex:**
- Dysgranular to agranular
- Part of the mesocortical transition zone

### Implications for Connectivity

**Inputs to Motor Cortex:**

When parietal cortex (e.g., Area 5, MIP, AIP) sends "feedforward" projections to motor cortex, these do NOT target Layer IV as in sensory hierarchies. Instead:
- Projections from parietal Layer III terminate in M1 Layers I, II/III, and upper V
- Thalamic (VL) projections terminate in Layers III and V
- This reflects motor cortex's role as an output structure, not an input-processing hierarchy level

**Outputs from Motor Cortex:**

Layer V remains the primary output layer:
- Corticospinal tract (to spinal cord)
- Corticobulbar tract (to brainstem motor nuclei)
- Projections to striatum (putamen)
- Projections to pontine nuclei (→ cerebellum)
- Projections to red nucleus

Layer VI provides thalamic feedback (to VL, VA).

### Layer V Subpopulations in Motor Cortex

Layer V in motor cortex contains distinct neuronal populations:

**Layer Va (upper Layer V):**
- Projects to striatum
- Intratelencephalic (IT) neurons

**Layer Vb (lower Layer V):**
- Contains Betz cells (in M1)
- Projects to spinal cord, brainstem, thalamus
- Pyramidal tract (PT) neurons

This distinction matters: the same "Layer V output" includes functionally distinct pathways to different targets.

## Part 3: Primary Motor Cortex (M1)

**Location:** Precentral gyrus, immediately anterior to the central sulcus

**Brodmann Area:** 4

**Function:** Direct control of voluntary movement. Contains the motor homunculus — a somatotopic map of the body. Origin of the corticospinal tract, the primary pathway for voluntary movement.

### Somatotopic Organization (Motor Homunculus)

M1 contains a complete map of the body's musculature:
- **Medial (top of brain and paracentral lobule):** Foot, leg, trunk
- **Lateral (side of brain):** Arm, hand, face, tongue
- **Cortical magnification:** Hand and face have disproportionately large representation (fine motor control)

The motor homunculus is a distorted representation — body parts requiring fine control occupy more cortical territory.

### Cytoarchitecture

M1 is the archetype of **agranular cortex**:

**Layer I:** Molecular layer; receives some thalamic and cortical inputs to apical dendrites

**Layer II/III:** Pyramidal neurons; receives cortical inputs from premotor and parietal areas; corticocortical output

**Layer IV:** Essentially absent — this is the defining feature of agranular cortex

**Layer V:** Extremely thick; contains:
- **Betz cells** — giant pyramidal neurons (60-100 μm soma diameter), among the largest neurons in the CNS
- Upper Layer V (Va): Intratelencephalic neurons → striatum, contralateral cortex
- Lower Layer V (Vb): Pyramidal tract neurons → spinal cord, brainstem

**Layer VI:** Corticothalamic neurons → VL thalamus

### Within Frontal Lobe

**From Premotor Cortex (PM):**
- PM Layer III → M1 Layers II/III and upper V
- Movement plans for execution
- Both PMd and PMv project to M1

**From Supplementary Motor Area (SMA):**
- SMA Layer III → M1 Layers II/III and upper V
- Internally-generated movement sequences

**To Premotor Cortex (Feedback):**
- M1 Layer III → PM
- Efference copy of motor commands

**From Prefrontal Cortex:**
- Indirect, via premotor areas
- Goal-directed modulation of movement

### With Parietal Lobe

**From Primary Somatosensory Cortex (S1):**
- S1 Layer III → M1 Layers II/III, V
- **MAJOR INPUT** — somatosensory feedback
- Proprioceptive information for movement control
- Especially from areas 3a (proprioception) and 2 (shape)

**From Area 5:**
- Body schema information
- Proprioceptive context for movement

**To S1 (Feedback):**
- M1 Layer V/VI → S1 Layer I
- Efference copy — predicts sensory consequences of movement
- Enables sensory attenuation (why you can't tickle yourself)

### With Thalamus

**From VL (Ventral Lateral Nucleus):**
- **MAJOR INPUT** — driver input
- VL → M1 Layers III and V (not Layer IV, which is absent)
- Carries cerebellar output (from dentate and interposed nuclei)
- Cerebellar influence on movement execution

**From VA (Ventral Anterior Nucleus):**
- VA → M1 (minor)
- Carries basal ganglia output
- More prominent to premotor than M1

**To VL (Feedback):**
- **Layer VI → VL**
- Modulates thalamic relay

**To TRN:**
- Layer VI axon collaterals → TRN
- Gating of motor thalamic input

### With Basal Ganglia

**To Putamen:**
- **Layer V (upper, IT neurons) → Putamen**
- Somatotopically organized
- Motor cortex → putamen → GPi → VL → motor cortex (motor loop)

**From Basal Ganglia (via Thalamus):**
- GPi → VL/VA → M1
- Basal ganglia disinhibition enables selected movements
- Action selection influence on motor execution

### With Cerebellum (via Brainstem)

**To Pontine Nuclei:**
- **Layer V → Pontine nuclei**
- Pontine nuclei → cerebellum (via middle cerebellar peduncle)
- Provides cerebellum with copy of motor commands (efference copy)

**From Cerebellum (via Thalamus):**
- Cerebellar nuclei (dentate, interposed) → VL → M1
- Error correction, timing, coordination

### Direct Subcortical Output (Layer V)

**Corticospinal Tract:**
- **Layer Vb (Betz cells and other PT neurons) → Spinal cord**
- Largest and most important motor pathway
- Approximately 1 million axons per side
- ~30% from M1, ~30% from premotor/SMA, ~40% from parietal (S1, area 5)
- Crosses at pyramidal decussation (medulla) — contralateral control
- Terminates on spinal interneurons and (for some fibers) directly on motor neurons

**Corticobulbar Tract:**
- **Layer V → Brainstem motor nuclei**
- Controls facial muscles (facial nucleus → facial nerve)
- Controls tongue (hypoglossal nucleus → XII nerve)
- Controls jaw, pharynx, larynx
- Some bilateral innervation (upper face); mostly contralateral (lower face)

**To Red Nucleus:**
- **Layer V → Red nucleus**
- Rubrospinal tract — accessory motor pathway
- Flexor facilitation

**To Superior Colliculus:**
- **Layer V → Superior colliculus**
- Orienting movements
- Eye-hand coordination

**To Reticular Formation:**
- **Layer V → Reticular formation**
- Reticulospinal tract — postural control, proximal movements

### From Brainstem (Neuromodulation)

**From Locus Coeruleus:**
- Noradrenergic to all layers
- Enhances motor performance, readiness

**From Raphe Nuclei:**
- Serotonergic modulation
- Motor inhibition, postural tone

**From VTA/SNc:**
- Dopaminergic (minor to M1)
- More prominent to premotor and prefrontal

**From Basal Forebrain:**
- Cholinergic (nucleus basalis)
- Plasticity, motor learning

### Functional Properties

**Movement Encoding:**
- Individual neurons broadly tuned for movement direction
- Population vector accurately predicts movement trajectory
- Also encodes force, velocity, muscle activity

**Motor Learning:**
- Plasticity with practice
- Use-dependent plasticity (larger representation for trained movements)
- Reorganization after injury

**Direct Corticomotoneuronal Connections:**
- Some M1 neurons synapse directly on spinal motor neurons (monosynaptic)
- Most prominent for hand/finger control
- Enables fine, fractionated finger movements
- Primates especially; minimal in other mammals

**Lesion Effects:**
- Contralateral weakness/paralysis (hemiparesis/hemiplegia)
- Loss of fine motor control (especially hand)
- Spasticity develops over time (loss of inhibition)
- Some recovery possible via other motor areas

### Layer-by-Layer Summary for M1

| Layer | Receives From | Sends To | Function |
|-------|--------------|----------|----------|
| I | Thalamus (some); premotor, SMA, S1 feedback | Local modulation | Dendritic integration |
| II/III | Premotor, SMA, S1; Layer V | Premotor, SMA; contralateral M1 | Corticocortical; processing |
| IV | Absent | — | — |
| V | Thalamus (VL); premotor, S1; Layers II/III | Spinal cord, brainstem, putamen, pons, red nucleus, SC | **Primary output** |
| VI | Layer V | VL thalamus, TRN | Thalamic feedback |

## Part 4: Premotor Cortex — Dorsal Premotor (PMd)

**Location:** Posterior part of superior and middle frontal gyri; anterior to the arm/hand region of M1; lateral surface

**Brodmann Area:** 6 (lateral, dorsal portion)

**Other Names:** F2 (in macaque nomenclature); dorsal premotor area

**Function:** Planning and preparation of reaching movements, especially those guided by spatial cues. Transforms spatial information into motor plans. Part of the reaching network with MIP/PRR in parietal cortex.

### Cytoarchitecture

PMd is **agranular to dysgranular** — thin Layer IV, thick Layer V (but no Betz cells).

### Within Frontal Lobe

**To M1:**
- **Layer III → M1 Layers II/III, V**
- Transmits reach plans for execution
- Somatotopically organized (arm/hand regions)

**From M1 (Feedback):**
- M1 → PMd
- Efference copy

**To/From PMv:**
- Coordination of reaching and grasping

**To/From SMA:**
- Movement sequence coordination

**From Prefrontal Cortex (DLPFC):**
- PFC Layer III → PMd Layers II/III
- Goal-directed movement planning
- Abstract rules for movement

**From FEF:**
- Eye-hand coordination

### With Parietal Lobe

**From MIP/PRR (Medial Intraparietal / Parietal Reach Region):**
- **MIP Layer III → PMd Layers II/III, V**
- **MAJOR INPUT**
- Visual target location transformed into reach coordinates
- This is the core **reaching network**: MIP → PMd → M1

**From Area 5:**
- Proprioceptive information
- Arm position for reach planning

**From Area 7:**
- Visuospatial information

**From SPL (Superior Parietal Lobule):**
- Spatial coordinates

**To Parietal (Feedback):**
- PMd Layer V/VI → Parietal Layer I
- Motor-related modulation of spatial processing

### With Thalamus

**From VL (Ventral Lateral Nucleus):**
- Cerebellar input
- Layers III, V

**From VA (Ventral Anterior Nucleus):**
- Basal ganglia input
- More prominent than to M1

**From Pulvinar:**
- Visual spatial

**To Thalamus:**
- Layer VI → VL, VA

### With Basal Ganglia

**To Putamen:**
- **Layer V → Putamen**
- Motor loop (arm/hand region)

**From Basal Ganglia (via Thalamus):**
- GPi → VA → PMd
- Action selection for reaching

### With Cerebellum (via Brainstem)

**To Pontine Nuclei:**
- **Layer V → Pontine nuclei → Cerebellum**
- Reach planning for cerebellar coordination

**From Cerebellum (via Thalamus):**
- Dentate → VL → PMd
- Timing and coordination of reaching

### Functional Properties

**Reach Planning:**
- Neurons active during delay period before reaching
- Encodes intended reach direction
- Activity predicts upcoming reach

**Spatial-Motor Transformation:**
- Converts visual target location into motor coordinates
- Accounts for hand position, target position

**Cue-Based Movement:**
- Particularly active for movements guided by external spatial cues
- "Look at this target, reach to it"

**Conditional Motor Learning:**
- Learning arbitrary cue-response associations
- "If green light, reach left; if red light, reach right"

**Preparatory Activity:**
- Strong activity during preparation
- Diminishes during movement execution (handed off to M1)

### Comparison with PMv

| Property | PMd | PMv |
|----------|-----|-----|
| Location | Dorsal lateral | Ventral lateral |
| Primary function | Reaching | Grasping |
| Key parietal input | MIP/PRR | AIP |
| Spatial vs. object | Spatial targets | Object features |
| M1 projection | Arm/hand | Hand/face |

## Part 5: Premotor Cortex — Ventral Premotor (PMv)

**Location:** Posterior part of inferior frontal gyrus; anterior to the face/hand region of M1; ventral lateral surface

**Brodmann Area:** 6 (lateral, ventral portion); overlaps with 44

**Other Names:** F4, F5 (in macaque nomenclature); ventral premotor area

**Function:** Planning and preparation of grasping and manipulation movements. Contains neurons that respond to object shape for grasp planning. Part of the grasping network with AIP in parietal cortex. Contains **mirror neurons** — neurons that fire both during action execution and action observation.

### Subdivisions

**F4 (more posterior):**
- Represents peripersonal space (near the body)
- Integrates visual, tactile, proprioceptive for near-space
- Defensive movements

**F5 (more anterior):**
- Grasp planning and execution
- Mirror neurons
- Overlaps with Brodmann area 44 (part of Broca's area)

### Within Frontal Lobe

**To M1:**
- **Layer III → M1 Layers II/III, V**
- Transmits grasp plans for execution
- Hand and face regions especially

**From M1 (Feedback):**
- M1 → PMv
- Efference copy

**To/From PMd:**
- Coordination of reaching and grasping
- Reach-to-grasp integration

**To/From Broca's Area:**
- F5 and BA 44 overlap and strongly interact
- Possible evolutionary link: manual gestures → language

**From Prefrontal Cortex:**
- Goal-directed grasping
- Object selection

### With Parietal Lobe

**From AIP (Anterior Intraparietal Area):**
- **AIP Layer III → PMv (F5) Layers II/III, V**
- **MAJOR INPUT**
- 3D object shape for grasp planning
- This is the core **grasping network**: AIP → PMv → M1

**From VIP (Ventral Intraparietal):**
- Near-space representation
- Defensive movements (to F4)

**From Inferior Parietal Lobule:**
- Tool use information
- Action semantics

**To Parietal (Feedback):**
- PMv Layer V/VI → AIP, VIP Layer I
- Motor modulation of object processing

### With Temporal Lobe

**From STS (Superior Temporal Sulcus):**
- Biological motion
- Observed actions (input to mirror neuron system)

**From Inferior Temporal:**
- Object identity (what to grasp)

### With Thalamus

**From VL, VA:**
- Motor thalamus
- Cerebellar and basal ganglia input

**From Pulvinar:**
- Visual object information

**To Thalamus:**
- Layer VI feedback

### With Basal Ganglia

**To Putamen:**
- **Layer V → Putamen**
- Motor loop (hand/face region)

### Functional Properties

**Grasp Planning:**
- Neurons encode grip type (precision grip, power grip, etc.)
- Activity during preparation for grasping

**Mirror Neurons:**
- Discovered in F5 by Rizzolatti et al. (1996)
- Fire both when monkey performs action AND when monkey observes same action
- Proposed role in action understanding, imitation, empathy
- Controversial whether true mirror neurons exist in humans

**Canonical Neurons:**
- Fire when viewing graspable objects
- Encode "affordances" — potential actions the object allows
- Active even without intention to grasp

**Hand-Object Interaction:**
- Neurons respond to specific hand-object combinations
- Pre-shaping of hand during reaching

**Peripersonal Space (F4):**
- Bimodal neurons (visual + tactile)
- Visual receptive fields anchored to body parts
- Defensive responses to looming stimuli

### Mirror Neuron System

The mirror neuron system is thought to include:

**Core regions:**
- PMv (F5) — action execution and observation
- AIP — object affordances and observed grasping
- Posterior STS — biological motion

**Extended regions:**
- Inferior frontal gyrus (including Broca's area)
- Inferior parietal lobule
- Possibly M1 (motor facilitation during observation)

**Proposed functions:**
- Action understanding (understanding others' goals)
- Imitation learning
- Empathy (controversial)
- Language evolution (gestural origins hypothesis)

## Part 6: Supplementary Motor Area (SMA) and Pre-SMA

**Location:** Medial surface of the hemisphere, in the superior frontal gyrus, anterior to the leg representation of M1

**Brodmann Area:** 6 (medial portion)

**Function:** Planning and coordination of movement sequences, especially internally-generated movements. Bimanual coordination. Preparation for self-initiated actions.

### Subdivisions

**SMA Proper (F3):**
- More posterior, adjacent to M1
- Directly involved in movement execution
- Contains somatotopic map
- Projects directly to M1 and spinal cord

**Pre-SMA (F6):**
- More anterior
- Higher-order planning
- Sequence learning
- Cognitive control of action
- No direct spinal projection

### Somatotopic Organization (SMA Proper)

SMA proper contains a somatotopic map:
- Face represented anteriorly
- Arm in middle
- Leg posteriorly (adjacent to M1 leg area)

Pre-SMA is NOT somatotopically organized — it operates at a more abstract level.

### Within Frontal Lobe

**To M1:**
- **SMA Layer III → M1 Layers II/III, V**
- Movement sequence execution
- Bimanual coordination commands

**To Premotor Cortex:**
- SMA ↔ PMd, PMv
- Coordination of reaching/grasping sequences

**From Prefrontal Cortex:**
- **DLPFC → Pre-SMA**
- Goal-directed movement planning
- Abstract sequence rules

**To/From Pre-SMA ↔ SMA Proper:**
- Hierarchical: Pre-SMA plans, SMA executes
- Pre-SMA Layer III → SMA

**To/From Anterior Cingulate Cortex:**
- Pre-SMA ↔ dACC
- Effort, conflict monitoring for motor decisions

**From FEF:**
- Eye-movement coordination with body movements

### With Parietal Lobe

**From Superior Parietal Lobule (Area 5):**
- Body schema for movement sequences

**From Posterior Parietal:**
- Spatial information for movement planning

**To Parietal (Feedback):**
- SMA Layer V/VI → Parietal Layer I

### With Thalamus

**From VL (Ventral Lateral Nucleus):**
- Cerebellar input
- Sequence timing

**From VA (Ventral Anterior Nucleus):**
- **MAJOR INPUT to Pre-SMA**
- Basal ganglia input
- Action selection

**To Thalamus:**
- Layer VI → VL, VA

### With Basal Ganglia

**To Striatum:**
- **SMA proper → Putamen**
- **Pre-SMA → Caudate (rostral)**
- Motor and cognitive loops

**From Basal Ganglia (via Thalamus):**
- GPi → VA → Pre-SMA/SMA
- Sequence selection and initiation

The SMA is particularly dependent on basal ganglia input. Parkinson's disease (basal ganglia dysfunction) severely impairs SMA function, causing:
- Difficulty initiating self-generated movements
- Impaired movement sequences
- Micrographia (small handwriting — a sequence task)

### With Cerebellum (via Brainstem)

**To Pontine Nuclei:**
- **Layer V → Pons → Cerebellum**
- Sequence timing

**From Cerebellum (via Thalamus):**
- Dentate → VL → SMA
- Timing, coordination of sequences

### Direct Spinal Output

**SMA Proper:**
- **Layer V → Spinal cord**
- Part of the corticospinal tract (~10% of fibers)
- Contributes to axial and proximal movement control

**Pre-SMA:**
- NO direct spinal projection
- Influences movement only via SMA proper and premotor areas

### Functional Properties

**Internally-Generated vs. Externally-Cued Movement:**
- SMA: More active for internally-generated movements (self-initiated)
- PM: More active for externally-cued movements (stimulus-triggered)
- Classic dissociation: SMA lesions impair self-initiated but not cued movements

**Movement Sequences:**
- Critical for learning and executing sequential movements
- Piano playing, typing, speech production
- Pre-SMA: Learning new sequences
- SMA: Executing learned sequences

**Bimanual Coordination:**
- SMA coordinates movements between two hands
- Lesions cause bimanual coordination deficits

**Readiness Potential (Bereitschaftspotential):**
- EEG signal beginning ~1-2 seconds before voluntary movement
- Originates largely in SMA
- Reflects preparation for self-initiated action
- Famous Libet experiments on "free will" targeted this signal

**Supplementary Eye Field (SEF):**
- Located in the anterior part of SMA region
- Analogous to FEF but for internally-generated saccade sequences

### Comparison: SMA Proper vs. Pre-SMA

| Property | SMA Proper | Pre-SMA |
|----------|------------|---------|
| Location | Posterior | Anterior |
| Somatotopy | Yes | No |
| Spinal projection | Yes | No |
| Primary function | Execute sequences | Plan/learn sequences |
| Movement timing | Immediate | Extended |
| Thalamic input | VL (cerebellar) | VA (basal ganglia) |

## Part 7: Frontal Eye Fields (FEF)

**Location:** Posterior part of middle frontal gyrus, at the junction of precentral and superior frontal sulci; anterior to premotor cortex

**Brodmann Area:** 8 (and parts of 6)

**Function:** Control of voluntary saccadic eye movements and visual attention. Critical node linking attention (parietal) with eye movement execution (superior colliculus).

### Within Frontal Lobe

**To/From Premotor Cortex:**
- Eye-hand coordination
- Gaze anchoring during reaching

**To/From SMA/Pre-SMA:**
- Saccade sequences
- SEF (supplementary eye field) interaction

**To/From DLPFC:**
- **Bidirectional**
- Attentional control
- Spatial working memory

**To M1 (Face Region):**
- Minor connections
- Head movements accompanying saccades

### With Parietal Lobe

**To/From LIP (Lateral Intraparietal Area):**
- **MAJOR BIDIRECTIONAL CONNECTION**
- FEF Layer III → LIP Layer IV (feedforward saccade plans)
- LIP Layer III → FEF Layer IV (spatial attention/salience)
- Core **saccade/attention network**

**From VIP:**
- Head-centered spatial information

**From Area 7:**
- Visuospatial information

**To Parietal (Feedback):**
- FEF → LIP, Area 7
- Top-down attention modulation

### With Occipital Lobe

**To/From Visual Cortex:**
- **FEF → V4, MT (feedback/attention)**
- Top-down attention modulation of visual processing
- FEF stimulation enhances visual responses in attended locations

**From Visual Areas:**
- Visual input for saccade targeting

### With Thalamus

**From MD (Mediodorsal Nucleus):**
- Prefrontal thalamus
- Executive input

**From Pulvinar:**
- Visual spatial
- Attention

**From VA:**
- Basal ganglia (oculomotor loop)

**To Thalamus:**
- Layer VI feedback

### With Basal Ganglia

**To Caudate (Body):**
- **Layer V → Caudate**
- Oculomotor loop of basal ganglia
- FEF → Caudate → SNr → SC

**From Basal Ganglia (via Thalamus):**
- SNr → VA/MD → FEF
- Saccade selection/suppression

### With Superior Colliculus

**To Superior Colliculus:**
- **Layer V → Superior colliculus (intermediate layers)**
- **MAJOR OUTPUT**
- Direct saccade commands
- FEF can drive saccades even without SC, but normally works together

**From Superior Colliculus:**
- Feedback about saccade execution
- Via thalamus

### Functional Properties

**Saccade Generation:**
- Electrical stimulation evokes saccades
- Neurons encode saccade direction and amplitude
- Activity builds up before saccade

**Attention Without Eye Movement:**
- FEF contributes to covert attention (attending without looking)
- Subthreshold stimulation enhances visual processing at attended location

**Visual Responses:**
- Some FEF neurons have visual receptive fields
- Respond to visual stimuli, especially saccade targets

**Target Selection:**
- Neurons distinguish targets from distractors
- Activity reflects attentional priority

**Antisaccades:**
- FEF critical for suppressing reflexive saccades
- Voluntary saccades away from stimulus

**Smooth Pursuit:**
- FEF also involved in smooth pursuit eye movements
- Tracking moving targets

### FEF in the Attention Network

FEF is a critical hub linking:
- **Dorsal attention network:** FEF ↔ IPS/SPL (top-down attention)
- **Oculomotor system:** FEF → SC → brainstem saccade generators
- **Visual cortex:** FEF → V4, MT (attention modulation)

The classic view that FEF is "just" an eye movement area is outdated. FEF plays a central role in spatial attention even when no eye movement occurs.

## Part 8: Broca's Area

**Location:** Inferior frontal gyrus, left hemisphere (in most people)

**Brodmann Areas:** 44 (pars opercularis) and 45 (pars triangularis)

**Function:** Speech production, syntactic processing, phonological processing. Part of the language network. Also involved in action understanding (overlaps with PMv/F5).

### Subdivisions

**BA 44 (Pars Opercularis):**
- More posterior
- Phonological and articulatory processing
- Syntactic processing
- Overlaps with ventral premotor cortex (F5)
- Contains mirror neuron-like properties

**BA 45 (Pars Triangularis):**
- More anterior
- Semantic retrieval
- Sentence-level processing
- More "prefrontal" in character

### Within Frontal Lobe

**To M1 (Face/Mouth Region):**
- **BA 44 Layer III → M1 Layers II/III, V**
- Articulatory commands
- Speech motor output

**To/From PMv:**
- BA 44 overlaps with PMv
- Action-language interface

**To/From DLPFC:**
- Working memory for language
- Sentence processing

**To/From VLPFC (BA 47):**
- Semantic processing
- Controlled retrieval

**To/From Pre-SMA:**
- Speech sequence planning
- Articulation timing

**From OFC:**
- Pragmatic aspects of language
- Social context

### With Parietal Lobe

**To/From Supramarginal Gyrus (BA 40):**
- **Bidirectional via arcuate fasciculus (dorsal branch)**
- Phonological loop
- Articulatory rehearsal

**To/From Angular Gyrus (BA 39):**
- Semantic aspects
- Reading

### With Temporal Lobe

**To/From Wernicke's Area (Posterior STG):**
- **Arcuate fasciculus** (major white matter tract)
- Dorsal language stream
- Phonological processing
- **Layer III ↔ bidirectional**

**To/From MTG:**
- Semantic processing
- Ventral language stream (via IFOF, uncinate)

**To/From Anterior Temporal:**
- Sentence-level meaning
- Ventral stream

**From Auditory Cortex:**
- Speech sound input
- Via posterior STG

### With Thalamus

**From Pulvinar:**
- Attention to language

**From MD:**
- Prefrontal association

**From VA:**
- Basal ganglia input (speech motor)

**To Thalamus:**
- Layer VI feedback

### With Basal Ganglia

**To Striatum:**
- **Layer V → Caudate (head), Putamen**
- Speech motor loop
- Procedural aspects of language

### Functional Properties

**Speech Production:**
- Converts linguistic representations into articulatory plans
- Coordinates speech musculature

**Syntactic Processing:**
- Processing sentence structure
- Hierarchical phrase structure
- More complex sentences → more BA 44 activity

**Phonological Processing:**
- Working memory for speech sounds
- Articulatory rehearsal (subvocal)

**Action-Language Interface:**
- BA 44 responds to observed actions (mirror properties)
- Possible evolutionary link: gesture → speech

**Broca's Aphasia:**
- Damage to Broca's area causes:
- Non-fluent, effortful speech
- Telegraphic speech (omits function words)
- Impaired syntax production
- Relatively preserved comprehension (though syntactic comprehension may be impaired)
- Patient is typically aware of deficit

### Modern Understanding

The classical Broca-Wernicke model is oversimplified. Modern understanding:

**Broca's area is not just "speech production":**
- Also involved in comprehension of complex syntax
- Involved in non-linguistic sequence processing
- Involved in action understanding

**Language is distributed:**
- Broca's is one node in a network
- Works with temporal, parietal language regions
- Connected by white matter tracts (arcuate, uncinate, IFOF)

**BA 44 vs. BA 45:**
- BA 44: More phonological/syntactic
- BA 45: More semantic

## Part 9: Dorsolateral Prefrontal Cortex (DLPFC)

**Location:** Middle frontal gyrus and superior frontal gyrus; anterior to premotor cortex; lateral surface

**Brodmann Areas:** 9, 46

**Function:** Executive function — working memory, cognitive control, rule representation, planning, reasoning. The "conductor" of cognition.

### Cytoarchitecture

DLPFC is **dysgranular** — moderate Layer IV, allowing substantial input processing while maintaining output capability.

### Within Frontal Lobe

**To Premotor Cortex:**
- **Layer III → Premotor Layers II/III**
- Translates goals into movement plans

**To/From Pre-SMA:**
- **Bidirectional**
- Action sequence planning

**To/From FEF:**
- Spatial attention
- Working memory for locations

**To/From VLPFC:**
- **Bidirectional**
- Cognitive control coordination
- DLPFC maintains; VLPFC selects/inhibits

**To/From OFC:**
- Value-based decision making
- Goal-directed behavior

**To/From ACC:**
- Conflict monitoring → control adjustment
- Effort allocation

**To/From Frontopolar Cortex:**
- Metacognition
- Prospective memory

### With Parietal Lobe

**To/From Posterior Parietal Cortex:**
- **MAJOR BIDIRECTIONAL CONNECTION**
- Spatial working memory (spatial sketchpad)
- Attention control
- DLPFC ↔ IPS/SPL forms **frontoparietal control network**

**To/From Angular Gyrus:**
- Semantic working memory
- Rule representation

**To/From Precuneus:**
- Episodic retrieval
- Prospective cognition

### With Temporal Lobe

**To/From Inferior Temporal:**
- Object working memory
- Visual categorization
- "What" information maintenance

**To/From MTG:**
- Semantic working memory

**To/From Temporal Pole:**
- Social knowledge
- Conceptual information

**To/From Hippocampus (via Parahippocampal):**
- Working memory ↔ long-term memory interface
- Encoding, retrieval

### With Thalamus

**To/From MD (Mediodorsal Nucleus):**
- **MAJOR BIDIRECTIONAL**
- The "prefrontal thalamus"
- MD lesions cause prefrontal-like deficits
- DLPFC Layer VI → MD; MD → DLPFC Layers III, IV

**From Pulvinar:**
- Attention modulation

**From VA:**
- Basal ganglia influence

### With Basal Ganglia

**To Caudate (Head):**
- **Layer V → Caudate (head)**
- **Cognitive loop** of basal ganglia
- DLPFC → Caudate → GPi → VA/MD → DLPFC
- Thought selection, cognitive flexibility

**From Basal Ganglia (via Thalamus):**
- GPi → MD/VA → DLPFC
- Gating of working memory contents

### With Limbic Structures

**To/From Hippocampus:**
- Via parahippocampal, entorhinal
- Memory encoding and retrieval

**To/From Amygdala:**
- Emotional regulation
- Emotion-cognition integration

### Functional Properties

**Working Memory:**
- Maintains information over short delays
- Classic delay-period activity — neurons fire throughout delay
- Capacity limited (~4 items)
- Spatial working memory (locations) — more dorsal DLPFC
- Object working memory (identities) — more ventral DLPFC

**Cognitive Control:**
- Top-down biasing of processing
- Implements task rules
- Overcomes prepotent responses

**Rule Representation:**
- Represents abstract rules
- "If X, do Y"
- Conditional associations

**Planning:**
- Multi-step planning
- Goal maintenance across time
- Subgoal management

**Reasoning:**
- Relational reasoning
- Analogical thinking

**Monitoring:**
- Monitors for conflict, errors
- Adjusts control accordingly

### Goldman-Rakic's Working Memory Model

Patricia Goldman-Rakic (1987) established DLPFC as critical for working memory:

**Key findings:**
- Neurons maintain activity during delay period
- Activity is stimulus-specific (different neurons for different locations/objects)
- Lesions cause delay-dependent deficits
- Dopamine modulation is critical (inverted-U function)

**Model:**
- DLPFC maintains representations "online"
- Allows behavior guided by internal representations, not just immediate stimuli
- Foundation of goal-directed behavior

### Development

DLPFC is among the **last brain regions to fully mature**:
- Protracted development into mid-20s
- Explains adolescent impulsivity, risk-taking
- Reward sensitivity develops before cognitive control
- Implications for legal, educational policy

## Part 10: Ventrolateral Prefrontal Cortex (VLPFC)

**Location:** Inferior frontal gyrus, anterior to premotor cortex; ventral lateral surface

**Brodmann Areas:** 44, 45, 47 (anterior)

**Function:** Response inhibition, controlled memory retrieval, language (overlaps with Broca's area). The "brake" for behavior.

### Relationship to Broca's Area

VLPFC overlaps with Broca's area (BA 44, 45). The distinction is:
- **Broca's area:** Language-specific functions (speech production, syntax)
- **VLPFC:** Domain-general functions (inhibition, retrieval control)

BA 47 (pars orbitalis) is more purely "prefrontal" — not part of classical Broca's area.

### Within Frontal Lobe

**To/From DLPFC:**
- **Bidirectional**
- DLPFC maintains; VLPFC selects/inhibits
- Cognitive control coordination

**To/From OFC:**
- Value-based selection
- Emotional modulation of retrieval

**To Premotor Cortex:**
- Selected actions
- Inhibitory control of movement

**To/From Pre-SMA:**
- Response selection
- Stop signals

### With Parietal Lobe

**To/From IPL (Inferior Parietal Lobule):**
- Attention capture
- Reorienting

**To/From Supramarginal Gyrus:**
- Phonological retrieval
- Language (see Broca's area section)

### With Temporal Lobe

**To/From Temporal Cortex:**
- **Controlled memory retrieval**
- Selects relevant information from temporal lobe stores
- Suppresses irrelevant information

**To/From MTG:**
- Semantic retrieval
- Selection among competing meanings

**To/From Inferior Temporal:**
- Visual memory retrieval

**To/From Hippocampus:**
- Episodic retrieval control

### With Thalamus

**From MD:**
- Prefrontal thalamus

**From Pulvinar:**
- Attention

### With Basal Ganglia

**To Striatum:**
- **Layer V → Caudate**
- Cognitive loop
- Response selection

### Functional Properties

**Response Inhibition:**
- Stopping prepotent responses
- Go/No-Go tasks
- Stop-signal tasks
- Right VLPFC particularly important

**Controlled Retrieval:**
- Retrieving information according to current goals
- Selecting among competing alternatives
- More active when retrieval is difficult

**Selection Among Competitors:**
- When multiple responses/memories compete
- VLPFC selects task-relevant, suppresses irrelevant

**Reversal Learning:**
- Changing behavior when contingencies change
- Updating stimulus-response associations

### Lateralization

**Right VLPFC:**
- Response inhibition
- Attentional capture
- Stopping

**Left VLPFC:**
- Verbal retrieval
- Semantic selection
- Language (Broca's area overlap)

### Stop-Signal and Inhibitory Control

The **stop-signal paradigm** (Logan et al.) isolates inhibitory control:
- Go trials: Respond to stimulus
- Stop trials: Inhibit response when stop signal appears
- Stop-signal reaction time (SSRT): ~200 ms
- Right VLPFC (especially BA 44/45) critical for stopping

**Hyperdirect pathway involvement:**
- Right VLPFC → STN → GPi (hyperdirect pathway)
- Rapid global inhibition
- Faster than going through striatum

## Part 11: Orbitofrontal Cortex (OFC)

**Location:** Ventral surface of frontal lobe, above the eye sockets (orbits)

**Brodmann Areas:** 10, 11, 47 (orbital parts)

**Function:** Value computation, reward-based decision making, emotional evaluation, flexible behavior based on changing outcomes. The "value computer."

### Anatomical Organization

OFC is organized into **medial-lateral** and **anterior-posterior** gradients:

**Medial OFC:** Reward, positive affect, approach
**Lateral OFC:** Punishment, negative affect, avoidance

**Posterior OFC:** Sensory-linked (taste, smell, touch)
**Anterior OFC:** Abstract value, expected outcomes

### Cytoarchitecture

OFC is **agranular to dysgranular**, reflecting its position as a transition between neocortex and allocortex. Posterior OFC (near olfactory) is more agranular; anterior is more granular.

### Within Frontal Lobe

**To/From DLPFC:**
- Value information for decision making
- Goal-directed behavior

**To/From VLPFC:**
- Emotional modulation of retrieval
- Value-based selection

**To/From vmPFC:**
- Strong connections
- Often considered together as "ventromedial PFC"

**To/From ACC:**
- Value signals for effort decisions
- Outcome monitoring

**To Premotor/Motor:**
- Value guides action selection
- Indirect influence on movement

### With Temporal Lobe

**To/From Temporal Pole:**
- **MAJOR BIDIRECTIONAL**
- Social and emotional semantics
- Person knowledge with emotional valence

**To/From Inferior Temporal:**
- Object-value associations
- "What is this worth?"

**From Auditory, Visual Association:**
- Sensory information for evaluation

### With Thalamus

**From MD (Mediodorsal Nucleus):**
- **MAJOR INPUT**
- Particularly medial MD → OFC

**To MD:**
- Layer VI feedback

### With Basal Ganglia

**To Ventral Striatum (Nucleus Accumbens):**
- **Layer V → Nucleus accumbens**
- **Limbic loop** of basal ganglia
- OFC → NAcc → Ventral pallidum → MD → OFC
- Reward-based action selection

### With Limbic Structures

**To/From Amygdala:**
- **MAJOR BIDIRECTIONAL**
- Amygdala → OFC: Emotional significance
- OFC → Amygdala: Top-down emotional regulation
- Value assignment to stimuli

**To/From Hippocampus:**
- Via entorhinal, parahippocampal
- Value of remembered outcomes

### With Sensory Systems

**From Primary Olfactory Cortex:**
- **DIRECT INPUT** (unique among PFC regions)
- OFC is secondary olfactory cortex
- Smell → OFC for odor identification and hedonics

**From Gustatory Cortex (Insula):**
- Taste information
- OFC integrates taste, smell, texture → flavor

**From Somatosensory:**
- Texture, temperature
- Pleasant touch

### With Hypothalamus

**To/From Hypothalamus:**
- Homeostatic state information
- Hunger, thirst modulate OFC value signals
- OFC influences motivated behavior

### Functional Properties

**Value Computation:**
- Represents subjective value of stimuli, actions, outcomes
- Integrates multiple attributes (taste, smell, texture, etc.)
- Computes "common currency" for comparison

**Outcome Expectancy:**
- Predicts outcomes of actions
- Represents expected reward

**Flexible Behavior:**
- Updates value when outcomes change
- Reversal learning: "This is no longer rewarding"
- Extinction: "This no longer predicts reward"

**Sensory-Specific Satiety:**
- Decreases value of recently consumed food
- Promotes dietary variety

**Economic Decision Making:**
- Comparative value
- Choice between options

**Social Evaluation:**
- Value of social stimuli
- Facial attractiveness
- Social reward

### OFC Damage

OFC damage causes striking behavioral changes (e.g., Phineas Gage):

**Impaired decision making:**
- Choices based on immediate reward, not long-term outcomes
- Iowa Gambling Task deficits
- Disadvantageous choices

**Emotional dysregulation:**
- Inappropriate emotional responses
- Irritability, impulsivity

**Social inappropriateness:**
- Violation of social norms
- Poor judgment in social situations

**Perseveration:**
- Difficulty changing behavior when contingencies change
- Reversal learning deficits

**To/From Angular Gyrus:**
- DMN hub

### With Temporal Lobe

**To/From Temporal Pole:**
- Personal/social semantics

**To/From Hippocampus:**

**To/From Parahippocampal:**

### With Thalamus

**From MD:**
- Medial MD especially

**From Anterior Thalamus:**

### With Limbic Structures

**To/From Amygdala:**
- **MAJOR BIDIRECTIONAL**

### With Hypothalamus

### With Brainstem

**To PAG (Periaqueductal Gray):**

### Functional Properties

**Self-Referential Processing:**
- Thinking about oneself
- Self-evaluation
- Personal relevance

**Default Mode Network Hub:**
- Highly active at rest

**Emotion Regulation:**

**Fear Extinction:**

**Moral Cognition:**

- Risky decision making
- Impaired fear extinction

- Reduced self-referential processing

## Part 13: Anterior Cingulate Cortex (ACC)

**Location:** Medial surface, wrapping around the genu (front) of the corpus callosum

**Brodmann Areas:** 24, 25, 32, 33

**Function:** Conflict monitoring, error detection, effort allocation, emotion regulation. Interface between cognition and emotion.

### Anatomical Note

The ACC is sometimes classified as part of the **limbic lobe** rather than the frontal lobe, given its position around the corpus callosum and its limbic connections. However, it is functionally integrated with prefrontal executive functions and is therefore included here.

### Subdivisions

**Dorsal ACC (dACC) — BA 24, 32 (dorsal):**
- Cognitive division
- Conflict monitoring
- Error detection
- Cognitive control
- Contains **anterior midcingulate cortex (aMCC)** in some classifications

**Ventral/Subgenual ACC (sgACC) — BA 25, 32 (ventral):**
- Emotional/affective division
- Emotion regulation
- Mood
- Heavily connected with amygdala, hypothalamus
- Implicated in depression

**Rostral ACC (rACC) — BA 24 (anterior), 32:**
- Intermediate
- Emotion-cognition integration
- Reward-based decision making

### Within Frontal Lobe

**To/From DLPFC:**
- **dACC → DLPFC**
- Conflict signal triggers increased control
- "More effort needed"

**To/From vmPFC:**
- **sgACC ↔ vmPFC**
- Emotion regulation

**To/From OFC:**
- Value and effort integration

**To/From Pre-SMA:**
- **dACC ↔ Pre-SMA**
- Action selection
- Effort-based decisions

**To/From FEF:**
- Attentional control

### With Parietal Lobe

**To/From Posterior Parietal:**
- Attention network
- dACC part of **salience network**

### With Thalamus

**From MD:**
- Prefrontal thalamus

**From Anterior Thalamus:**
- Limbic circuits (Papez)

**To Thalamus:**
- Layer VI feedback

### With Basal Ganglia

**To Striatum:**
- **dACC → Caudate, Putamen**
- Effort-based action selection

**To Ventral Striatum:**
- **sgACC → Nucleus accumbens**
- Reward, motivation

### With Limbic Structures

**To/From Amygdala:**
- **sgACC ↔ Amygdala**
- Emotion regulation
- Fear, anxiety

**To/From Hippocampus:**
- Memory, especially emotional

### With Hypothalamus and Brainstem

**To Hypothalamus:**
- Autonomic regulation
- Stress response

**To PAG:**
- Pain modulation
- Defensive behaviors

**To Brainstem Autonomic Centers:**
- Heart rate, blood pressure
- ACC damage can cause autonomic dysregulation

### Functional Properties

**Conflict Monitoring:**
- Detects when responses conflict
- "Stroop effect" — saying "red" when word is "blue"
- dACC signals conflict → DLPFC increases control

**Error Detection:**
- Error-related negativity (ERN) — EEG signal when errors made
- Originates in dACC
- "Something went wrong"

**Cognitive Control Adjustment:**
- Post-error slowing
- Post-conflict adaptation
- Adjusts strategy based on difficulty

**Effort-Based Decision Making:**
- Integrates reward value with effort cost
- "Is it worth the effort?"
- dACC active for high-effort choices

**Pain Processing:**
- Affective component of pain ("how much does it bother me")
- Distinct from sensory component (S1, S2)
- ACC lesions reduce pain unpleasantness, not sensation

**Emotion Regulation:**
- sgACC involved in mood
- Hyperactive in depression
- Target for deep brain stimulation treatment

### Conflict Monitoring Theory (Botvinick et al., 2001)

The dominant theory of dACC function:

**Model:**
1. dACC monitors for response conflict
2. Conflict signal sent to DLPFC
3. DLPFC increases cognitive control
4. Reduced conflict on subsequent trials

**Evidence:**
- dACC active on high-conflict trials
- Activity predicts subsequent control adjustment
- Lesions impair conflict adaptation

**Alternative views:**
- Foraging/explore-exploit decisions
- Expected value of control
- Prediction error for actions

### Depression and sgACC

**Subgenual ACC is hyperactive in depression:**
- Increased metabolism, blood flow
- Normalizes with treatment

**Treatment implications:**
- Deep brain stimulation of sgACC
- Target for antidepressant effects
- Ketamine may act partly via sgACC

## Part 14: Frontopolar Cortex (Rostral PFC)

**Location:** Most anterior portion of the frontal lobe; frontal pole

**Brodmann Area:** 10

**Function:** Metacognition, prospective memory, relational reasoning, multitasking, branching (maintaining a goal while pursuing subgoals). The most abstract level of the frontal hierarchy.

### Evolutionary Significance

Frontopolar cortex (BA 10) is **disproportionately expanded in humans** compared to other primates:
- ~2x larger than expected from brain size scaling
- May underlie distinctively human cognitive abilities
- Last region to myelinate developmentally

### Cytoarchitecture

Frontopolar cortex is **granular** — well-developed Layer IV. This is the most "sensory-like" prefrontal region in terms of laminar organization, despite being furthest from sensory input.

### Within Frontal Lobe

**To/From DLPFC:**
- Hierarchical: Frontopolar sets overarching goals
- DLPFC implements more immediate rules

**To/From VLPFC:**
- Retrieval of intended actions

**To/From vmPFC:**
- Self-referential aspects of planning
- Personal goals

**To/From OFC:**
- Long-term value considerations

**To/From ACC:**
- Monitoring of goal progress

### With Parietal Lobe

**To/From Angular Gyrus:**
- Relational reasoning
- Semantic integration

**To/From Precuneus:**
- Episodic prospection
- DMN connection

### With Temporal Lobe

**To/From Temporal Pole:**
- Semantic knowledge for planning

**To/From Hippocampus:**
- Prospective memory
- Future imagination

### With Thalamus

**From MD:**
- Most anterior MD regions

### Functional Properties

**Metacognition:**
- Thinking about thinking
- Monitoring one's own cognitive processes
- "How confident am I?"
- "What strategy am I using?"

**Prospective Memory:**
- Remembering to do things in the future
- Maintaining intentions over delays
- "Remember to stop at store on way home"

**Relational Reasoning:**
- Second-order relations
- Analogical reasoning
- A:B :: C:D relationships

**Branching / Cognitive Branching:**
- Maintaining a primary goal while pursuing subgoals
- Switching between goal levels
- "I'm working on X, but I need to do Y first, then return to X"

**Multitasking:**
- Coordinating multiple goals
- Task switching
- Keeping track of multiple ongoing tasks

**Exploration vs. Exploitation:**
- Deciding when to stick with current strategy vs. try new approach
- Foraging decisions
- Related to ACC explore/exploit signals

### Gateway Hypothesis (Burgess et al.)

**Model:**
- Frontopolar cortex acts as "gateway" between:
  - Stimulus-oriented (SO) thought: Responding to environment
  - Stimulus-independent (SI) thought: Internal mentation

**Function:**
- Biases processing toward SO or SI
- Enables maintenance of SI goals while engaged in SO tasks
- Critical for prospective memory and multitasking

### Hierarchical Control

Frontopolar cortex sits at the **apex of the frontal hierarchy**:

```
Frontopolar (BA 10) — Most abstract, longest time horizon
    ↓
DLPFC (BA 9, 46) — Rules, working memory
    ↓
Premotor (BA 6) — Movement plans
    ↓
Motor (BA 4) — Movement execution
```

Each level:
- Operates on longer timescales than the level below
- Represents more abstract information
- Sends bias signals to lower levels

### Key Thalamic Nuclei for Frontal Lobe

**VL (Ventral Lateral):**
- Receives from: Cerebellum (dentate, interposed)
- Projects to: M1, premotor, SMA
- Function: Movement execution, timing

**VA (Ventral Anterior):**
- Receives from: Basal ganglia (GPi, SNr)
- Projects to: Premotor, SMA, Pre-SMA, prefrontal
- Function: Action selection, initiation

**MD (Mediodorsal):**
- Receives from: Basal ganglia (limbic), amygdala, temporal
- Projects to: All prefrontal cortex
- Function: Executive function, value, emotion
- "The prefrontal thalamus"

### Thalamocortical vs. Corticothalamic

**Thalamocortical (ascending):**
- To motor cortex: Layers III, V (no Layer IV)
- To prefrontal: Layers III, IV

**Corticothalamic (descending):**
- Layer VI → same thalamic nucleus
- Modulates thalamic relay
- Approximately 10:1 ratio (more corticothalamic than thalamocortical)

## Part 17: Communication with Basal Ganglia — Summary

### Frontal-Basal Ganglia Loops

The frontal lobe participates in parallel loops through the basal ganglia:

**Motor Loop:**
```
M1, Premotor, SMA → Putamen → GPi → VL → Motor cortex
```
- Function: Movement selection and execution
- Somatotopically organized

**Oculomotor Loop:**
```
FEF, SEF → Caudate (body) → SNr → VA, MD → FEF
```
- Function: Saccade selection

**Cognitive Loop:**
```
DLPFC, VLPFC → Caudate (head) → GPi → VA, MD → DLPFC
```
- Function: Working memory gating, cognitive flexibility

**Limbic Loop:**
```
OFC, ACC, vmPFC → Nucleus Accumbens → Ventral Pallidum → MD → OFC
```
- Function: Reward-based decision making, motivation

### Frontal Projections to Striatum

| Frontal Area | Striatal Target | Loop |
|--------------|-----------------|------|
| M1 | Putamen (dorsolateral) | Motor |
| Premotor | Putamen | Motor |
| SMA | Putamen | Motor |
| FEF | Caudate (body) | Oculomotor |
| DLPFC | Caudate (head) | Cognitive |
| VLPFC | Caudate (head) | Cognitive |
| OFC | Nucleus accumbens | Limbic |
| vmPFC | Nucleus accumbens | Limbic |
| ACC | Caudate, NAcc | Cognitive/Limbic |

### Direct, Indirect, and Hyperdirect Pathways

All frontal areas that project to striatum engage the basal ganglia pathways:

**Direct pathway:** Facilitates selected action
**Indirect pathway:** Suppresses competing actions
**Hyperdirect pathway:** Rapid global inhibition (especially VLPFC → STN for stopping)

**Frontal:** vmPFC, dmPFC, frontopolar

## Part 20: Clinical Implications

### Motor Cortex Lesions

**Contralateral hemiparesis/hemiplegia:**
- Weakness or paralysis of opposite side
- Face and arm often worse than leg (middle cerebral artery territory)

**Loss of fine motor control:**
- Especially hand/finger movements
- Precision grip impaired

**Spasticity:**
- Develops over weeks after acute lesion
- Increased muscle tone, hyperreflexia

### Prefrontal Lesions

**Dysexecutive Syndrome:**
- Impaired planning, organization
- Poor working memory
- Difficulty with multi-step tasks

**Perseveration:**
- Repetition of responses
- Difficulty switching strategies

**Disinhibition:**
- Impulsive behavior
- Social inappropriateness

**Apathy:**
- Reduced motivation
- Especially with medial prefrontal damage

### Specific Syndromes

**Broca's Aphasia:**
- Non-fluent, effortful speech
- Impaired syntax
- Relatively preserved comprehension

**Utilization Behavior:**
- Compulsive use of objects in environment
- Associated with frontal lesions
- Loss of top-down control over action

**Environmental Dependency:**
- Behavior overly controlled by environment
- Loss of goal-directed behavior

**Alien Hand Syndrome:**
- Hand acts "on its own"
- Associated with SMA, ACC, corpus callosum lesions

### Psychiatric Implications

**Depression:**
- Hypoactive DLPFC (left)
- Hyperactive sgACC
- Target for TMS, DBS

**ADHD:**
- Prefrontal-striatal dysfunction
- Impaired cognitive control
- Dopamine modulation abnormal

**Addiction:**
- OFC-NAcc circuit dysfunction
- Impaired value computation
- Compulsive drug seeking

**Schizophrenia:**
- DLPFC hypofunction
- Working memory deficits
- "Hypofrontality"
