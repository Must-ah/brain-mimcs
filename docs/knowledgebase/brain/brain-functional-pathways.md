# Brain Functional Pathways: End-to-End Signal Tracing

This document traces major functional pathways through the brain from input to output. Each pathway follows the actual anatomical route that signals take, identifying every relay point, processing station, and transformation along the way. This supplements the Brain Communication Architecture document by showing how information flows for specific functions rather than how regions connect anatomically.

---

## Part 1: Sensory Pathways to Conscious Perception

These pathways trace how sensory information from the environment reaches conscious awareness. Each pathway has distinct characteristics — different relay points, different crossing locations, different processing stages.

### 1.1 Auditory Pathway: Sound to Conscious Hearing

**Function:** Transform mechanical sound waves into conscious auditory perception, including speech comprehension.

**Complete pathway:**

Sound waves enter the external ear and travel through the ear canal to the tympanic membrane (eardrum), which vibrates. These vibrations transfer through the three ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes) in the middle ear to the oval window of the cochlea.

In the cochlea (inner ear), mechanical vibrations are transformed into electrical signals. Hair cells along the basilar membrane depolarize in response to specific frequencies — high frequencies at the base, low frequencies at the apex. This is the origin of tonotopic organization that persists throughout the auditory pathway.

**Cochlear nerve (cranial nerve VIII, auditory division)** carries signals from hair cells to the brainstem. The cell bodies of these first-order neurons are in the spiral ganglion within the cochlea.

**Cochlear nuclei (medulla)** are the first central processing station. The dorsal cochlear nucleus and ventral cochlear nucleus receive input from the cochlear nerve. These nuclei begin extracting features: timing, intensity, frequency. Output projects bilaterally — some fibers cross, some stay ipsilateral. This bilateral projection means auditory information from each ear reaches both hemispheres, which is why unilateral brain damage rarely causes complete deafness.

**Superior olivary complex (pons)** receives input from both cochlear nuclei. This is the first station where information from both ears converges, enabling computation of interaural time differences and interaural intensity differences — the cues for sound localization. The lateral superior olive compares intensity; the medial superior olive compares timing.

**Lateral lemniscus** is the ascending fiber tract carrying auditory information through the brainstem. The nuclei of the lateral lemniscus provide additional processing.

**Inferior colliculus (midbrain tectum)** is a major integration center. All ascending auditory pathways converge here. The inferior colliculus refines sound localization computations, integrates spectral and temporal information, and projects to the auditory thalamus. The inferior colliculus also connects to the superior colliculus (for audiovisual integration and orienting responses) and to brainstem motor nuclei (for the acoustic startle reflex — a fast pathway that bypasses cortex entirely).

**Medial geniculate nucleus (MGN) of the thalamus** is the auditory relay nucleus. It receives input from the inferior colliculus and projects to auditory cortex. The MGN has three divisions: ventral (precise frequency information to primary auditory cortex), dorsal (multisensory, projects to auditory association cortex), and medial (broadly tuned, involved in auditory attention). Like all thalamic relay nuclei, the MGN receives massive feedback from cortex and is modulated by the thalamic reticular nucleus, allowing top-down filtering of auditory input.

**Primary auditory cortex (A1)** is located in Heschl's gyrus on the superior temporal gyrus, mostly hidden within the lateral sulcus. A1 receives MGN input in layer IV and processes basic auditory features: frequency, timing, intensity. A1 is tonotopically organized — different frequencies map to different cortical locations. A1 is the first stage of conscious auditory processing.

**Secondary auditory cortex (belt and parabelt regions)** surrounds A1. These regions process more complex features: auditory objects (voices, instruments, environmental sounds), patterns, and sequences. Processing becomes increasingly abstract as signals move away from A1.

**At this point, conscious auditory perception exists — you hear a sound. But to understand what the sound means, processing continues.**

**Superior temporal sulcus (STS)** processes voice identity, biological sounds, and integrates auditory with visual information (lip reading, audiovisual speech). The STS is involved in social perception — recognizing who is speaking and their emotional state from voice.

**Wernicke's area (posterior superior temporal gyrus, typically left hemisphere)** processes speech comprehension — extracting meaning from spoken language. Wernicke's area accesses semantic representations stored in temporal and parietal cortex.

**Angular gyrus and temporal-parietal junction** integrate auditory language with semantic knowledge, visual word forms, and spatial information. These regions are involved in reading, naming, and linking words to meanings.

**Prefrontal cortex** receives processed auditory information for evaluation, decision-making, and response selection. Dorsolateral prefrontal cortex maintains auditory information in working memory. Orbitofrontal cortex evaluates the significance and emotional valence of auditory input.

**Total pathway:** Cochlea → cochlear nerve → cochlear nuclei (medulla) → superior olivary complex (pons) → lateral lemniscus → inferior colliculus (midbrain) → MGN (thalamus) → A1 (temporal cortex) → auditory association cortex → STS → Wernicke's area → angular gyrus → prefrontal cortex.

**Key features:** Multiple crossings produce bilateral representation. Tonotopic organization preserved from cochlea through A1. Parallel processing streams for different auditory attributes. Fast subcortical pathway (inferior colliculus to motor nuclei) enables reflexive responses before conscious perception.


### 1.2 Visual Pathway: Light to Conscious Seeing

**Function:** Transform electromagnetic radiation (light) into conscious visual perception, including object recognition, spatial awareness, and reading.

**Complete pathway:**

Light enters the eye through the cornea and pupil, is focused by the lens, and strikes the retina at the back of the eye.

**Retina** contains photoreceptors (rods for low-light vision, cones for color and detail) that convert light into electrical signals. Retinal processing is substantial: bipolar cells, horizontal cells, and amacrine cells process signals before retinal ganglion cells (RGCs) generate action potentials. The retina performs edge detection, contrast enhancement, and motion detection before signals leave the eye. RGC axons form the optic nerve.

**Optic nerve (cranial nerve II)** carries signals from retina toward the brain. At the optic chiasm, fibers from the nasal (medial) retina cross to the opposite side; fibers from the temporal (lateral) retina stay ipsilateral. This partial decussation means each hemisphere receives information from the contralateral visual field (left hemisphere sees right visual field, right hemisphere sees left visual field).

**Optic tract** carries post-chiasm fibers to multiple targets. Approximately 90% of fibers go to the lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN) for conscious vision. Approximately 10% go to the superior colliculus for orienting and reflexive eye movements. A small number go to the pretectum for pupillary reflexes and to the suprachiasmatic nucleus for circadian rhythm entrainment.

**Lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN) of the thalamus** is the visual relay nucleus. The LGN has six layers, each receiving input from one eye and processing one type of visual information. Magnocellular layers (1-2) process motion and coarse form. Parvocellular layers (3-6) process color and fine detail. Koniocellular layers (between the main layers) process color. The LGN is not a passive relay — it receives massive feedback from visual cortex (more corticothalamic than thalamocortical fibers), and the thalamic reticular nucleus can gate what visual information reaches cortex. Attention modulates LGN activity.

**Optic radiation** carries LGN output through the white matter to visual cortex. The superior fibers travel directly; the inferior fibers loop forward (Meyer's loop) before turning back — important clinically because temporal lobe lesions can damage Meyer's loop and cause visual field deficits.

**Primary visual cortex (V1, striate cortex)** is located in the occipital lobe around the calcarine sulcus. V1 receives LGN input in layer IV (which is subdivided into IVa, IVb, IVcα, IVcβ for different input streams). V1 processes basic visual features: oriented edges, spatial frequency, color, motion direction. V1 is retinotopically organized — neighboring retinal locations map to neighboring cortical locations. The fovea (central vision) has greatly enlarged representation. V1 is the first stage of conscious visual processing.

**V2 (secondary visual cortex)** surrounds V1 and processes more complex features: illusory contours (edges inferred but not physically present), figure-ground segregation, texture. V2 projects to higher visual areas.

**From V2, visual processing splits into two streams:**

**Ventral stream ("what" pathway):** V2 → V4 (color processing, intermediate form) → inferior temporal cortex (IT, complex object recognition). The ventral stream flows forward along the temporal lobe, processing increasingly complex and abstract features. Within IT are specialized regions: the fusiform face area (FFA) for face recognition, the parahippocampal place area (PPA) for scene recognition, the visual word form area (VWFA) for reading. The ventral stream answers "what is this object?"

**Dorsal stream ("where/how" pathway):** V2 → V3 (dynamic form) → V5/MT (motion processing) → posterior parietal cortex (spatial location, visually guided action). The dorsal stream flows upward into the parietal lobe, processing spatial relationships and guiding movement. The superior parietal lobule handles reaching and grasping guidance. The intraparietal sulcus handles eye-hand coordination and spatial attention. The dorsal stream answers "where is it?" and "how do I interact with it?"

**Both streams converge on prefrontal cortex:**

**Prefrontal cortex** receives processed visual information from both streams. Dorsolateral prefrontal cortex maintains visual information in working memory and uses it for planning. Orbitofrontal cortex evaluates visual stimuli for value and relevance. Frontal eye fields (at the junction of frontal and premotor cortex) use visual information to guide voluntary eye movements.

**For visual language processing (reading):** V1 → V2 → ventral stream → visual word form area (VWFA, left fusiform gyrus) → angular gyrus (linking visual word to phonology and meaning) → Wernicke's area (comprehension) → prefrontal cortex (evaluation, response).

**Total pathway for object recognition:** Retina → optic nerve → optic chiasm → optic tract → LGN → optic radiation → V1 → V2 → V4 → IT (FFA/PPA/VWFA) → prefrontal cortex.

**Total pathway for spatial/action:** Retina → optic nerve → optic chiasm → optic tract → LGN → optic radiation → V1 → V2 → V3 → V5/MT → posterior parietal cortex → prefrontal cortex.

**Key features:** Partial crossing at optic chiasm produces contralateral visual field representation. Retinotopic organization preserved through V1. Parallel processing streams (ventral/dorsal) for different visual functions. Massive feedback at every stage. Subcortical pathway (superior colliculus) enables reflexive orienting without cortical involvement.


### 1.3 Somatosensory Pathway: Touch to Conscious Feeling

**Function:** Transform mechanical, thermal, and nociceptive stimuli from the body into conscious tactile perception, temperature awareness, and pain.

**Two parallel pathways carry different somatosensory modalities:**

#### 1.3.1 Dorsal Column-Medial Lemniscus Pathway (Fine Touch, Proprioception, Vibration)

**Sensory receptors** in skin (Meissner's corpuscles for light touch, Merkel's discs for pressure, Pacinian corpuscles for vibration) and in muscles/joints (muscle spindles, Golgi tendon organs, joint receptors) detect mechanical stimuli.

**First-order neurons** have cell bodies in the dorsal root ganglia. Their peripheral axons connect to receptors; their central axons enter the spinal cord through the dorsal root and immediately ascend in the ipsilateral dorsal column WITHOUT synapsing in the spinal cord. Fibers from lower body travel in the fasciculus gracilis (medial); fibers from upper body travel in the fasciculus cuneatus (lateral).

**Nucleus gracilis and nucleus cuneatus (lower medulla)** are where first-order neurons finally synapse. These nuclei are the first central processing station. Second-order neurons send axons that cross the midline immediately (sensory decussation, in the medulla) and ascend as the medial lemniscus.

**Medial lemniscus** ascends through the medulla, pons, and midbrain near the midline. No additional synapses occur in the brainstem — this is a direct pathway.

**Ventral posterolateral nucleus (VPL) of the thalamus** receives medial lemniscus input. VPL is somatotopically organized — neighboring body parts map to neighboring thalamic neurons. Third-order neurons project to somatosensory cortex.

**Primary somatosensory cortex (S1)** is located in the postcentral gyrus of the parietal lobe. S1 receives VPL input in layer IV. S1 is organized as a somatotopic map (sensory homunculus) — different body parts represented in different cortical areas, with hands, face, and lips having disproportionately large representations reflecting their high receptor density. S1 processes location, intensity, and quality of tactile stimuli.

**Secondary somatosensory cortex (S2)** is located in the parietal operculum. S2 processes more complex tactile features: object shape, texture, bilateral integration.

**Posterior parietal cortex** integrates somatosensory information with visual and motor information for spatial awareness and action guidance.

**Prefrontal cortex** receives processed somatosensory information for evaluation and decision-making.

**Total pathway:** Receptor → dorsal root ganglion → dorsal column (spinal cord, ipsilateral) → nucleus gracilis/cuneatus (medulla) → CROSS → medial lemniscus → VPL (thalamus) → S1 → S2 → posterior parietal cortex → prefrontal cortex.

**Key features:** Crosses in the medulla (not spinal cord). Fast, large myelinated fibers. Precise somatotopic organization preserved throughout. Enables fine tactile discrimination.


#### 1.3.2 Spinothalamic Pathway (Pain, Temperature, Crude Touch)

**Sensory receptors:** Nociceptors (pain receptors, free nerve endings detecting tissue damage or intense stimuli), thermoreceptors (cold and warm receptors), and some mechanoreceptors for crude touch.

**First-order neurons** have cell bodies in the dorsal root ganglia. Their central axons enter the spinal cord through the dorsal root and synapse immediately in the dorsal horn (laminae I, II, and V). This is different from the dorsal column pathway — first-order neurons synapse in the spinal cord rather than ascending to the medulla.

**Dorsal horn processing (laminae I, II, V)** is substantial. Lamina II (substantia gelatinosa) is a critical site for pain modulation. Interneurons can inhibit or facilitate pain transmission. This is where gate control occurs, where descending modulation acts, and where opioids produce analgesia.

**Second-order neurons** in the dorsal horn send axons that cross the midline within the spinal cord (in the anterior white commissure, within 1-2 segments of entry) and ascend in the anterolateral part of the spinal cord as the spinothalamic tract. This crossing within the spinal cord is critical clinically — a spinal cord lesion causes loss of pain and temperature on the opposite side of the body.

**Spinothalamic tract** ascends through the brainstem without synapsing. Some fibers send collaterals to the reticular formation (spinoreticular component, contributing to arousal and emotional aspects of pain), to the superior colliculus (spinotectal component, for orienting), and to the periaqueductal gray (for pain modulation).

**Ventral posterolateral nucleus (VPL) of the thalamus** receives spinothalamic input in a different region than it receives medial lemniscus input. Additionally, the intralaminar thalamic nuclei receive spinothalamic input and contribute to the affective (emotional) component of pain. Third-order neurons project to cortex.

**Primary somatosensory cortex (S1)** processes the sensory-discriminative aspects of pain: location, intensity, quality.

**Insular cortex** processes the affective-emotional aspects of pain: unpleasantness, suffering. The insula receives input both from the thalamus directly and from S1.

**Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)** processes the motivational aspect of pain: the urge to escape or avoid. ACC is involved in the suffering component of pain and in pain-related decision-making.

**Prefrontal cortex** evaluates pain and guides behavioral responses.

**Total pathway:** Receptor → dorsal root ganglion → dorsal horn (spinal cord) → CROSS (in spinal cord) → spinothalamic tract → VPL (thalamus) + intralaminar nuclei → S1 + insula + ACC → prefrontal cortex.

**Key features:** Crosses in the spinal cord (not medulla). Slower fibers (Aδ for fast pain, C fibers for slow pain). Multiple branches for different pain functions (sensory, emotional, arousal). Subject to modulation at spinal and brainstem levels.


### 1.4 Gustatory Pathway: Taste to Conscious Perception

**Function:** Transform chemical stimuli on the tongue into conscious taste perception.

**Taste receptors** are located in taste buds on the tongue and soft palate. They detect five basic tastes: sweet, salty, sour, bitter, and umami. Different regions of the tongue are sensitive to all tastes (the "taste map" is a myth, though there are slight regional differences in sensitivity).

**First-order neurons** travel in three cranial nerves depending on tongue region: facial nerve (VII) for anterior two-thirds of tongue, glossopharyngeal nerve (IX) for posterior third of tongue, and vagus nerve (X) for epiglottis and pharynx. All first-order taste neurons synapse in the same location.

**Nucleus of the solitary tract (NTS, rostral gustatory portion, medulla)** receives all taste input. The NTS is the first central processing station. Second-order neurons project upward.

**Parabrachial nucleus (pons)** receives NTS input and relays taste information toward the thalamus. The parabrachial nucleus is also involved in feeding behavior and integrates taste with other visceral information.

**Ventral posteromedial nucleus (VPM, specifically the parvicellular division) of the thalamus** is the gustatory relay nucleus. It projects to gustatory cortex.

**Primary gustatory cortex** is located in the anterior insula and the frontal operculum. This region processes basic taste qualities and is the first stage of conscious taste perception.

**Secondary gustatory cortex** in the orbitofrontal cortex integrates taste with smell (creating flavor), with visual appearance of food, and with hunger/satiety state. The orbitofrontal cortex computes the reward value of food — how pleasant or unpleasant the taste is given current needs.

**Amygdala** receives gustatory information and attaches emotional significance — learned taste preferences and aversions.

**Hypothalamus** receives gustatory information relevant to feeding behavior and energy homeostasis.

**Total pathway:** Taste receptor → cranial nerves VII/IX/X → NTS (medulla) → parabrachial nucleus (pons) → VPM (thalamus) → primary gustatory cortex (insula/frontal operculum) → secondary gustatory cortex (orbitofrontal) → amygdala/hypothalamus.

**Key features:** Ipsilateral pathway (minimal crossing). Integration with olfaction in orbitofrontal cortex creates flavor perception. Strong connections to limbic and hypothalamic structures reflect the biological importance of taste for feeding and poison avoidance.


### 1.5 Olfactory Pathway: Smell to Conscious Perception

**Function:** Transform airborne chemical stimuli into conscious smell perception.

**The olfactory pathway is unique — it is the only sensory pathway that does not relay through the thalamus before reaching cortex.**

**Olfactory receptors** are located in the olfactory epithelium high in the nasal cavity. Each receptor neuron expresses one type of olfactory receptor (out of approximately 400 types in humans). Receptor neurons are unusual — they are replaced continuously throughout life.

**Olfactory nerve (cranial nerve I)** consists of the axons of olfactory receptor neurons. These axons pass through the cribriform plate of the ethmoid bone to reach the olfactory bulb. These are first-order neurons.

**Olfactory bulb** sits on top of the cribriform plate. It is actually a part of the brain (telencephalon), not a separate ganglion. In the olfactory bulb, receptor axons synapse on mitral cells and tufted cells in structures called glomeruli. Each glomerulus receives input from receptors expressing the same receptor type. The olfactory bulb performs substantial processing: lateral inhibition sharpens odor discrimination, and feedback from cortex modulates bulb activity. Output travels via the olfactory tract.

**Olfactory tract** carries output from the olfactory bulb to the brain. Unlike other sensory pathways, this goes directly to cortex without thalamic relay.

**Primary olfactory cortex (piriform cortex and surrounding areas)** receives direct input from the olfactory bulb. This is conscious smell perception — the first cortical processing of odor. Primary olfactory cortex is located on the ventral surface of the brain, at the junction of frontal and temporal lobes.

**From primary olfactory cortex, projections go to multiple areas:**

**Orbitofrontal cortex** receives olfactory input and computes the pleasantness and reward value of odors. It integrates smell with taste to create flavor.

**Amygdala** receives direct projections from both the olfactory bulb and piriform cortex. This provides the emotional response to smells — learned associations between odors and emotional experiences.

**Entorhinal cortex** receives olfactory input and connects to the hippocampus. This pathway underlies the strong connection between smell and memory — odors can trigger vivid autobiographical memories.

**Hypothalamus** receives olfactory input relevant to feeding, reproduction, and social behavior.

**Thalamus (mediodorsal nucleus)** receives olfactory input AFTER cortical processing (not before, unlike other senses). The thalamus then projects to orbitofrontal cortex, providing a secondary pathway for conscious odor awareness.

**Total pathway:** Olfactory epithelium → olfactory nerve → olfactory bulb → olfactory tract → primary olfactory cortex (piriform) → orbitofrontal cortex / amygdala / entorhinal cortex / hypothalamus. Also: piriform → thalamus (MD) → orbitofrontal (secondary pathway).

**Key features:** Only sensory system that bypasses thalamus initially. Direct projections to amygdala and entorhinal cortex explain smell's strong links to emotion and memory. Ipsilateral pathway (each nostril projects primarily to the same-side cortex).


### 1.6 Vestibular Pathway: Balance to Spatial Orientation

**Function:** Transform mechanical stimuli about head position and movement into perception of balance and spatial orientation.

**Vestibular receptors** are located in the inner ear, in the vestibular labyrinth adjacent to the cochlea. The semicircular canals (three on each side, oriented in different planes) detect rotational head movements. The otolith organs (utricle and saccule) detect linear acceleration and head tilt relative to gravity.

**Vestibular nerve (cranial nerve VIII, vestibular division)** carries signals from vestibular hair cells to the brainstem. Cell bodies are in Scarpa's ganglion (vestibular ganglion).

**Vestibular nuclei (pons and medulla)** are the first central processing station. There are four vestibular nuclei: superior, lateral, medial, and inferior. They receive vestibular nerve input and also receive input from the cerebellum (allowing cerebellar modulation of vestibular processing). The vestibular nuclei have multiple outputs:

**To spinal cord:** Vestibulospinal tracts control postural adjustments. The lateral vestibulospinal tract (from lateral vestibular nucleus) descends to all spinal levels and facilitates extensor muscles for upright posture. The medial vestibulospinal tract (from medial vestibular nucleus) descends to cervical levels and coordinates head position.

**To eye movement nuclei:** The medial longitudinal fasciculus connects vestibular nuclei to oculomotor (III), trochlear (IV), and abducens (VI) nuclei. This mediates the vestibulo-ocular reflex (VOR) — when your head moves, your eyes move in the opposite direction to keep gaze stable.

**To cerebellum:** Vestibular nuclei project to the flocculonodular lobe (vestibulocerebellum) for calibration of vestibular reflexes.

**To thalamus:** Vestibular nuclei project to multiple thalamic nuclei (VPL, VPI, and others) for conscious perception of balance and motion.

**Thalamus** relays vestibular information to cortex. Unlike other senses, there is no single "vestibular relay nucleus" — vestibular information goes to several thalamic regions.

**Vestibular cortex** is not a single area but a distributed network. The parieto-insular vestibular cortex (PIVC, in the posterior insula and adjacent parietal operculum) is considered the core vestibular cortex. Additional vestibular processing occurs in posterior parietal cortex (spatial orientation), premotor cortex (postural control), and hippocampus (spatial navigation).

**Total pathway for conscious perception:** Vestibular labyrinth → vestibular nerve → vestibular nuclei (pons/medulla) → thalamus → vestibular cortex (PIVC, parietal).

**Parallel pathways for reflexes:** Vestibular nuclei → vestibulospinal tracts → spinal cord (postural reflexes). Vestibular nuclei → medial longitudinal fasciculus → eye movement nuclei (VOR).

**Key features:** Bilateral input (both ears contribute to balance perception). Multiple output pathways for different functions. Tight integration with cerebellum. Most vestibular processing is unconscious — you're not aware of the constant vestibular signals maintaining your balance.

## Part 2: Motor Pathways from Intention to Movement

These pathways trace how intentions become actions. Motor control is hierarchical: prefrontal cortex sets goals, premotor regions plan movements, motor cortex specifies muscle commands, basal ganglia select actions, cerebellum coordinates timing, and spinal cord executes the final motor neurons.

### 2.1 Voluntary Movement: Intention to Muscle Contraction

**Function:** Transform an intention (goal) into a coordinated voluntary movement.

**This is not a single linear pathway but a set of parallel, interacting loops. I will trace the primary pathway first, then the modulatory loops.**

#### Primary Pathway (Corticospinal System)

**Prefrontal cortex** generates the goal or intention. If you decide to pick up a coffee cup, prefrontal cortex represents this goal and maintains it in working memory. Dorsolateral prefrontal cortex maintains the goal; orbitofrontal cortex evaluates whether this action is worth doing.

**Posterior parietal cortex** contributes spatial information: where is the cup, where is my hand, what trajectory is needed? The posterior parietal cortex integrates visual information (from the dorsal stream) with proprioceptive information (from somatosensory cortex) to compute the spatial parameters of the movement.

**Premotor cortex** plans the movement. The premotor cortex (lateral surface) is involved in movements guided by external cues (reaching toward a visual target). It specifies the overall movement plan: which limb, what direction, what sequence. The premotor cortex receives input from prefrontal cortex (goals) and posterior parietal cortex (spatial parameters).

**Supplementary motor area (SMA)** plans internally generated movements and movement sequences. If you're performing a memorized series of actions (playing piano from memory), SMA is heavily involved. SMA is on the medial surface of the frontal lobe.

**Primary motor cortex (M1)** specifies the detailed muscle commands. M1 is organized somatotopically (motor homunculus): hand area controls hand muscles, face area controls face muscles. Large pyramidal neurons in layer V (including Betz cells) send axons down the corticospinal tract. M1 activity specifies force, direction, and timing of muscle contractions.

**Corticospinal tract** carries motor commands from M1 (and from premotor and somatosensory cortex) to the spinal cord. The pathway: M1 → internal capsule (posterior limb) → cerebral peduncle (midbrain) → basilar pons → pyramids (medulla) → pyramidal decussation (approximately 85-90% of fibers cross) → lateral corticospinal tract (spinal cord lateral column).

**Spinal cord interneurons (laminae VII and VIII)** receive most corticospinal input. These interneurons coordinate activity across multiple motor neuron pools, translating cortical commands into appropriate patterns of muscle activation. Some corticospinal fibers (especially those for hand muscles) synapse directly on motor neurons.

**Alpha motor neurons (lamina IX)** are the final common pathway — all motor commands must ultimately activate alpha motor neurons to produce movement. Each alpha motor neuron innervates multiple muscle fibers (a motor unit). Motor neuron firing rate determines muscle force.

**Neuromuscular junction:** Motor neuron axons release acetylcholine at the neuromuscular junction, causing muscle fiber contraction.

**Total primary pathway:** Prefrontal cortex (goal) → posterior parietal cortex (spatial parameters) → premotor cortex / SMA (movement plan) → M1 (muscle commands) → corticospinal tract → spinal interneurons → alpha motor neurons → muscles.


#### Basal Ganglia Loop (Action Selection)

The basal ganglia do not generate movements — they select which movements to execute and which to suppress.

**Motor cortex** (M1, premotor, SMA) sends a copy of motor plans to the striatum.

**Putamen (motor striatum)** receives motor cortical input. Multiple potential actions are represented as competing patterns of activity in the putamen.

**Direct pathway:** Putamen D1 neurons → inhibit GPi → release thalamus from inhibition → thalamus excites motor cortex → selected action proceeds. The direct pathway facilitates the selected action.

**Indirect pathway:** Putamen D2 neurons → inhibit GPe → release STN from inhibition → STN excites GPi → GPi inhibits thalamus → competing actions suppressed. The indirect pathway suppresses competing actions.

**Hyperdirect pathway:** Motor cortex → directly excites STN → STN excites GPi → GPi inhibits thalamus → global suppression. This provides fast, broad inhibition — a "hold" signal before selection resolves.

**Thalamus (VL and VA nuclei)** receives basal ganglia output (from GPi) and projects back to motor cortex. The basal ganglia loop returns to where it started, but now the motor cortex representation of the selected action is enhanced and competing actions are suppressed.

**Dopamine modulation:** Substantia nigra pars compacta (SNc) releases dopamine to the putamen. Dopamine excites D1 neurons (strengthening direct pathway) and inhibits D2 neurons (weakening indirect pathway). This biases the system toward action, especially toward actions that have been rewarded.

**The basal ganglia loop operates in parallel with the primary pathway.** Motor cortex sends commands down the corticospinal tract while simultaneously sending copies to the basal ganglia for selection. The basal ganglia loop reinforces the selected action via the return through thalamus to cortex.


#### Cerebellar Loop (Coordination and Timing)

The cerebellum does not generate movements — it monitors motor commands and sensory feedback, detects errors, and sends corrections to make movements smooth, accurate, and well-timed.

**Motor cortex** sends a copy of motor commands (efference copy) to the pontine nuclei.

**Pontine nuclei (basilar pons)** relay cortical motor plans to the cerebellum via the middle cerebellar peduncle as mossy fiber input.

**Cerebellar cortex** processes the efference copy alongside sensory feedback (proprioception via spinocerebellar tracts, entering via inferior cerebellar peduncle). The cerebellum compares the intended movement (efference copy) with the actual movement (sensory feedback). The output of the cerebellar cortex (Purkinje cells) is inhibitory, projecting to deep cerebellar nuclei.

**Deep cerebellar nuclei (primarily dentate nucleus for limb movements)** integrate excitatory mossy/climbing fiber input with inhibitory Purkinje cell input. The deep nuclei output represents the cerebellar correction signal.

**Superior cerebellar peduncle** carries output from dentate nucleus to the thalamus.

**Thalamus (VL nucleus)** receives cerebellar output and projects to motor cortex. The correction signal modulates ongoing motor cortex activity.

**If an error is detected (intended movement ≠ actual movement):**

**Inferior olivary nucleus (medulla)** receives error information from the spinal cord (via spino-olivary tract) and from the red nucleus. The inferior olive computes the error signal.

**Climbing fibers** from the inferior olive project to the cerebellar cortex. Climbing fiber activity triggers synaptic plasticity in the Purkinje cells, adjusting their response to mossy fiber input. This is motor learning — the cerebellum calibrates its internal model to reduce future errors.

**The cerebellar loop operates in parallel with the primary pathway and the basal ganglia loop.** The cerebellum is continually monitoring and correcting movement, not initiating it.


#### Putting It Together

All three systems operate simultaneously:

**Primary pathway** carries the motor commands from cortex to muscles.

**Basal ganglia loop** selects which action to execute and suppresses alternatives.

**Cerebellar loop** monitors execution and sends corrections to maintain accuracy.

Damage to different components causes different deficits:
- Corticospinal tract damage: weakness or paralysis (especially of fine finger movements)
- Basal ganglia damage: difficulty initiating movement (Parkinson's) or involuntary movements (Huntington's)
- Cerebellar damage: ataxia (uncoordinated movement), dysmetria (inaccurate reaching), intention tremor


### 2.2 Eye Movement: Visual Target to Saccade

**Function:** Rapidly shift gaze from one point to another (saccade).

**Two parallel pathways — cortical (voluntary) and subcortical (reflexive):**

#### Cortical Pathway (Voluntary Saccades)

**Prefrontal cortex** generates the intention to look at a target. Working memory may maintain the location of a target no longer visible.

**Frontal eye fields (FEF)** plan voluntary saccades. FEF receives visual information from posterior parietal cortex and represents potential saccade targets. FEF activity specifies saccade direction and amplitude.

**Superior colliculus (midbrain tectum)** receives FEF input and translates saccade plans into motor commands. The superior colliculus contains a motor map of saccade vectors — different locations code for different directions and amplitudes of eye movement.

**Brainstem saccade generators** receive superior colliculus output:
- **Paramedian pontine reticular formation (PPRF)** generates horizontal saccades. PPRF projects to the abducens nucleus (VI) for horizontal eye movement.
- **Rostral interstitial nucleus of the MLF (riMLF, in midbrain)** generates vertical saccades. riMLF projects to the oculomotor (III) and trochlear (IV) nuclei.

**Abducens nucleus (VI, pons)** controls the lateral rectus muscle (moves eye laterally). For conjugate horizontal gaze, the abducens nucleus also contains interneurons that project via the medial longitudinal fasciculus (MLF) to the contralateral oculomotor nucleus, activating the medial rectus of the other eye.

**Oculomotor nucleus (III, midbrain)** controls most eye muscles: medial rectus, superior rectus, inferior rectus, inferior oblique, and levator palpebrae.

**Trochlear nucleus (IV, midbrain)** controls the superior oblique muscle.

**Extraocular muscles** execute the saccade, moving the eyes rapidly to the new target.

**Total cortical pathway:** Prefrontal cortex (intention) → FEF (saccade plan) → superior colliculus (motor command) → PPRF/riMLF (saccade generator) → abducens/oculomotor/trochlear nuclei → extraocular muscles.


#### Subcortical Pathway (Reflexive Saccades)

**Retina** detects visual stimulus, especially in peripheral vision.

**Superior colliculus** receives direct retinal input (via optic tract). A sudden visual stimulus in the periphery activates the corresponding location in the superior colliculus's visual map, which is aligned with its motor map.

**Brainstem saccade generators** receive superior colliculus commands and generate the saccade.

This pathway bypasses cortex entirely — reflexive saccades toward sudden stimuli can occur before conscious visual processing is complete.

**Total subcortical pathway:** Retina → superior colliculus → PPRF/riMLF → eye movement nuclei → extraocular muscles.

**Basal ganglia modulation:** The substantia nigra pars reticulata (SNr) tonically inhibits the superior colliculus. To make a saccade, this inhibition must be released. The caudate nucleus (receiving input from FEF) can inhibit SNr, releasing the superior colliculus. This is how the basal ganglia gate saccade initiation — action selection for eye movements.


### 2.3 Speech Production: Thought to Spoken Word

**Function:** Transform an intention to communicate into articulated speech.

**Prefrontal cortex** generates the intention to speak and the general message to convey. Working memory maintains the discourse plan.

**Wernicke's area (posterior superior temporal gyrus)** retrieves word meanings (semantics) and word sounds (phonology) for the intended message. This region accesses the mental lexicon.

**Arcuate fasciculus** connects Wernicke's area to Broca's area. This white matter tract carries the phonological representation of the planned utterance forward to the speech production system.

**Broca's area (left inferior frontal gyrus)** plans the motor sequence for speech. Broca's area specifies the grammatical structure, the sequence of phonemes, and the articulatory gestures needed to produce the utterance. This is motor planning for speech, analogous to premotor cortex for limb movement.

**Primary motor cortex (face and mouth region)** specifies the detailed muscle commands for the articulators: tongue, lips, jaw, soft palate, larynx.

**Corticobulbar tract** carries motor commands from motor cortex to brainstem cranial nerve nuclei:
- **Facial nucleus (VII):** Controls lip movements
- **Trigeminal motor nucleus (V):** Controls jaw movements
- **Hypoglossal nucleus (XII):** Controls tongue movements
- **Nucleus ambiguus (IX, X):** Controls pharynx, larynx, and soft palate

**Cranial nerves** carry motor commands from brainstem nuclei to muscles.

**Speech muscles** execute the coordinated movements: airflow from lungs, vocal fold vibration in larynx, articulation by tongue/lips/jaw.

**Basal ganglia** contribute to speech motor control. Parkinson's disease (basal ganglia dysfunction) causes hypophonia (soft speech) and reduced prosody.

**Cerebellum** coordinates the precise timing and sequencing of speech movements. Cerebellar damage causes dysarthria (slurred, poorly coordinated speech).

**Total pathway:** Prefrontal cortex (intention) → Wernicke's area (word retrieval) → arcuate fasciculus → Broca's area (motor planning) → motor cortex (face region) → corticobulbar tract → cranial nerve nuclei (VII, V, XII, IX, X) → speech muscles.

**Auditory feedback loop:** During speech, auditory cortex processes the speaker's own voice. This feedback is compared with the intended output; if there's a mismatch, corrections are made. This loop involves: speech production → auditory input → superior temporal cortex → back to Broca's area / motor cortex.

## Part 3: Reflexes and Fast Responses

These pathways prioritize speed over flexibility. They produce rapid, stereotyped responses to specific stimuli, often without cortical involvement.

### 3.1 Stretch Reflex: Muscle Stretch to Contraction

**Function:** Maintain muscle length by contracting muscles that are stretched.

**This is the fastest neural pathway — monosynaptic, approximately 25 milliseconds from stimulus to response.**

**Muscle spindle** (sensory receptor within muscle) detects muscle stretch. When the muscle lengthens, the intrafusal fibers of the spindle are stretched, activating sensory endings.

**Type Ia sensory neuron** (large, myelinated, fast) carries the stretch signal from the muscle spindle. Cell body is in the dorsal root ganglion.

**Spinal cord:** The Ia sensory axon enters through the dorsal root and synapses directly on alpha motor neurons in the ventral horn (lamina IX) — no interneurons involved. This is the only monosynaptic pathway in the classic reflex arc.

**Alpha motor neuron** fires action potentials in response to the sensory input.

**Neuromuscular junction:** The motor neuron releases acetylcholine, causing the muscle to contract.

**Muscle contraction** resists the stretch, returning the muscle toward its original length.

**Simultaneous reciprocal inhibition:** A collateral branch of the Ia sensory axon synapses on an inhibitory interneuron, which inhibits the alpha motor neuron to the antagonist muscle. This allows the agonist to contract unopposed.

**Total pathway:** Muscle spindle → Ia sensory neuron → alpha motor neuron → muscle. Time: ~25 ms.

**Modulation:** The gain of the stretch reflex is not fixed. Descending pathways (reticulospinal, vestibulospinal) can enhance or suppress the reflex. Gamma motor neurons adjust the sensitivity of muscle spindles.


### 3.2 Withdrawal Reflex: Pain to Limb Retraction

**Function:** Rapidly withdraw a limb from a harmful stimulus.

**Nociceptor (pain receptor)** detects tissue damage or intense stimulus — heat, sharp object, chemical irritant.

**Aδ fiber (fast pain) or C fiber (slow pain)** carries the pain signal. Cell body is in the dorsal root ganglion.

**Dorsal horn (laminae I, II, V):** The pain fiber synapses on interneurons, not directly on motor neurons. This is a polysynaptic reflex.

**Excitatory interneurons** in the intermediate zone activate alpha motor neurons to flexor muscles of the affected limb.

**Inhibitory interneurons** simultaneously inhibit alpha motor neurons to extensor muscles of the affected limb (allowing flexion to occur).

**Alpha motor neurons to flexors** fire, causing flexor muscles to contract and pull the limb away from the stimulus.

**Crossed extensor reflex (simultaneous):** Commissural interneurons cross to the opposite side of the spinal cord. There, excitatory interneurons activate extensor motor neurons and inhibitory interneurons suppress flexor motor neurons. The opposite limb extends to support the body as the injured limb withdraws.

**Total pathway:** Nociceptor → pain fiber → dorsal horn interneurons → motor neurons → flexor muscles. Time: ~30-50 ms.

**Ascending information (parallel):** While the reflex withdraws the limb, the pain signal also ascends via the spinothalamic tract to reach consciousness. But the reflex acts before conscious pain perception — your hand is already moving away before you consciously feel the pain.


### 3.3 Pupillary Light Reflex: Light to Pupil Constriction

**Function:** Constrict the pupil in response to bright light, protecting the retina.

**Photoreceptors (retina)** respond to light intensity.

**Retinal ganglion cells** send axons via the optic nerve. A subset of intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs) containing melanopsin are particularly important for this reflex.

**Optic tract** carries signals past the lateral geniculate nucleus (these fibers don't synapse in the LGN).

**Pretectal nuclei (midbrain)** receive input from the optic tract and integrate light intensity information from both eyes. Each pretectal nucleus projects to both Edinger-Westphal nuclei.

**Edinger-Westphal nucleus (midbrain)** contains preganglionic parasympathetic neurons. Both nuclei receive input from both pretectal nuclei, which is why shining light in one eye causes both pupils to constrict (consensual response).

**Oculomotor nerve (III)** carries parasympathetic fibers from Edinger-Westphal nucleus to the orbit.

**Ciliary ganglion (in the orbit)** contains postganglionic parasympathetic neurons.

**Short ciliary nerves** carry postganglionic fibers to the eye.

**Sphincter pupillae muscle (iris)** contracts, constricting the pupil.

**Total pathway:** Retina → optic nerve → optic tract → pretectal nuclei → Edinger-Westphal nucleus → oculomotor nerve → ciliary ganglion → sphincter pupillae. Time: ~200-300 ms.

**Clinical significance:** The pupillary light reflex tests the integrity of this pathway. Abnormal responses can indicate damage to the optic nerve, midbrain, or oculomotor nerve.


### 3.4 Vestibulo-Ocular Reflex: Head Movement to Eye Movement

**Function:** Keep gaze stable during head movement by moving the eyes in the opposite direction.

**Vestibular labyrinth (semicircular canals)** detects head rotation. When the head rotates, endolymph fluid in the semicircular canals deflects hair cells, generating signals proportional to rotational velocity.

**Vestibular nerve (VIII)** carries signals from hair cells to the brainstem.

**Vestibular nuclei (pons and medulla)** receive vestibular nerve input and calculate the compensatory eye movement needed to stabilize gaze.

**Medial longitudinal fasciculus (MLF)** connects vestibular nuclei to eye movement nuclei.

**Abducens nucleus (VI) and oculomotor nucleus (III)** receive vestibular input via the MLF. For horizontal head rotation:
- Rotation to the right activates the right lateral rectus (via right abducens) and left medial rectus (via left oculomotor), moving the eyes to the left
- Rotation to the left activates the opposite muscles, moving the eyes to the right

**Extraocular muscles** execute the compensatory eye movement. The eyes move in the direction opposite to head rotation, keeping gaze fixed on the target.

**Total pathway:** Semicircular canal → vestibular nerve → vestibular nuclei → MLF → abducens and oculomotor nuclei → extraocular muscles. Time: ~10-15 ms (one of the fastest reflexes).

**Cerebellar calibration:** The vestibulocerebellum (flocculonodular lobe) receives vestibular and visual input and calibrates the VOR gain. If the VOR is inaccurate (images slip during head movement), the cerebellum adjusts the reflex.


### 3.5 Acoustic Startle Reflex: Loud Sound to Whole-Body Flinch

**Function:** Produce a rapid defensive response to sudden loud sounds.

**Cochlea** transduces the loud sound into neural signals.

**Cochlear nerve** carries auditory signals to the brainstem.

**Cochlear nuclei (medulla)** receive auditory input and relay it to the startle circuit.

**Nucleus reticularis pontis caudalis (PnC, pons)** is the critical relay for the startle response. It receives auditory input and projects to motor neurons.

**Reticulospinal tract** carries the startle command down the spinal cord.

**Motor neurons** throughout the spinal cord are activated, producing widespread muscle contraction — a whole-body flinch.

**Total pathway:** Cochlea → cochlear nuclei → PnC → reticulospinal tract → motor neurons → muscles. Time: ~30-40 ms for first muscle activation.

**Modulation:** The startle reflex can be modulated by emotional state. The amygdala potentiates the startle reflex during fear (fear-potentiated startle). Prepulse inhibition (a weak stimulus preceding the loud sound reduces the startle) is mediated by the pedunculopontine nucleus.

## Part 4: Emotional Pathways

These pathways trace how emotions are generated, experienced, and expressed.

### 4.1 Fear Response: Threat Detection to Defensive Behavior

**Function:** Detect potential threats and generate appropriate defensive responses.

**Two parallel pathways — fast (subcortical) and slow (cortical):**

#### Fast Pathway (Low Road)

**Sensory receptor** (any modality) detects potentially threatening stimulus.

**Thalamus (sensory relay nuclei)** receives sensory input. The thalamus sends a DIRECT projection to the amygdala — a fast, crude signal that bypasses cortical processing.

**Amygdala (lateral nucleus)** receives this direct thalamic input. The signal is processed quickly but imprecisely. The amygdala can initiate a fear response before detailed cortical analysis determines exactly what the stimulus is.

**Amygdala (central nucleus)** generates outputs that produce the fear response:
- → **Hypothalamus (paraventricular nucleus):** Activates the HPA axis (hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal), releasing cortisol for sustained stress response
- → **Lateral hypothalamus:** Activates sympathetic nervous system (increased heart rate, blood pressure, sweating)
- → **Periaqueductal gray (PAG):** Generates behavioral responses (freezing from ventrolateral PAG, fight/flight from dorsolateral PAG)
- → **Parabrachial nucleus:** Modulates respiration (gasping, hyperventilation)
- → **Nucleus reticularis pontis caudalis:** Potentiates startle reflex
- → **Locus coeruleus:** Increases norepinephrine release, enhancing arousal and attention

**This pathway operates in approximately 12-15 milliseconds from stimulus to amygdala response** — fast enough to initiate defensive behavior before conscious perception.


#### Slow Pathway (High Road)

**Thalamus** sends sensory input to the appropriate cortical area (visual to V1, auditory to A1, etc.).

**Sensory cortex** processes the stimulus in detail, identifying exactly what it is.

**Association cortex** integrates the sensory information with context and memory.

**Hippocampus** provides contextual information — is this a safe or dangerous situation? The same stimulus might be threatening in one context and harmless in another.

**Prefrontal cortex** evaluates the stimulus, considering goals and consequences.

**Amygdala (basal nucleus)** receives detailed, processed information from cortex and hippocampus. This input can confirm or override the initial fast-pathway response.

**The slow pathway takes several hundred milliseconds** but provides accurate identification and context-appropriate responses.


#### Interaction of Pathways

The fast pathway generates an immediate defensive response. The slow pathway can then modulate this response based on detailed analysis. If cortical processing determines the stimulus is harmless, prefrontal cortex sends inhibitory input to the amygdala, suppressing the fear response. If cortical processing confirms the threat, the fear response continues and intensifies.

**Example:** You see a curved shape in the grass (fast pathway activates — possible snake → freeze, heart rate increases). Detailed visual analysis identifies it as a stick (slow pathway). Prefrontal cortex inhibits amygdala → fear response diminishes.


### 4.2 Reward and Motivation: Reward to Behavioral Change

**Function:** Detect rewards, generate pleasure, and modify behavior to seek future rewards.

**Unexpected reward occurs** (food when hungry, social approval, drug of abuse, etc.).

**Ventral tegmental area (VTA, midbrain)** dopamine neurons detect the reward prediction error — the difference between expected and received reward. For unexpected rewards, VTA neurons fire phasically (burst of activity).

**Dopamine release** occurs in two major targets:

**Nucleus accumbens (ventral striatum)** receives mesolimbic dopamine. Dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens produces the "wanting" component of reward — the motivation to pursue rewards. The nucleus accumbens integrates reward information with motor plans via its connections with the basal ganglia motor loop. Dopamine in the nucleus accumbens strengthens synaptic connections for the action that preceded the reward, making that action more likely to occur in the future.

**Prefrontal cortex** receives mesocortical dopamine. Dopamine in prefrontal cortex modulates working memory and decision-making, biasing decisions toward rewarded options. Orbitofrontal cortex updates value representations: this stimulus/action is now associated with reward.

**Amygdala** receives information about the reward and associates it with the stimuli and context present when the reward occurred. This enables conditioned responses — stimuli that predict reward become rewarding themselves.

**Hippocampus** encodes the episodic memory of the rewarding experience — what happened, where, when.


#### Learning from Reward

**With repeated experience:**

**VTA dopamine response transfers** from the reward itself to the earliest reliable predictor of reward. If a tone consistently precedes food, VTA neurons eventually fire to the tone rather than to the food.

**Expected rewards produce no dopamine response.** When the reward is fully predicted, VTA neurons show no change from baseline.

**Omitted expected rewards produce a dopamine pause.** When an expected reward fails to occur, VTA neurons briefly stop firing (negative prediction error). This dopamine pause signals "worse than expected" and weakens the associations that predicted the reward.

This pattern — increase for better than expected, no change for expected, decrease for worse than expected — is exactly the temporal difference (TD) learning signal used in reinforcement learning algorithms.


### 4.3 Pain Pathway: Tissue Damage to Suffering

**Function:** Transform tissue damage into conscious pain experience with its sensory, emotional, and motivational components.

**Pain has multiple dimensions, each processed by different pathways:**

#### Sensory-Discriminative Pathway (Where and What)

**Nociceptors** detect tissue damage through thermal, mechanical, or chemical stimuli.

**Aδ fibers (fast pain, sharp) and C fibers (slow pain, burning)** carry signals to the spinal cord.

**Dorsal horn (laminae I, II, V)** processes incoming nociceptive signals. Substantial modulation occurs here — gate control, descending inhibition.

**Spinothalamic tract** carries pain signals, after crossing in the spinal cord, to the thalamus.

**VPL thalamus** relays pain information to cortex.

**Primary somatosensory cortex (S1)** processes the sensory-discriminative aspects: where is the pain, how intense, what quality (burning, sharp, aching).


#### Affective-Emotional Pathway (Unpleasantness)

**Spinothalamic tract** also projects to the intralaminar thalamic nuclei and to the parabrachial nucleus.

**Intralaminar thalamic nuclei** project broadly to cortex and are involved in the arousing, attention-grabbing aspect of pain.

**Parabrachial nucleus (pons)** projects to the amygdala and hypothalamus.

**Amygdala** generates the emotional response to pain — fear, anxiety, aversion.

**Insular cortex** processes the subjective feeling of pain — the interoceptive awareness of bodily distress. The anterior insula is particularly involved in the affective component.

**Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)** processes the "suffering" component of pain — the unpleasantness, the urge to escape. The ACC is critical for the motivational aspect: pain motivates behavior to escape the cause.


#### Descending Modulation Pathway (Pain Control)

**Prefrontal cortex** can initiate pain suppression based on cognitive factors (attention, expectation, perceived control).

**Amygdala** can enhance or suppress pain based on emotional state.

**Periaqueductal gray (PAG, midbrain)** is the master control center for descending pain modulation. PAG receives input from prefrontal cortex, amygdala, and hypothalamus.

**Rostral ventromedial medulla (RVM)** receives PAG input and projects to the spinal cord dorsal horn.

**Spinal cord dorsal horn:** Descending fibers from RVM release serotonin and norepinephrine that activate inhibitory interneurons, suppressing pain transmission. This is how stress-induced analgesia works — during emergency situations, PAG activation suppresses pain to allow continued action.

**Endogenous opioids** (endorphins, enkephalins) are released in the PAG, RVM, and spinal cord during pain suppression. They act on opioid receptors to inhibit pain transmission.

## Part 5: Memory Pathways

These pathways trace how experiences become memories and how memories are retrieved.

### 5.1 Memory Encoding: Experience to Stored Memory

**Function:** Transform an ongoing experience into a lasting memory trace.

**Sensory cortices** (visual, auditory, somatosensory, etc.) process the incoming experience. These distributed cortical representations are the raw material of memory.

**Attention and significance:** For an experience to be encoded into long-term memory, it must receive attention and/or be emotionally significant. Prefrontal cortex directs attention; amygdala signals emotional significance.

**Entorhinal cortex (medial temporal lobe)** serves as the gateway to the hippocampus. Entorhinal cortex receives convergent input from widespread cortical areas — visual, auditory, somatosensory, and association cortices. It transforms distributed cortical representations into a format the hippocampus can process.

**Perforant path** carries entorhinal cortex output to the dentate gyrus.

**Dentate gyrus** performs pattern separation. Similar experiences (today's breakfast vs. yesterday's breakfast) are transformed into non-overlapping representations to prevent interference. The dentate gyrus has sparse activity — only a small fraction of neurons fire for any given input, ensuring distinct representations. (The dentate gyrus may also generate new neurons throughout life — adult neurogenesis — though this is debated in humans.)

**Mossy fibers** carry dentate gyrus output to CA3.

**CA3** has extensive recurrent connections — neurons in CA3 connect to each other, forming an autoassociative network. This network can store patterns (via Hebbian learning) and later complete partial patterns. CA3 creates rapid, one-shot associations: the elements of an experience are bound together.

**Schaffer collaterals** carry CA3 output to CA1.

**CA1** processes temporal order — when things happened in sequence — and integrates information from CA3 with direct input from entorhinal cortex.

**Subiculum** receives CA1 output and is the main output structure of the hippocampus.

**Output from hippocampus:**
- → **Back to entorhinal cortex** (completing the loop)
- → **Mammillary bodies** (via fornix, part of Papez circuit)
- → **Anterior thalamus** (via mammillothalamic tract)
- → **Prefrontal cortex** (via direct projections and via anterior thalamus)


#### Consolidation: From Hippocampus to Cortex

Initially, memories depend on the hippocampus. Over time (days to years), memories become independent of the hippocampus and are stored in cortical networks.

**During sleep (especially slow-wave sleep):**

**Sharp-wave ripples** occur in the hippocampus — brief bursts of activity where recent experiences are "replayed" at high speed.

**Thalamocortical spindles** (generated by the thalamic reticular nucleus) coincide with hippocampal ripples.

**Slow oscillations** in the cortex coordinate ripples and spindles, creating windows for synaptic plasticity.

**Repeated replay** gradually strengthens cortical connections, transferring the memory trace from hippocampus to cortex. This is systems consolidation.

**After consolidation,** the memory can be retrieved by cortical activity alone, without hippocampal involvement. Very old memories (remote memories) survive hippocampal damage.


### 5.2 Memory Retrieval: Cue to Remembered Experience

**Function:** Use a cue to reactivate a stored memory.

**Retrieval cue** (a word, an image, a smell, a question) is processed by sensory and association cortex.

**Prefrontal cortex** initiates retrieval — the effort to remember. Dorsolateral prefrontal cortex maintains the retrieval goal; ventrolateral prefrontal cortex selects among competing memories.

**For recent, hippocampus-dependent memories:**

**Entorhinal cortex** receives the retrieval cue (processed by cortex) and projects to the hippocampus.

**CA3** receives input and performs pattern completion. A partial cue activates a subset of the stored pattern; recurrent connections activate the complete pattern. This is how a fragment can trigger a complete memory.

**CA1 and subiculum** output the completed pattern back to entorhinal cortex.

**Entorhinal cortex** projects back to cortex, reactivating the distributed cortical representation of the original experience.

**Cortical reactivation** is the retrieved memory — the reconstructed experience in sensory and association cortex.


**For remote, cortex-dependent memories:**

After consolidation, retrieval does not require hippocampus. Cortical networks can reactivate the memory directly.

**Retrieval cue** activates associated cortical neurons.

**Cortical pattern completion** occurs through cortico-cortical connections — the stored pattern reactivates without hippocampal involvement.

**Prefrontal cortex** monitors retrieval, evaluating whether the retrieved memory matches the retrieval goal.


#### Reconsolidation

When a memory is retrieved, it enters a labile (unstable) state and must be reconsolidated. During this window:

**The memory can be modified** — new information can be incorporated, emotional valence can change.

**The memory can be weakened** — blocking reconsolidation (e.g., with certain drugs) can impair the memory.

This has therapeutic implications: traumatic memories might be modified during reconsolidation to reduce their emotional impact.

## Part 6: Decision-Making Pathways

These pathways trace how the brain selects among options and commits to action.

### 6.1 Perceptual Decision: Sensory Evidence to Choice

**Function:** Decide which of several interpretations fits the sensory evidence (e.g., is this a face or not? Is the motion leftward or rightward?).

**Sensory cortex** processes the incoming stimulus. For motion discrimination, area MT/V5 represents motion direction. The neural representation is noisy — it fluctuates over time.

**Posterior parietal cortex (lateral intraparietal area, LIP)** accumulates evidence over time. Neurons in LIP show "ramping" activity — their firing rate gradually increases as evidence favors one option. This is neural evidence accumulation, analogous to the drift-diffusion model of decision-making.

**When accumulated evidence reaches a threshold** (represented by a certain firing rate level), the decision is made.

**Prefrontal cortex** may set the threshold — higher thresholds produce slower but more accurate decisions (speed-accuracy tradeoff).

**Motor cortex / eye movement areas** receive the decision and execute the response (e.g., a saccade indicating the perceived direction).

**Dopamine (from VTA)** may signal confidence or reward prediction, modulating the decision process.

**Total pathway:** Sensory cortex (evidence) → posterior parietal cortex (accumulation) → threshold reached → motor response.


### 6.2 Value-Based Decision: Options to Chosen Action

**Function:** Choose among options based on their subjective value (e.g., apple vs. orange, job A vs. job B).

**Options are represented** in relevant cortical areas. If deciding between foods, visual cortex represents their appearance, gustatory cortex represents their taste, etc.

**Orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)** computes subjective value for each option. OFC integrates multiple factors: sensory properties, past outcomes, current needs (hunger level affects food value), probability of obtaining the outcome. OFC neurons encode value on a common scale, allowing comparison of different types of rewards.

**Ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC)** integrates value with self-relevant information and emotional significance. vmPFC and OFC work together in value computation.

**Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)** monitors decision difficulty. When options are close in value, ACC signals "conflict" — more processing is needed. ACC also tracks effort costs and opportunity costs.

**Dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC)** maintains goals and applies rules. If there's a rule (e.g., "I'm trying to eat healthier"), DLPFC can override value signals from OFC.

**Value comparison** likely occurs through competition. Options with higher value have stronger representations; the option with the strongest representation "wins."

**Basal ganglia (ventral striatum and related loops)** implement the competition and selection, as in motor action selection. The option that wins the competition is selected for action.

**Dopamine (from VTA)** encodes reward prediction error. If the choice leads to better outcomes than expected, dopamine increases and strengthens the choice tendency. If worse than expected, dopamine decreases and weakens the choice tendency.

**Total pathway:** Option representations → OFC/vmPFC (value computation) → ACC (conflict monitoring) → DLPFC (goal maintenance, rule application) → basal ganglia (selection) → motor output.


### 6.3 Go/No-Go Decision: Act or Withhold

**Function:** Decide whether to execute or withhold an action (e.g., should I reach for that cookie?).

This decision relies heavily on basal ganglia circuitry:

**Cortical representation** of the potential action exists (reaching for the cookie).

**Prefrontal cortex** evaluates whether the action is appropriate given goals (I'm on a diet) and context (social setting).

**Direct pathway (GO):** If prefrontal/motor cortex strongly activates D1 neurons in striatum → GPi inhibition → thalamus released → action executed.

**Indirect pathway (NO-GO):** If prefrontal cortex activates D2 neurons in striatum → GPe inhibition → STN released → GPi activated → thalamus inhibited → action withheld.

**Hyperdirect pathway (STOP):** If prefrontal cortex (particularly right inferior frontal gyrus) directly activates STN → GPi activated → thalamus inhibited → action aborted, even if already initiated.

**Balance determines outcome:** The relative activation of direct vs. indirect pathways determines whether the action occurs. Dopamine biases toward GO (D1 excitation, D2 inhibition).

**Impulsivity** (acting without sufficient consideration) may reflect weak indirect pathway or hyperdirect pathway function.

**Compulsivity** (inability to stop actions) may reflect overactive direct pathway or damaged stop circuits.

## Part 7: Arousal and State Pathways

These pathways trace how global brain state is set and modulated.

### 7.1 Sleep-Wake Transition: Asleep to Awake

**Function:** Transition the brain from sleep state to wake state.

**During sleep,** thalamic relay neurons are in "burst mode" — they fire in bursts that prevent faithful transmission of sensory information. Cortex shows slow waves. Consciousness of the external world is absent.

**Waking stimulus** (alarm clock, sunrise, internal circadian signal) initiates the wake transition.

**Suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN, hypothalamus)** is the master circadian clock. SCN activity varies with time of day, promoting wakefulness during the day (in diurnal animals) and sleep at night. SCN projects to other hypothalamic areas.

**Lateral hypothalamus** contains orexin (hypocretin) neurons. When active, orexin neurons powerfully promote wakefulness by exciting the arousal nuclei. Loss of orexin neurons causes narcolepsy.

**Arousal nuclei** are activated by orexin and other inputs:
- **Locus coeruleus (pons):** Norepinephrine to cortex and thalamus
- **Raphe nuclei (brainstem):** Serotonin to cortex and thalamus
- **Basal forebrain:** Acetylcholine to cortex and hippocampus
- **Tuberomammillary nucleus (hypothalamus):** Histamine to cortex and thalamus
- **Ventral tegmental area (midbrain):** Dopamine to prefrontal cortex and striatum

**These neuromodulators act on thalamus and cortex:**

**Thalamus:** Neuromodulators depolarize relay neurons, shifting them from burst mode to "tonic mode" — faithful transmission of sensory information becomes possible.

**Cortex:** Neuromodulators activate cortical neurons, shifting from slow waves to desynchronized activity characteristic of waking.

**Ascending reticular activating system (ARAS):** This classical concept captures the idea that brainstem reticular formation activation maintains cortical arousal. The modern understanding is that multiple neuromodulatory systems contribute, not a single "reticular activating system."

**Result:** The brain transitions to wake state — thalamus relays sensory information, cortex processes information, consciousness of the external world returns.


### 7.2 Attention Shifting: Distracted to Focused

**Function:** Shift from diffuse attention to focused attention on a specific stimulus or task.

**Salient stimulus** (sudden sound, visual motion, task-relevant signal) is detected.

**Superior colliculus and pulvinar** (for spatial attention) detect stimulus salience and location.

**Locus coeruleus** fires phasically — a burst of activity in response to the salient stimulus. This releases norepinephrine throughout the brain.

**Norepinephrine effects:**
- Increases signal-to-noise ratio in cortex (attended signal is enhanced, noise is suppressed)
- Resets ongoing processing ("interrupt signal")
- Increases alertness

**Frontal eye fields and posterior parietal cortex** shift spatial attention to the stimulus location. These regions form the dorsal attention network for top-down, goal-directed attention.

**Temporoparietal junction and ventral frontal cortex** detect behaviorally relevant stimuli, especially unexpected ones. These regions form the ventral attention network for bottom-up, stimulus-driven attention.

**Thalamic reticular nucleus** is modulated by attention signals. Attention to a location/modality enhances thalamic relay of that information while suppressing relay of unattended information.

**Prefrontal cortex** maintains the attentional set — what should be attended? If the salient stimulus is task-irrelevant, prefrontal cortex can redirect attention back to the task.

**Result:** Attention shifts to the salient stimulus, sensory processing of that stimulus is enhanced, processing of other stimuli is suppressed.


### 7.3 Stress Response: Threat to Mobilization

**Function:** Mobilize the body's resources to deal with a threat or challenge.

**Threat detection** by amygdala (as in fear pathway) or by cortical assessment of challenging situation.

**Two parallel stress pathways:**

#### Fast Pathway: Sympathetic-Adrenal-Medullary (SAM) Axis

**Amygdala → Hypothalamus (lateral) → Brainstem sympathetic centers → Spinal cord (T1-L2 sympathetic neurons) → Sympathetic chain ganglia → Target organs:**

- Heart: Increased rate and contractility
- Blood vessels: Constriction (increased blood pressure), except in muscles (dilation)
- Lungs: Bronchodilation
- Liver: Glucose release
- Pupils: Dilation
- Sweat glands: Activation

**Adrenal medulla** (inner part of adrenal gland) receives sympathetic input and releases epinephrine (adrenaline) into bloodstream, amplifying and prolonging the sympathetic effects.

**Time course:** Seconds to minutes. This is the "fight or flight" response.


#### Slow Pathway: Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) Axis

**Amygdala → Hypothalamus (paraventricular nucleus, PVN) → releases corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH)**

**CRH travels via hypothalamic-pituitary portal system to anterior pituitary**

**Anterior pituitary** releases adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) into bloodstream

**ACTH travels to adrenal cortex**

**Adrenal cortex** (outer part of adrenal gland) releases cortisol into bloodstream

**Cortisol effects:**
- Mobilizes energy (increases blood glucose)
- Suppresses non-essential functions (immune function, digestion, reproduction)
- Modulates brain function (affects memory consolidation, can damage hippocampus with chronic exposure)

**Time course:** Minutes to hours. This is the sustained stress response.

**Negative feedback:** Cortisol inhibits CRH and ACTH release, limiting the stress response. This feedback acts at the hypothalamus, pituitary, and hippocampus.


**Prefrontal cortex can modulate the stress response:**

**Prefrontal inhibition of amygdala** can reduce stress response when the threat is evaluated as manageable.

**Cognitive reappraisal** (changing how you think about a stressor) activates prefrontal cortex and reduces amygdala activity and stress hormones.

**Chronic stress impairs prefrontal function** and enhances amygdala function, creating a vicious cycle.

## Part 8: Language Pathways

These pathways trace how language is comprehended and produced.

### 8.1 Speech Comprehension: Spoken Word to Meaning

**Function:** Transform acoustic speech input into semantic understanding.

**Cochlea** transduces speech sounds into neural signals.

**Ascending auditory pathway:** Cochlear nuclei → superior olivary complex → inferior colliculus → MGN → primary auditory cortex (A1).

**A1 and auditory belt regions** process acoustic features of speech: frequency, timing, amplitude envelope.

**Superior temporal sulcus (STS)** processes speech-specific features, distinguishing speech from non-speech sounds, extracting phonetic information.

**Posterior superior temporal gyrus (Wernicke's area, typically left hemisphere)** processes phonological information (the sounds of language) and maps sounds to lexical representations (words). Wernicke's area accesses the mental lexicon — the stored knowledge of words.

**Middle and inferior temporal cortex** store semantic representations — the meanings of words and concepts. These regions are activated as Wernicke's area accesses word meanings.

**Angular gyrus** integrates phonological, semantic, and visual word form information. Involved in linking sounds to meanings and in reading comprehension.

**Prefrontal cortex** integrates sentence-level meaning, maintains discourse context in working memory, and makes inferences beyond the literal meaning.

**Comprehension is achieved:** You understand what was said.

**Total pathway:** Auditory input → A1 → auditory association cortex → STS → Wernicke's area → temporal semantic areas → angular gyrus → prefrontal cortex.

**Damage to Wernicke's area causes Wernicke's aphasia:** Fluent but meaningless speech, poor comprehension, poor repetition.


### 8.2 Reading: Written Word to Meaning

**Function:** Transform visual text into semantic understanding.

**Visual pathway:** Retina → LGN → V1 → V2 → ventral stream.

**Visual word form area (VWFA, left fusiform gyrus)** specializes for recognizing written words. This region develops with literacy — it is present in readers but not in illiterates. The VWFA treats words as visual objects to be recognized, extracting letter identities and their combinations.

**Two routes to pronunciation and meaning:**

**Lexical route:** VWFA → directly to temporal semantic areas → meaning accessed directly from visual word form. This route works for familiar words.

**Phonological route:** VWFA → angular gyrus → conversion to phonological code → Wernicke's area → meaning accessed via sound representation. This route works for unfamiliar words (you "sound them out" internally).

**Both routes converge on semantic representations** in temporal cortex.

**Prefrontal cortex** integrates meaning across sentences and paragraphs.

**Total pathway:** Visual input → V1 → ventral stream → VWFA → (direct route) temporal semantics OR (phonological route) angular gyrus → Wernicke's area → temporal semantics → prefrontal cortex.

**Dyslexia** involves impaired phonological route — difficulty converting letters to sounds. The VWFA may be underactive or show abnormal connectivity.

## Part 9: Summary of Major Pathways

### Sensory Pathways (External World → Conscious Perception)

**Auditory:** Cochlea → cochlear nuclei → superior olive → inferior colliculus → MGN → A1 → association cortex → Wernicke's → prefrontal

**Visual:** Retina → LGN → V1 → V2 → ventral stream (object recognition) and dorsal stream (spatial/action) → prefrontal

**Somatosensory (fine touch):** Receptors → dorsal columns → medullary nuclei → CROSS → VPL → S1 → association cortex

**Somatosensory (pain):** Nociceptors → dorsal horn → CROSS in cord → spinothalamic → VPL + intralaminar → S1 + insula + ACC

**Gustatory:** Tongue → NTS → parabrachial → VPM → gustatory cortex → orbitofrontal

**Olfactory:** Nose → olfactory bulb → piriform cortex → orbitofrontal / amygdala / entorhinal (bypasses thalamus initially)

**Vestibular:** Labyrinth → vestibular nuclei → thalamus → PIVC / parietal


### Motor Pathways (Intention → Movement)

**Voluntary movement:** Prefrontal (goal) → premotor (plan) → M1 (command) → corticospinal → spinal cord → muscle. Modulated by: basal ganglia loop (selection), cerebellar loop (coordination)

**Eye movement (voluntary):** FEF → superior colliculus → brainstem generators → eye movement nuclei → extraocular muscles

**Eye movement (reflexive):** Retina → superior colliculus → brainstem generators → muscles

**Speech:** Prefrontal → Wernicke's → arcuate fasciculus → Broca's → motor cortex → corticobulbar → cranial nuclei → speech muscles


### Reflex Pathways (Stimulus → Fast Response)

**Stretch reflex:** Spindle → Ia fiber → motor neuron → muscle (~25 ms, monosynaptic)

**Withdrawal reflex:** Nociceptor → dorsal horn → interneurons → motor neurons → flexors (~30-50 ms)

**Pupillary reflex:** Retina → pretectum → Edinger-Westphal → ciliary ganglion → pupil

**Vestibulo-ocular reflex:** Canals → vestibular nuclei → eye nuclei → muscles (~10-15 ms)


### Emotional Pathways

**Fear (fast):** Thalamus → amygdala → hypothalamus/brainstem → defensive responses

**Fear (slow):** Thalamus → cortex → amygdala → responses (can be modulated by prefrontal cortex)

**Reward:** Reward → VTA → nucleus accumbens / prefrontal → behavioral change

**Pain:** Nociceptor → dorsal horn → spinothalamic → S1 (location) + insula (feeling) + ACC (suffering)


### Memory Pathways

**Encoding:** Sensory cortex → entorhinal → dentate gyrus → CA3 → CA1 → subiculum → back to cortex

**Consolidation:** Hippocampal replay during sleep → gradually strengthens cortical connections

**Retrieval:** Cue → entorhinal → hippocampus (pattern completion) → cortical reactivation


### Decision Pathways

**Perceptual decision:** Sensory cortex → parietal (evidence accumulation) → threshold → motor response

**Value-based decision:** Options → OFC (value) → ACC (conflict) → DLPFC (goals) → basal ganglia (selection) → action

**Go/No-Go:** Cortex → striatum → direct pathway (GO) vs. indirect pathway (NO-GO) → thalamus → cortex


### State Pathways

**Wake transition:** Circadian signal + orexin → arousal nuclei (LC, raphe, basal forebrain) → NE/5-HT/ACh release → thalamus tonic mode + cortex desynchronized

**Attention shift:** Salient stimulus → LC phasic firing → NE release → enhanced processing

**Stress:** Threat → amygdala → SAM axis (fast, sympathetic) + HPA axis (slow, cortisol)

## Part 10: Integrated Scenarios — Multiple Pathways in Parallel

Real experiences do not follow single pathways. When something happens to you, multiple systems engage simultaneously, interact with each other, and converge on perception, emotion, decision, and action. This section traces complete scenarios showing how multiple pathways operate in parallel and how they influence each other.

### 10.1 Stepping on a Sharp Object

**Scenario:** You are walking barefoot and your foot comes down on a piece of broken glass.

**Time 0 ms: Contact**

The sharp edge of the glass penetrates the skin of your foot. Tissue damage occurs. Multiple receptor types activate simultaneously.

**Nociceptors** (free nerve endings) detect tissue damage through mechanical deformation and chemical signals released by damaged cells. Both Aδ fibers (myelinated, fast) and C fibers (unmyelinated, slow) begin generating action potentials.

**Mechanoreceptors** detect the pressure and texture of the object.

**Time 1-5 ms: Signals Enter the Spinal Cord**

**Aδ fibers** (conducting at 5-30 m/s) reach the spinal cord first. They enter through the dorsal root and synapse in the dorsal horn, primarily in laminae I and V.

**The reflex arc activates immediately:**

Sensory neurons synapse on interneurons in lamina VII. These interneurons have two immediate effects:

First, excitatory interneurons activate alpha motor neurons in lamina IX that innervate flexor muscles of the affected leg — tibialis anterior, hamstrings, hip flexors. This is the withdrawal component.

Second, inhibitory interneurons suppress alpha motor neurons to extensor muscles of the affected leg — gastrocnemius, quadriceps, gluteals. This allows the flexion to occur without opposition.

**Simultaneously, commissural interneurons cross to the opposite side of the spinal cord:**

On the contralateral side, excitatory interneurons activate extensor motor neurons and inhibitory interneurons suppress flexor motor neurons. This is the crossed extensor reflex — the opposite leg extends to support your body weight as the injured leg withdraws.

**Time 25-40 ms: Reflex Movement Begins**

Motor neurons fire and signals travel down motor axons to the neuromuscular junctions. Acetylcholine release triggers muscle contraction.

Your injured foot begins lifting off the glass. Your opposite leg begins extending and stiffening to catch your weight.

**This reflex is complete before any signal has reached your brain. You are withdrawing your foot before you consciously know anything has happened.**

**Time 5-20 ms: Ascending Signals Begin Their Journey**

While the reflex executes locally, pain signals begin ascending toward the brain.

**Spinothalamic tract:** Second-order neurons in the dorsal horn (laminae I and V) send axons that cross the midline in the anterior white commissure (within 1-2 segments of entry) and ascend in the anterolateral spinal cord.

**Spinoreticular tract:** Collateral branches project to the reticular formation throughout the brainstem. This pathway contributes to arousal and the alerting aspect of pain.

**Spinocerebellar tracts:** Proprioceptive information about the sudden movement of your leg ascends to the cerebellum via the dorsal and ventral spinocerebellar tracts. The cerebellum will use this information to coordinate your balance response.

**Time 50-100 ms: Signals Reach the Brainstem**

**Reticular formation activation:** Spinoreticular fibers activate the reticular formation. This begins to increase arousal. The ascending reticular activating system begins alerting the cortex that something significant is happening.

**Locus coeruleus activation:** The alerting signal reaches the locus coeruleus in the pons. Locus coeruleus neurons fire phasically — a burst of activity. Norepinephrine is released throughout the brain: cortex, thalamus, amygdala, hippocampus.

**Effect of norepinephrine release:** Signal-to-noise ratio increases throughout the brain. Attention narrows. Ongoing cognitive activity is interrupted. The brain shifts into an alert, focused state. This is the neural "interrupt signal."

**Parabrachial nucleus:** Pain signals reach the parabrachial nucleus, which contributes to autonomic responses and the emotional aspects of pain.

**Time 100-150 ms: Signals Reach the Thalamus**

**Spinothalamic tract terminals** arrive at the ventral posterolateral nucleus (VPL) of the thalamus. The VPL is somatotopically organized — the foot area receives these signals.

**Intralaminar thalamic nuclei** also receive spinothalamic input. These nuclei project broadly to cortex and contribute to the arousing, attention-grabbing quality of pain.

**VPL neurons fire** and send signals to the somatosensory cortex via the thalamocortical radiations.

**Time 150-200 ms: Signals Reach the Cortex — Conscious Pain Begins**

**Primary somatosensory cortex (S1), foot area:** Thalamocortical input arrives in layer IV. S1 processes the sensory-discriminative aspects of pain: where exactly on the foot, how intense, what quality (sharp, cutting). You now consciously perceive the location and nature of the injury.

**Insular cortex:** The insula receives input from the thalamus (intralaminar nuclei) and from S1. The insula processes the interoceptive, feeling quality of pain — the subjective sense of "this hurts." The anterior insula is particularly involved in the conscious experience of pain as an unpleasant bodily state.

**Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC):** The ACC processes the affective-motivational dimension of pain — the suffering, the unpleasantness, the urge to do something about it. The ACC signals "this is bad, this needs attention, this needs action."

**At approximately 150-200 ms, you consciously feel pain.** The reflex withdrawal is already well underway — your foot may already be off the glass — but now you know why you're moving.

**Time 200-300 ms: Emotional and Evaluative Processing**

**Amygdala activation:** The amygdala receives pain-related input from the parabrachial nucleus, from the insula, and from sensory cortex. The amygdala generates the emotional response to pain: alarm, distress, fear of further injury. If you've been hurt by glass before, the amygdala retrieves that learned association, intensifying the response.

**Amygdala outputs:**
- To hypothalamus: Begins activating stress response
- To periaqueductal gray (PAG): May initiate pain modulation or defensive behaviors
- To locus coeruleus: Reinforces the alerting response
- To prefrontal cortex: Signals emotional significance

**Hippocampus activation:** The hippocampus receives the pain-related cortical activity plus the norepinephrine and emotional signals. It begins encoding this event as a memory. The spatial context (where you are), the circumstances, and the pain are being bound together into an episodic memory trace. You will remember this.

**Time 300-500 ms: Evaluation and Decision**

**Prefrontal cortex engagement:** By now, prefrontal cortex is fully engaged.

**Dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC):** Maintains working memory of what just happened. Holds the goal: assess the situation, respond appropriately.

**Orbitofrontal cortex (OFC):** Evaluates the situation. How bad is this? What are the consequences? What should I do?

**Ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC):** Integrates emotional information with decision-making. The emotional signals from the amygdala and insula inform the evaluation.

**Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC):** Monitors the situation for conflict and uncertainty. There may be competing demands: check my foot for injury vs. get away from more glass vs. call for help.

**Time 500-1000 ms: Motor Planning and Execution**

You have already completed the reflex withdrawal. Now voluntary, cortically-planned action takes over.

**Posterior parietal cortex:** Computes spatial parameters. Where is safe ground? Where is the glass? What trajectory should my limbs take?

**Premotor cortex and supplementary motor area:** Plan the next movements. Maybe: put weight carefully on the uninjured parts of the foot, hop to safety, sit down to examine the injury.

**Basal ganglia:** The planned action must be selected. Multiple possible actions compete in the striatum. The direct pathway facilitates the selected action (sit down and examine foot); the indirect pathway suppresses alternatives (keep walking, run away).

**Primary motor cortex:** Once an action is selected, motor cortex generates the detailed muscle commands.

**Corticospinal tract:** Commands descend to the spinal cord.

**Spinal cord execution:** Motor neurons fire, muscles contract, you execute the planned movement.

**Cerebellar involvement throughout:** The cerebellum has been monitoring the entire motor sequence. It received efference copies (via pontine nuclei) and proprioceptive feedback (via spinocerebellar tracts). It detected any mismatches and sent corrections to thalamus and motor cortex. The cerebellum is why your balance recovery is smooth rather than jerky.

**Time 1-5 seconds: Stress Response Engages**

**SAM axis (fast sympathetic response):** Already activated by amygdala and hypothalamus output:
- Heart rate increases
- Blood pressure rises
- Pupils dilate
- Sweating may begin
- Blood flow shifts to muscles

**HPA axis (slower hormonal response):** The hypothalamus (paraventricular nucleus) releases CRH, triggering the pituitary to release ACTH, which will cause the adrenal cortex to release cortisol over the next several minutes. Cortisol will mobilize energy and modulate the immune and inflammatory response.

**Time 5-30 seconds: Visual Inspection and Assessment**

You have sat down (or are standing on one foot) and are examining your foot.

**Visual pathway activates:** Light from your foot enters your eyes, is processed through the retina, LGN, V1, and the ventral visual stream. You see blood, you see the wound.

**Visual information reaches prefrontal cortex:** You evaluate what you see. How deep is the cut? Is there glass still in the wound? Is this serious enough to need medical attention?

**Memory retrieval:** The hippocampus and prefrontal cortex retrieve relevant knowledge. What should you do for a cut? Clean it, apply pressure, bandage. Do you need a tetanus shot?

**Decision-making:** Prefrontal cortex weighs options. OFC evaluates outcomes. ACC monitors difficulty. Basal ganglia select an action plan.

**Time 30 seconds onward: Action and Memory Consolidation**

You execute your chosen plan — cleaning the wound, bandaging, putting on shoes, being careful where you walk.

**Memory consolidation begins:** Over the following hours and during subsequent sleep, the hippocampus will replay this event during sharp-wave ripples. The memory will gradually be consolidated into cortical networks. You will remember the day you stepped on glass, where it happened, how it felt, what you did.

**Learning occurs:** The next time you walk in this area, you will be more vigilant. The association between this location and danger is now encoded. Your amygdala will generate a mild alerting response in this context. You have learned from the experience.


### Summary of Simultaneous Pathway Engagement

In the single event of stepping on glass, the following pathways activated:

**Milliseconds 0-50 (Before Consciousness):**
- Withdrawal reflex (spinal cord): Nociceptor → dorsal horn → interneurons → motor neurons → muscles
- Crossed extensor reflex (spinal cord): Commissural interneurons → contralateral motor neurons
- Ascending pain signals begin (spinothalamic, spinoreticular)
- Proprioceptive signals to cerebellum (spinocerebellar)

**Milliseconds 50-200 (Approach to Consciousness):**
- Brainstem arousal (reticular formation, locus coeruleus)
- Norepinephrine release (global alerting)
- Thalamic relay (VPL, intralaminar)
- Cortical pain processing begins (S1, insula, ACC)

**Milliseconds 200-500 (Conscious Experience and Evaluation):**
- Full conscious pain perception (S1, insula, ACC)
- Emotional response (amygdala)
- Memory encoding begins (hippocampus)
- Cognitive evaluation (prefrontal cortex)
- Voluntary motor planning (premotor, basal ganglia, motor cortex)

**Seconds 1-30 (Response and Recovery):**
- Stress response (SAM and HPA axes)
- Visual assessment (retina → V1 → ventral stream → prefrontal)
- Decision-making (OFC, ACC, DLPFC, basal ganglia)
- Voluntary action execution (corticospinal)
- Cerebellar coordination throughout

**Hours to Days (Consolidation):**
- Memory consolidation (hippocampal replay → cortical storage)
- Learning (amygdala-hippocampus associations)

This is the reality of neural processing: not a single pathway, but dozens of pathways operating in parallel, influencing each other, and converging on integrated experience and action.


### 10.2 Hearing Your Name Called

**Scenario:** You are at a noisy party, engaged in conversation. Someone across the room calls your name.

**Time 0 ms: Sound Waves Arrive**

Sound waves carrying your name reach your ears. The sound exists within a cacophony of other sounds — music, multiple conversations, clinking glasses.

**Cochlea:** Hair cells along the basilar membrane transduce the mechanical vibrations into electrical signals. Your name is not separated from other sounds at this stage — it's all mixed together in the frequency components.

**Time 1-20 ms: Ascending Auditory Pathway**

**Cochlear nerve** carries signals to the brainstem.

**Cochlear nuclei (medulla):** First processing station. Begins extracting timing and frequency information.

**Superior olivary complex (pons):** Compares input from both ears. Computes interaural time difference and intensity difference — localizes the sound source. The sound came from the left, approximately 45 degrees from where you're facing.

**Lateral lemniscus → Inferior colliculus (midbrain):** Further processing of auditory features. The inferior colliculus integrates spectral and temporal information.

**Time 20-40 ms: Thalamic Relay**

**Medial geniculate nucleus (MGN):** Receives input from inferior colliculus. Relays to auditory cortex.

Here is where attention matters: Your attention is currently focused on your conversation partner. The MGN is being modulated by top-down signals from cortex. Attended sounds (your partner's voice) are enhanced; unattended sounds (background noise) are suppressed.

But your name has special status. Even unattended, it can break through — the "cocktail party effect."

**Time 40-100 ms: Primary Auditory Processing**

**Primary auditory cortex (A1, Heschl's gyrus):** Receives MGN input in layer IV. Processes basic auditory features: frequency, timing, intensity. Your name is processed as an auditory pattern at this stage.

**Auditory belt and parabelt regions:** Process more complex features. Begin extracting phonetic information — the speech sounds that make up your name.

**Time 100-200 ms: Recognition and Salience Detection**

**Superior temporal sulcus (STS):** Processes voice characteristics. Recognizes this as a human voice calling something.

**Temporal cortex (speech processing areas):** The phonetic pattern is matched against stored representations. Your name is a highly overlearned pattern — you have heard it tens of thousands of times. The pattern match occurs quickly and automatically.

**The critical moment: Your name is recognized.**

**Salience detection:** Your name is intrinsically salient — it has been associated with self-relevant events your entire life. This salience signal does not require conscious attention to be detected.

**Ventral attention network activates:** The temporoparietal junction (TPJ) and ventral frontal cortex detect the unexpected, behaviorally relevant stimulus. This network acts as a "circuit breaker" — it interrupts ongoing processing to redirect attention.

**Time 150-250 ms: Attention Reorientation**

**Locus coeruleus fires phasically:** The salient stimulus triggers a burst of norepinephrine release throughout the brain. Arousal increases. Ongoing processing is interrupted. Attention shifts.

**Dorsal attention network engages:** Frontal eye fields (FEF) and intraparietal sulcus (IPS) prepare to reorient spatial attention. Based on the localization computed earlier (left, ~45 degrees), these areas begin preparing a saccade and head turn.

**Disengagement from current focus:** The conversation you were attending to is temporarily suppressed. The previous focus of attention is released.

**Time 200-300 ms: Conscious Awareness**

**Prefrontal cortex engagement:** You become consciously aware that someone called your name. Working memory (DLPFC) now holds this information.

**At approximately 200-300 ms, you consciously perceive "someone called my name" and "it came from over there."**

**Time 250-350 ms: Orienting Response**

**Superior colliculus receives converging input:** Auditory location information (from inferior colliculus), attention signals (from FEF), and top-down commands (from cortex) converge on the superior colliculus, which contains aligned maps of auditory and visual space.

**Eye movement command generated:**

**PPRF (horizontal saccade generator)** receives superior colliculus output.

**Abducens nucleus (VI)** and **oculomotor nucleus (III)** fire.

**Extraocular muscles contract:** Your eyes move rapidly (saccade) toward the sound source.

**Time 300-400 ms: Head Turn Begins**

**Tectospinal tract** (from superior colliculus) and **corticospinal tract** (from motor cortex) send commands to neck motor neurons.

**Neck muscles contract:** Your head begins turning toward the sound.

**Time 400-600 ms: Visual Identification**

**Your eyes have now oriented to the sound source.** Visual information begins streaming in.

**Visual pathway activates:** Retina → LGN → V1 → V2 → ventral stream (who is this?) and dorsal stream (where exactly?).

**Fusiform face area (FFA):** Processes the face of the person who called you. Face recognition processes engage.

**Time 600-1000 ms: Recognition and Social Evaluation**

**Face recognition:** If this is someone you know, the FFA and associated temporal cortex areas retrieve the stored face representation. You recognize the person.

**Name retrieval:** If you know the person, their name may be retrieved (though this can be slow and sometimes fails — the "tip of the tongue" phenomenon).

**Social evaluation:** Orbitofrontal cortex and medial prefrontal cortex evaluate the social context. Who is this person? What is your relationship? What do they likely want?

**Amygdala may activate:** Depending on your relationship with this person, the amygdala may generate an emotional response — pleasure at seeing a friend, anxiety at seeing someone you've been avoiding.

**Time 1-2 seconds: Decision and Response**

**Prefrontal cortex decides:** Do I go over to them? Do I wave? Do I call back?

**Basal ganglia select the action:** Multiple possible responses compete. The selected action (wave and smile, start walking over, or pretend you didn't hear) wins the competition.

**Motor execution:** Premotor cortex and motor cortex plan the movement. Corticospinal and corticobulbar tracts execute the motor commands. You wave, you smile (facial nerve controls facial muscles), you may call back (speech production pathway), you may begin walking toward them.

**Social cognition throughout:** The medial prefrontal cortex and STS are computing what this person knows, what they expect from you, how they might be feeling. You are modeling their mind while preparing your response.


### Summary of Pathways in "Hearing Your Name"

**Automatic processing (before consciousness):**
- Auditory pathway: cochlea → cochlear nuclei → superior olive → inferior colliculus → MGN → A1
- Sound localization: superior olivary complex (binaural comparison)
- Speech/name recognition: auditory association areas, temporal cortex

**Attention reorientation:**
- Salience detection: ventral attention network (TPJ, ventral frontal)
- Alerting: locus coeruleus → norepinephrine release
- Attention shift: dorsal attention network (FEF, IPS)

**Orienting response:**
- Eye movement: superior colliculus → PPRF → eye muscle nuclei → saccade
- Head turn: tectospinal + corticospinal → neck motor neurons

**Visual identification:**
- Visual pathway: retina → LGN → V1 → ventral stream → FFA (face recognition)
- Memory retrieval: temporal cortex, hippocampus (who is this person?)

**Social processing and decision:**
- Social cognition: mPFC, STS (what do they want? how do they feel?)
- Emotional response: amygdala (depending on relationship)
- Decision: prefrontal cortex → basal ganglia → action selection
- Response execution: motor cortex → corticospinal/corticobulbar → muscles


### 10.3 Reaching for Food When Hungry

**Scenario:** You are hungry. You see a plate of food in front of you. You reach out, pick up a piece, and eat it.

**This scenario involves integration of homeostatic state, visual processing, value computation, motor planning, and reward.**

**Background State: Hunger**

You haven't eaten for several hours. Your homeostatic state has shifted.

**Hypothalamus (arcuate nucleus):** Detects low blood glucose and low leptin (satiety hormone from fat cells). Ghrelin (hunger hormone from stomach) is elevated. The arcuate nucleus neurons that promote feeding (NPY/AgRP neurons) are active; neurons that suppress feeding (POMC neurons) are inhibited.

**Lateral hypothalamus:** Receives input from arcuate nucleus. Orexin neurons are active, promoting arousal and food-seeking. The lateral hypothalamus projects to reward circuits, enhancing the value of food-related stimuli.

**This homeostatic state biases the entire brain:** Food stimuli will be more salient, more rewarding, and more likely to capture attention.

**Time 0 ms: Visual Input**

Light reflecting from the food enters your eyes.

**Retina → Optic nerve → LGN → V1:** Basic visual processing. Edges, colors, shapes.

**Time 50-150 ms: Visual Object Recognition**

**Ventral visual stream:** V2 → V4 (color, form) → inferior temporal cortex.

**Object recognition:** The visual pattern is matched against stored representations. This is food — specifically, it might be recognized as a specific food type (pizza, apple, etc.).

**Amygdala involvement:** The amygdala receives visual input and detects the biological significance of the stimulus. Food, when hungry, is highly significant. The amygdala activates.

**Time 100-200 ms: Value Computation**

**Orbitofrontal cortex (OFC):** The OFC computes the subjective value of the food. This computation integrates:
- Sensory properties (how does it look? smell?)
- Past experience (how good was this food before?)
- Current state (how hungry am I?)

Because you are hungry, the lateral hypothalamus is enhancing the value signal. The food has high value right now — much higher than it would if you were satiated.

**Amygdala → OFC connection:** The amygdala's significance signal enhances OFC value computation.

**Dopamine system primes:** The sight of food, with its high computed value, activates the VTA dopamine system. Dopamine is released in the nucleus accumbens and prefrontal cortex. This creates "wanting" — the motivation to obtain the food.

**Time 150-300 ms: Decision to Reach**

**Prefrontal cortex:** Evaluates whether to reach for the food. In this scenario, there's no reason not to — the food is available, it's yours to eat. The decision is easy.

If there were competing considerations (you're on a diet, the food belongs to someone else, you're in a formal setting), prefrontal cortex would weigh these against the desire to eat. DLPFC would maintain diet goals; ACC would signal conflict between desire and goals.

**For this scenario:** The value of the food (from OFC) is high, there are no strong competing considerations, and the decision is made: reach for the food.

**Time 200-400 ms: Motor Planning**

**Posterior parietal cortex (dorsal visual stream):** Computes the spatial parameters for the reach. Where is the food relative to my hand? What trajectory is needed? The superior parietal lobule and intraparietal sulcus process visuospatial information for action guidance.

**Premotor cortex:** Plans the reaching movement. Specifies which arm, what general movement pattern.

**Supplementary motor area (SMA):** Plans the sequence: reach, grasp, lift, bring to mouth.

**Basal ganglia selection:** The planned reach competes with other potential actions (do nothing, reach for something else) in the striatum. Given the high value signals from OFC and the clear motor plan, the "reach for food" action wins.

**Direct pathway:** D1 neurons representing "reach for food" inhibit GPi, releasing the thalamus, which excites motor cortex. The action is selected.

**Time 400-600 ms: Reach Initiation**

**Primary motor cortex (M1):** Generates the detailed muscle commands. The hand area of M1 is particularly active — reaching and grasping require fine motor control.

**Corticospinal tract:** Commands descend through internal capsule, cerebral peduncle, pons, pyramids, pyramidal decussation, and lateral corticospinal tract to the cervical spinal cord (C5-T1 for arm control).

**Spinal cord:** Motor neurons in the cervical ventral horn fire. Muscles contract.

**The reach begins:** Your arm extends toward the food.

**Cerebellar involvement:**

**Pontine nuclei** receive motor cortex output (efference copy) and relay to cerebellum.

**Cerebellar cortex** compares the motor plan with proprioceptive feedback arriving via spinocerebellar tracts. Is the arm going where it's supposed to?

**Deep cerebellar nuclei** (dentate) send corrections via superior cerebellar peduncle → thalamus (VL) → motor cortex.

The cerebellum ensures the reach is smooth and accurate, making ongoing corrections as the arm moves.

**Time 600-1000 ms: Reaching and Grasping**

**Visual guidance:** The dorsal stream continuously updates the motor plan based on visual feedback. If the target moves (someone shifts the plate), the reach adjusts.

**Proprioceptive feedback:** Spinocerebellar tracts continuously inform the cerebellum about arm position. The cerebellum corrects any deviations.

**Pre-shaping the hand:** As the hand approaches, the fingers pre-shape to the size and shape of the food item. This requires integration of visual information (object size and shape) with motor commands. The premotor cortex and parietal cortex coordinate this.

**Grasping:** Contact is made. Mechanoreceptors in the fingertips (Meissner's corpuscles, Merkel's discs) detect the texture and resistance of the food. This information ascends via the dorsal column pathway to S1, informing grip force adjustment.

**Grip force adjustment:** You don't want to crush the food or drop it. Sensory feedback allows precise calibration. This involves a fast loop through the cerebellum and a slower loop through somatosensory cortex.

**Time 1-2 seconds: Bringing Food to Mouth**

**Motor sequence continues:** SMA coordinates the sequence — lift, transport, bring to mouth. Motor cortex executes each phase. Cerebellum coordinates.

**Anticipatory mouth opening:** Before the food arrives, your mouth opens in anticipation. This is a learned, coordinated movement prepared by the motor system based on prediction.

**Time 2-3 seconds: Eating and Reward**

**The food enters your mouth.**

**Taste pathway activates:**

**Taste receptors** on the tongue detect sweet, salty, sour, bitter, umami components.

**Cranial nerves VII, IX, X** carry taste signals to the nucleus of the solitary tract (NTS) in the medulla.

**NTS → Parabrachial nucleus (pons) → VPM thalamus → Primary gustatory cortex (insula, frontal operculum).**

You consciously taste the food.

**Olfactory pathway:** Volatile compounds from the food reach the olfactory epithelium retronasally (from the back of the mouth). Olfaction combines with taste to create flavor.

**Orbitofrontal cortex:** Integrates taste and smell, computing the flavor. Compares actual reward with expected reward. If the food tastes as good as expected (or better), this is encoded.

**Reward response:**

**VTA dopamine neurons:** The rewarding taste activates VTA neurons. If the food is better than expected, there's a positive prediction error — VTA neurons fire phasically. If it's as expected, there's no change. If it's worse than expected, VTA neurons pause.

**Nucleus accumbens:** Receives dopamine. The "liking" and "wanting" of the food is processed. Pleasure is experienced.

**Endogenous opioid release:** The taste of palatable food triggers release of endogenous opioids in the nucleus accumbens and other regions. This contributes to the hedonic (pleasure) response.

**Learning:** The association between this food, this context, and the reward is strengthened. You will be more likely to eat this food again.

**Time 3-10 seconds: Continuing to Eat**

**Chewing and swallowing:** Motor commands from motor cortex and brainstem motor nuclei (trigeminal V for jaw muscles, hypoglossal XII for tongue) coordinate chewing. The swallowing reflex (brainstem mediated) is triggered when the bolus is ready.

**Satiety signals begin:** As food enters the stomach, stretch receptors and nutrient sensors generate satiety signals. These travel via the vagus nerve to the NTS, which relays to hypothalamus. Satiety hormones (CCK, PYY) begin rising.

**Value updating:** As you eat more, the value of the food decreases (sensory-specific satiety). The OFC updates its value representation. Each subsequent bite is slightly less rewarding than the last.

**Decision to continue or stop:** Prefrontal cortex integrates the changing value (from OFC), the satiety signals (via hypothalamus), and the goal (satisfy hunger). At some point, the value of continuing to eat will drop below the threshold for action, and you will stop.


### Summary of Pathways in "Reaching for Food"

**Homeostatic state (background):**
- Hypothalamus: arcuate nucleus (hunger detection), lateral hypothalamus (feeding drive)
- Dopamine system primed for food-seeking

**Visual processing:**
- Visual pathway: retina → LGN → V1 → ventral stream (object recognition) and dorsal stream (spatial location)
- Amygdala: significance detection

**Value computation:**
- OFC: integrates sensory properties, past experience, current hunger state
- Amygdala → OFC: enhances value
- Dopamine (VTA → accumbens, PFC): creates wanting/motivation

**Decision:**
- Prefrontal cortex: evaluates, decides
- Basal ganglia: selects action via direct pathway

**Motor execution:**
- Premotor/SMA: plans sequence
- Motor cortex: generates commands
- Corticospinal: transmits to spinal cord
- Cerebellum: coordinates, corrects
- Dorsal stream: provides visual guidance

**Reward:**
- Taste pathway: tongue → NTS → parabrachial → VPM → gustatory cortex
- Olfactory pathway: nose → olfactory bulb → piriform → OFC
- Reward system: VTA dopamine, nucleus accumbens, opioid release
- Learning: hippocampus (episodic), amygdala (emotional associations), striatum (action-outcome)


### 10.4 Seeing a Threat: Snake in the Path

**Scenario:** You are hiking on a trail. You see a snake coiled on the path ahead of you.

**This scenario involves the fast and slow fear pathways, rapid threat assessment, and coordinated defensive response.**

**Time 0 ms: Visual Input**

Light reflecting from the snake enters your eyes.

**Retina:** The visual pattern is transduced. At this stage, it's just a pattern of light — curved shape, possibly moving, certain colors.

**Time 1-10 ms: Subcortical Visual Processing**

**Superior colliculus:** Receives direct retinal input via a branch of the optic tract. The superior colliculus processes the visual stimulus before the cortex does. Certain primitive threat features — sudden movement, looming, curved coiled shapes — may be detected here.

**Time 10-40 ms: Fast Pathway to Amygdala (Low Road)**

**Thalamus (LGN and pulvinar):** Receives retinal input. Before detailed cortical analysis, the thalamus sends a fast, crude signal directly to the amygdala.

**Amygdala (lateral nucleus):** Receives the thalamic "low road" input. This signal is rapid but imprecise — it doesn't specify "definitely a snake," but something like "possibly threatening curved shape."

**The amygdala errs on the side of caution.** A false positive (fear response to a stick) is better than a false negative (no fear response to a snake). The amygdala activates based on this crude input.

**Time 30-60 ms: Amygdala Initiates Defensive Response**

**Amygdala (central nucleus) outputs begin:**

**To hypothalamus:** Activates the sympathetic nervous system and HPA axis. Heart rate begins increasing. Stress hormones will be released.

**To periaqueductal gray (PAG):** Initiates freezing response. The ventrolateral PAG produces immobility — you stop moving before you consciously know why.

**To locus coeruleus:** Triggers norepinephrine release. Arousal spikes. Attention narrows.

**To brainstem nuclei:** Potentiates startle reflex. Modulates respiration (you may gasp or hold your breath).

**You freeze.** Your body stops moving. Your heart rate jumps. Your eyes are locked on the threat location. This happens before you consciously perceive the snake.

**Time 50-150 ms: Cortical Visual Processing (Slow Road)**

**Visual pathway completes:** LGN → V1 → V2 → V4 → IT (ventral stream).

**Object recognition:** The visual pattern is analyzed in detail. Shape, texture, color, pattern. Is this a snake? The temporal cortex matches the visual input against stored representations.

**Time 100-200 ms: Conscious Threat Perception**

**You consciously see the snake.** The ventral stream has identified it. You now know what you're looking at.

**Hippocampus:** Provides contextual information. Where are you? Is this a place where snakes are common? What kind of snakes are here — venomous or not?

**Memory retrieval:** Relevant knowledge is accessed. What do you know about snakes? What should you do when you encounter one?

**Time 150-300 ms: Threat Assessment**

**Prefrontal cortex:** Evaluates the threat more precisely.

**How close is it?** Posterior parietal cortex computes distance.

**Is it moving toward me?** Motion processing (V5/MT, dorsal stream) tracks any movement.

**What kind of snake is it?** Visual memory and semantic knowledge (temporal cortex) are consulted.

**How dangerous is the situation?** OFC and vmPFC integrate all information to assess risk.

**The slow road (cortical) pathway converges on the amygdala:**

**Amygdala (basal nucleus):** Receives detailed cortical analysis. This input either confirms the threat ("yes, definitely a snake, possibly venomous") or modifies the initial fast response ("actually, it's a stick" or "it's a harmless garter snake").

**If threat is confirmed:** Amygdala activity remains high or increases. Defensive response continues and intensifies.

**If threat is disconfirmed:** Prefrontal cortex sends inhibitory signals to amygdala. Fear response diminishes. You relax — "it's just a stick."

**Time 300-500 ms: Decision and Action Planning (Assuming Real Snake)**

**Prefrontal cortex decides on action.** Options: freeze and wait for snake to leave, slowly back away, find a way around.

**Premotor cortex plans the movement.** If you decide to back away slowly, premotor cortex plans the sequence — shift weight, step back, maintain visual contact.

**Basal ganglia select the action.** The "back away slowly" action is selected over alternatives like "run" or "attack."

**Important: The hyperdirect pathway may be active.** The danger of the situation means you need to be able to stop any action instantly if something changes. The prefrontal cortex → STN connection keeps the "emergency stop" pathway primed.

**Time 500-1000 ms: Execution with Continuous Monitoring**

**Motor execution:** Motor cortex generates commands. Corticospinal tract carries them to spinal cord. You begin backing away slowly.

**Continuous visual monitoring:** Your eyes stay fixed on the snake. The dorsal visual stream tracks its position. Any movement by the snake will be detected immediately.

**Superior colliculus:** Primed for orienting. If the snake moves suddenly, the superior colliculus can trigger a rapid protective response before cortex fully processes the new information.

**Amygdala remains active:** The threat is still present. The emotional response continues. Attention remains narrowly focused on the threat.

**Time 1-10 seconds: Escape and Recovery**

**You back away until you're at a safe distance.**

**Threat assessment updates:** As distance increases, prefrontal evaluation of threat decreases. OFC updates: the danger level is now lower.

**Prefrontal inhibition of amygdala:** Once you're safe, prefrontal cortex increasingly inhibits amygdala. Fear response diminishes.

**Physiological recovery:** Heart rate begins slowing. Breathing normalizes. The acute stress response fades.

**But stress hormones persist:** Cortisol released by the HPA axis will remain elevated for minutes to hours, keeping you in a mildly alert state.

**Time 10-60 seconds: Processing and Memory**

**Hippocampal encoding:** The experience is encoded as an episodic memory. The context (this location on this trail), the stimulus (snake), and the emotional response (fear) are bound together.

**Amygdala-hippocampus interaction:** The emotional charge of the experience enhances memory consolidation. You will remember this vividly.

**Prefrontal reflection:** You may consciously think about what happened. "That was scary. I should watch where I step. I should tell others about the snake."

**Learning:** Multiple types of learning occur:
- **Hippocampal/spatial:** This location is associated with danger
- **Amygdala/emotional:** Snake-like stimuli are confirmed as threatening
- **Cortical/semantic:** Knowledge about snakes is reinforced
- **Procedural:** The "freeze then back away" response is reinforced as effective


### Summary of Pathways in "Seeing a Snake"

**Fast threat response (before consciousness):**
- Thalamus → amygdala (fast, crude)
- Amygdala → hypothalamus (sympathetic activation)
- Amygdala → PAG (freezing)
- Amygdala → locus coeruleus (norepinephrine release)

**Slow threat analysis (conscious):**
- Visual pathway: retina → LGN → V1 → ventral stream → object recognition
- Hippocampus: contextual information
- Prefrontal cortex: threat assessment
- Cortex → amygdala: confirms or modifies response

**Decision and action:**
- Prefrontal: decides on action
- Basal ganglia: selects action (hyperdirect pathway primed for emergency stop)
- Motor cortex → corticospinal → muscles: executes escape

**Continuous monitoring:**
- Dorsal visual stream: tracks threat location
- Superior colliculus: primed for rapid orienting
- Amygdala: maintains emotional response while threat present

**Recovery and memory:**
- Prefrontal inhibition of amygdala: reduces fear as threat recedes
- Hippocampus: encodes episodic memory
- Amygdala-hippocampus: emotional enhancement of memory


### 10.5 Learning a New Motor Skill: First Tennis Serve

**Scenario:** You have never played tennis. A coach is teaching you to serve. You attempt your first serve.

**This scenario highlights the role of the cerebellum in motor learning, the shift from prefrontal-controlled to automated execution, and the role of error signals.**

**Time 0: Instruction**

Your coach explains and demonstrates the serve. You watch and listen.

**Auditory pathway:** The verbal instructions are processed (cochlea → A1 → Wernicke's area → prefrontal cortex). You consciously understand the steps: toss the ball, reach back, swing up and through, hit at the highest point.

**Visual pathway:** The demonstration is processed (retina → V1 → dorsal stream for movement kinematics, ventral stream for object recognition). Mirror neuron systems in premotor cortex may activate as you watch the skilled movement.

**Prefrontal cortex:** Holds the instructions in working memory. The explicit rules are maintained consciously.

**Hippocampus:** Encodes the episodic memory of the lesson — what the coach said, what the demonstration looked like.

**Time 1: Attempt Initiation**

You decide to attempt the serve.

**Prefrontal cortex:** Retrieves the explicit instructions from working memory. "First, toss the ball."

**Premotor cortex and SMA:** Attempt to translate the verbal instructions into a motor plan. But you've never done this before — there's no stored motor program. The plan is constructed deliberately, step by step, from explicit knowledge.

**This is effortful and slow.** Each component must be consciously planned. This is "cognitive stage" learning.

**Basal ganglia:** Select the action. But without a well-practiced action representation, the selection is imprecise.

**Time 2: Motor Execution (First Attempt)**

**Motor cortex:** Generates commands based on the unpracticed plan.

**Corticospinal tract:** Carries commands to spinal cord.

**Movement begins:** You toss the ball and begin the swing.

**The movement is awkward.** Timing is wrong. Force is miscalibrated. Coordination is poor. The racket misses the ball or hits it weakly into the net.

**Why?** The cerebellum doesn't yet have an internal model for this movement. It cannot predict and correct errors accurately.

**Time 3: Error Detection and Feedback**

**Sensory feedback:** You see and feel the result. Visual feedback shows the ball went into the net. Proprioceptive feedback tells you how the movement felt.

**Prefrontal evaluation:** "That was wrong. The ball should have gone over the net."

**Error computation (multiple systems):**

**Cerebellum:** The cerebellum received an efference copy of the motor command (via pontine nuclei) and proprioceptive feedback (via spinocerebellar tracts). It detected a mismatch between predicted and actual movement. But more importantly:

**Inferior olivary nucleus:** Receives information about the motor error — the mismatch between intended outcome (serve in the box) and actual outcome (ball in net). The inferior olive computes an error signal.

**Climbing fibers:** The inferior olive sends this error signal to the cerebellar cortex via climbing fibers. Climbing fiber input to Purkinje cells triggers synaptic plasticity — the strength of parallel fiber → Purkinje cell synapses is modified.

This is cerebellar learning: the error signal adjusts the cerebellar internal model so that future movements will be more accurate.

**Dopamine system:** The failure to achieve the goal (successful serve) may produce a negative prediction error — things were worse than expected. VTA dopamine neurons pause briefly. This signal affects striatal learning, weakening the association between this particular motor program and reward.

**Time 4: Explicit Correction**

**Prefrontal cortex:** Analyzes what went wrong. "I tossed the ball too far in front. I swung too early. I didn't follow through."

**Verbal self-instruction:** You may consciously rehearse corrections. "Toss higher. Wait longer. Follow through to the target."

**Working memory:** Holds the updated instructions.

**Time 5: Second Attempt**

**Premotor cortex:** Generates a modified plan incorporating the corrections.

**Motor cortex → movement:** You try again.

**Result:** Still imperfect, but perhaps slightly better. The ball goes over the net but lands outside the service box.

**Error detection again:** New error signal. Climbing fibers fire. Cerebellar model updates.

**Time 6-100: Practice**

**Over many repetitions (perhaps dozens or hundreds of serves), multiple learning processes occur:**

**Cerebellar model refinement:** Each error triggers climbing fiber activity and synaptic plasticity. The cerebellar internal model for the serve becomes increasingly accurate. The cerebellum learns to predict the sensory consequences of motor commands and to generate corrections in real-time.

**Basal ganglia learning:** Successful serves (ball lands in the box) produce dopamine reward signals. The striatum strengthens the associations between the successful motor programs and their outcomes. Action selection becomes more reliable — the "good serve" program is more likely to be selected.

**Cortico-cortical plasticity:** The connections within motor cortex and between premotor and motor cortex strengthen for the serve-related neural patterns. The motor program becomes more efficiently encoded.

**Shift from explicit to implicit:** Gradually, the movement becomes less dependent on conscious, prefrontal-controlled step-by-step execution. It becomes more automatic, mediated by motor cortex and basal ganglia with less prefrontal involvement.

**Time 100+: Skilled Performance**

After extensive practice, the serve becomes skilled.

**Prefrontal cortex:** No longer needs to hold explicit instructions. You don't think "toss, reach back, swing." You just decide to serve.

**Basal ganglia:** The "serve" action is a single, well-learned program. It wins selection easily when you intend to serve.

**Motor cortex:** Executes the program smoothly. The movement is coordinated and consistent.

**Cerebellum:** Has an accurate internal model. It predicts the consequences of motor commands, detects errors instantly, and makes real-time corrections. Adjustments for different conditions (wind, fatigue, different opponent position) are computed automatically.

**The movement is now "procedural memory."** It's stored in motor cortex, basal ganglia, and cerebellum. It doesn't require hippocampal involvement to execute (though the hippocampus encoded the original learning episodes).

**Interference with explicit thought:** If you now try to consciously think through each step of the serve, performance may actually decrease. The explicit, prefrontal-controlled process interferes with the smooth, automatic execution. "Choking under pressure" often involves inappropriate prefrontal engagement in well-learned motor skills.


### Summary of Pathways in "Learning Tennis Serve"

**Initial instruction:**
- Auditory pathway → Wernicke's → prefrontal (verbal instructions)
- Visual pathway → dorsal stream → premotor (demonstration)
- Prefrontal: explicit rule storage
- Hippocampus: episodic memory of lesson

**Early attempts (cognitive stage):**
- Prefrontal: explicit, step-by-step control
- Premotor → motor cortex: deliberate, slow execution
- Cerebellum: no accurate internal model yet

**Error detection and learning:**
- Sensory feedback: visual, proprioceptive
- Inferior olive: computes error signal
- Climbing fibers → cerebellar cortex: triggers plasticity
- Dopamine system: reward/punishment signals for action outcomes

**Skill development (associative stage):**
- Cerebellar model refinement (climbing fiber-driven plasticity)
- Basal ganglia learning (dopamine-driven)
- Reduced prefrontal involvement

**Expert performance (autonomous stage):**
- Basal ganglia: selects "serve" as single program
- Motor cortex: smooth execution
- Cerebellum: accurate internal model, real-time correction
- Prefrontal: minimal involvement (unless strategic decisions needed)
