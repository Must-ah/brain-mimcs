# Region-Centric Communication Map: Cerebrum — Cerebral Cortex — Insular Lobe

This document provides a complete communication map for the insular lobe (insula). For each region, it specifies what that area communicates with at every level: within the insula, with other cortical lobes, with subcortical structures, and with brainstem structures — all with layer-level connectivity where applicable.

The insula is a "hidden" lobe, buried deep within the lateral sulcus. Despite its concealed location, it serves critical functions in interoception (sensing the body's internal state), emotion, and multimodal integration.

---

## Foundational Reference

**Before reading this document, ensure familiarity with the Canonical Cortical Microcircuit document** (`cerebrum-Cerebral-Cortex-Canonical-Microcircuit.md`), which describes:
- The six cortical layers and their cell types
- Feedforward connections: Layer III → Layer IV of target
- Feedback connections: Layer V/VI → Layer I of target (avoiding Layer IV)
- Layer IV as the input layer; Layer V as subcortical output; Layer VI as thalamic feedback
- Granular vs agranular cortex types

The insula contains a **posterior-to-anterior gradient** from granular (sensory) to agranular (limbic) cortex, paralleling the frontal lobe's motor-to-prefrontal gradient.

## Part 1: Overview of the Insular Lobe

### Anatomical Location

The insula is located deep within the lateral (Sylvian) fissure, hidden beneath the opercula (covering "lids") of the frontal, parietal, and temporal lobes. To visualize the insula, you must pull apart these overlying cortical regions.

**Boundaries:**
- **Superior:** Covered by frontal operculum and parietal operculum
- **Inferior:** Covered by temporal operculum
- **Anterior:** Limen insulae (threshold of insula) — transition to orbital cortex
- **Posterior:** Blends with parietal operculum (secondary somatosensory cortex, S2)

The insula is separated from surrounding cortex by the **circular sulcus** (peri-insular sulcus), which defines its boundaries.

### Gross Anatomy

The insular surface is organized into gyri separated by sulci:

**Central Sulcus of the Insula:**
- Divides insula into anterior and posterior portions
- Analogous to the central sulcus of the hemisphere

**Anterior Insula:**
- Three short gyri (anterior, middle, posterior short gyri)
- Agranular to dysgranular cortex
- More "limbic" in character

**Posterior Insula:**
- Two long gyri (anterior and posterior long gyri)
- Granular to dysgranular cortex
- More "sensory" in character

### Cytoarchitectonic Organization

The insula shows a **posterior-to-anterior gradient** in cytoarchitecture:

**Posterior (Granular) Insula:**
- Well-developed Layer IV
- Receives primary sensory input (visceral, gustatory, vestibular)
- Similar to primary sensory cortices

**Mid-Insula (Dysgranular):**
- Transitional
- Moderate Layer IV
- Integration zone

**Anterior (Agranular) Insula:**
- Layer IV thin or absent
- More similar to limbic/paralimbic cortex
- Emotional and cognitive functions

This gradient parallels the frontal lobe's motor (agranular) to prefrontal (granular) organization, but in reverse direction — the insula goes from sensory (posterior, granular) to limbic (anterior, agranular).

### Functional Overview

The insula serves as a **multimodal integration hub** with four major functions:

**Interoception:** Sensing the internal state of the body (heartbeat, breathing, hunger, thirst, temperature, pain, visceral sensations)

**Gustatory Processing:** Primary taste cortex — receives taste information from thalamus

**Emotional Awareness:** Transforms bodily signals into conscious emotional feelings; particularly disgust, but also other emotions

**Multimodal Integration:** Integrates information across sensory modalities; body ownership; sense of agency

### Brodmann Areas

The insula is not well-represented in Brodmann's original cytoarchitectonic map. Various numbering schemes exist:

- Some sources use BA 13, 14, 15, 16 for different insular regions
- More commonly, the insula is simply divided into anterior, mid, and posterior regions
- Agranular, dysgranular, and granular zones are more functionally meaningful

## Part 2: Posterior Insula — Primary Interoceptive and Vestibular Cortex

**Location:** Posterior portion of the insula, including the posterior long gyrus

**Cytoarchitecture:** Granular — well-developed Layer IV

**Function:** Primary interoceptive cortex. Receives visceral sensory information, vestibular information, and somatosensory information (especially pain and temperature). First cortical stage for sensing the body's internal state.

### Primary Sensory Functions

**Interoceptive Input:**
- Heartbeat awareness
- Breathing sensation
- Gut feelings (literally)
- Bladder/bowel fullness
- Temperature (internal)

**Vestibular Processing:**
- Part of the vestibular cortex (PIVC — Parieto-Insular Vestibular Cortex)
- Balance, spatial orientation
- Self-motion perception

**Somatosensory (Pain and Temperature):**
- Receives spinothalamic input (pain, temperature)
- Posterior insula is part of the "pain matrix"

### Within Insular Lobe

**To Mid-Insula:**
- **Layer III → Mid-insula Layer IV**
- Feedforward interoceptive information
- First stage of interoceptive hierarchy

**To Anterior Insula:**
- Via mid-insula
- Hierarchical processing toward awareness

**From Anterior Insula (Feedback):**
- Anterior insula Layer V/VI → Posterior insula Layer I
- Top-down modulation

### With Parietal Lobe

**To/From S2 (Secondary Somatosensory Cortex):**
- **Adjacent and strongly connected**
- Posterior insula continuous with parietal operculum
- Pain, temperature, tactile integration

**To/From Posterior Parietal Cortex:**
- Vestibular-spatial integration
- Body in space

**To/From VIP (Ventral Intraparietal):**
- PIVC ↔ VIP
- Vestibular-visual integration
- Self-motion perception

### With Temporal Lobe

**To/From Auditory Cortex:**
- Some connections
- Auditory-vestibular integration

**To/From Superior Temporal Regions:**
- Vestibular processing

### With Thalamus

**From VMpo (Ventral Medial Posterior Nucleus, pars posterior):**
- **MAJOR INPUT**
- Visceral sensory information
- Interoceptive signals from body
- Layer IV

**From VMb (Ventral Medial Basal Nucleus):**
- Taste information (also to anterior insula)
- Layer IV

**From VPL/VPM:**
- Somatosensory (pain, temperature)
- Spinothalamic pathway
- Layer IV

**From Vestibular Thalamus (VPP, VPI):**
- Vestibular input
- Layer IV

**To Thalamus (Feedback):**
- **Layer VI → Thalamic nuclei**
- Modulation of relay

### With Brainstem

**From Parabrachial Nucleus:**
- Via thalamus
- Visceral sensory relay
- Taste, cardiorespiratory, pain

**From Nucleus of the Solitary Tract (NTS):**
- Via thalamus (VMpo, parabrachial)
- Visceral afferent relay

### Functional Properties

**Primary Interoceptive Cortex:**
- First cortical representation of body's internal state
- Craig's theory: Posterior insula = primary interoceptive cortex (analogous to V1 for vision)

**Pain Processing:**
- Part of the "pain matrix"
- Sensory-discriminative aspects (where, intensity)
- Distinct from affective aspects (anterior insula, ACC)

**Vestibular Cortex (PIVC):**
- Parieto-Insular Vestibular Cortex
- Located at junction of posterior insula and parietal operculum
- Integrates vestibular, visual, somatosensory for spatial orientation

**Body Ownership:**
- Contributes to sense that body belongs to self
- Lesions can cause depersonalization

## Part 3: Mid-Insula — Integration Zone

**Location:** Central portion of the insula, around the central insular sulcus

**Cytoarchitecture:** Dysgranular — transitional between posterior and anterior

**Function:** Integration zone. Combines interoceptive information with other modalities. Processes sensorimotor aspects of interoception.

### Within Insular Lobe

**From Posterior Insula:**
- Posterior Layer III → Mid-insula Layer IV
- Feedforward interoceptive

**To Anterior Insula:**
- **Layer III → Anterior insula (Layers II/III, V)**
- Note: Anterior insula is agranular, so input doesn't target Layer IV

**To/From Posterior and Anterior:**
- Bidirectional integration

### With Frontal Lobe

**To/From Premotor Cortex:**
- Sensorimotor integration
- Body state for action

**To/From Motor Cortex:**
- Visceromotor coordination

### With Parietal Lobe

**To/From S2:**
- Strong connections
- Tactile-interoceptive integration

### Functional Properties

**Sensorimotor Integration:**
- Integrates body sensation with movement
- Coordinates autonomic with somatic

**Transition Zone:**
- Bridges sensory (posterior) and affective (anterior) processing
- Progressive abstraction of body signals

## Part 4: Anterior Insula — Emotional Awareness and Subjective Feelings

**Location:** Anterior portion of the insula, including the three short gyri

**Cytoarchitecture:** Agranular — thin or absent Layer IV; similar to limbic cortex

**Function:** Emotional awareness, subjective feelings, conscious interoception. Transforms raw body signals into felt emotional states. Critical hub of the **salience network**.

### Subdivisions

**Ventral Anterior Insula (vAI):**
- More connected with limbic structures
- Emotional processing
- Disgust, other basic emotions

**Dorsal Anterior Insula (dAI):**
- More connected with frontal cortex
- Cognitive-emotional integration
- Salience detection
- Part of salience network with dACC

### Within Insular Lobe

**From Mid-Insula:**
- Mid-insula Layer III → Anterior insula Layers II/III, V
- Feedforward (but anterior is agranular)

**From Posterior Insula:**
- Via mid-insula or direct
- Integrated interoceptive information

**To Posterior/Mid-Insula (Feedback):**
- Layer V/VI → Posterior/Mid Layer I
- Top-down emotional modulation of body sensation

### With Frontal Lobe

**To/From ACC (Anterior Cingulate Cortex):**
- **MAJOR BIDIRECTIONAL CONNECTION**
- Anterior insula + dACC = core of **salience network**
- Salience detection, switching between networks
- Emotional-cognitive integration

**To/From OFC (Orbitofrontal Cortex):**
- Value, reward
- Disgust processing
- Taste evaluation

**To/From vmPFC:**
- Emotional regulation
- Self-awareness

**To/From VLPFC:**
- Emotional working memory
- Cognitive control of emotion

**To/From DLPFC:**
- Interoceptive information for decision making
- "Gut feelings" in cognition

**From Broca's Area:**
- Speech production monitoring
- Articulatory planning

### With Parietal Lobe

**To/From Inferior Parietal:**
- Body awareness
- Self-other distinction

**To/From TPJ (Temporoparietal Junction):**
- Self-awareness
- Agency

### With Temporal Lobe

**To/From Amygdala:**
- **MAJOR BIDIRECTIONAL CONNECTION**
- Emotional processing
- Fear, disgust
- Anterior insula-amygdala circuit for emotion

**To/From Temporal Pole:**
- Social-emotional processing
- Person knowledge

**To/From Superior Temporal (STS):**
- Social perception
- Voice emotion

### With Thalamus

**From MD (Mediodorsal Nucleus):**
- Prefrontal-type input
- Bidirectional

**To MD:**
- Layer VI feedback

### With Basal Ganglia

**To Ventral Striatum (Nucleus Accumbens):**
- **Layer V → NAcc**
- Motivation, reward
- Interoceptive signals influence motivation

**To Caudate:**
- Cognitive aspects

### With Hypothalamus

**To/From Hypothalamus:**
- **Bidirectional**
- Autonomic control
- Anterior insula influences sympathetic/parasympathetic balance
- Receives homeostatic state information

### With Brainstem

**To PAG (Periaqueductal Gray):**
- Emotional behavior
- Autonomic responses

**To Autonomic Brainstem Nuclei:**
- Via hypothalamus
- Heart rate, blood pressure control
- Anterior insula stimulation can cause cardiovascular changes

### Functional Properties

**Emotional Awareness:**
- Transforms body signals into conscious feelings
- "How do I feel?"
- Craig's theory: Anterior insula represents the "sentient self"

**Disgust:**
- Primary cortical region for disgust
- Both physical disgust (bad taste, smell) and moral disgust
- Lesions reduce disgust recognition and experience

**Salience Detection:**
- Part of salience network (with dACC)
- Detects important events
- Switches attention to salient stimuli

**Empathy:**
- Activated when experiencing and observing emotions in others
- Particularly for pain empathy, disgust empathy
- Mirror-like properties for emotional states

**Interoceptive Awareness:**
- Individual differences in interoceptive accuracy correlate with anterior insula structure/function
- Better heartbeat detection = more anterior insula activity

**Social Emotions:**
- Guilt, embarrassment, pride
- Social evaluation

**Risk and Uncertainty:**
- Active during risky decisions
- "Gut feeling" about risk

**Time Perception:**
- Involved in subjective time estimation
- Interoceptive basis for time sense?

**Addiction:**
- Anterior insula implicated in craving
- Lesions can reduce addiction (case reports)
- Interoceptive signals drive drug seeking

### The Salience Network

Anterior insula is a core hub of the **salience network**:

**Core regions:**
- Anterior insula (bilateral)
- Dorsal ACC (dACC)

**Function:**
- Detects salient events (important, unexpected)
- Switches between default mode network (internal) and central executive network (external)
- "Circuit breaker" that redirects attention

**Clinical relevance:**
- Disrupted in many psychiatric conditions
- Anxiety, depression, schizophrenia, autism

## Part 5: Gustatory Cortex (Primary Taste Cortex)

**Location:** Anterior insula and adjacent frontal operculum

**Brodmann Area:** Sometimes called area G (gustatory)

**Function:** Primary taste cortex. Receives taste information from thalamus. Represents taste qualities (sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami).

### Thalamic Input

**From VPMpc (Ventral Posteromedial Nucleus, parvicellular):**
- **MAJOR INPUT** — driver
- Taste information from nucleus of solitary tract
- Topographic taste map (different qualities in different locations)
- Layer IV

### With Frontal Lobe

**To OFC:**
- **Layer III → OFC**
- Secondary taste cortex
- Taste + smell = flavor
- Hedonic value of taste

**From OFC (Feedback):**
- OFC → Gustatory cortex
- Top-down modulation
- Satiety signals

### With Amygdala

**To Amygdala:**
- Taste-emotion associations
- Aversive taste learning

### Functional Properties

**Taste Representation:**
- Sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami
- Topographic organization (debated)

**Taste Intensity:**
- Encodes intensity of taste

**Multimodal Integration:**
- Some integration with smell, texture
- Full "flavor" processing in OFC

## Part 6: Communication with Other Systems — Summary

### Thalamic Connections

| Insular Region | Thalamic Nucleus | Direction | Function |
|----------------|------------------|-----------|----------|
| Posterior | VMpo | From | Interoceptive (visceral) |
| Posterior | VPL/VPM | From | Somatosensory (pain, temp) |
| Posterior | Vestibular (VPP) | From | Vestibular |
| Posterior | Thalamus | To (Layer VI) | Feedback |
| Mid/Anterior | VMb | From | Taste |
| Anterior | MD | Bidirectional | Association, emotion |

### Cortical Connections Summary

**With Frontal Lobe:**
- Anterior insula ↔ ACC: Salience network
- Anterior insula ↔ OFC: Taste, value, emotion
- Anterior insula ↔ vmPFC: Emotion regulation
- Anterior insula ↔ DLPFC/VLPFC: Cognitive-emotional integration

**With Parietal Lobe:**
- Posterior insula ↔ S2: Somatosensory
- Posterior insula ↔ VIP: Vestibular-spatial
- Anterior insula ↔ IPL: Body awareness

**With Temporal Lobe:**
- Anterior insula ↔ Amygdala: Emotion
- Anterior insula ↔ Temporal pole: Social-emotional

### Subcortical Connections

**Amygdala:**
- Bidirectional with anterior insula
- Emotional processing

**Hypothalamus:**
- Bidirectional
- Autonomic control
- Homeostatic integration

**Ventral Striatum:**
- Layer V → Nucleus accumbens
- Motivation, reward

**Brainstem:**
- PAG, autonomic nuclei
- Autonomic, emotional responses

## Part 7: Functional Networks Involving Insula

### Salience Network

**Core hubs:** Anterior insula (bilateral), dACC

**Function:** Detects salient events, switches between networks

**Other regions:** Amygdala, ventral striatum, thalamus

**Role of insula:** Integrates interoceptive and external salience; triggers network switching

### Interoceptive Network

**Core hub:** Insula (posterior → anterior hierarchy)

**Function:** Represents body's internal state

**Other regions:** ACC, vmPFC, somatosensory cortex, hypothalamus

**Hierarchical organization:**
1. Posterior insula — primary interoceptive
2. Mid-insula — integration
3. Anterior insula — conscious awareness of body state

### Central Executive Network

**Core hubs:** DLPFC, posterior parietal

**Insula connection:** Anterior insula gates access to executive network

**Function:** Goal-directed cognition

### Default Mode Network

**Core hubs:** vmPFC, PCC/precuneus

**Insula connection:** Salience network (with insula) switches between DMN and executive network

**Anticorrelation:** Anterior insula activity anticorrelated with DMN during external attention

## Part 9: Clinical Implications

### Insular Lesions

**Right anterior insula lesions:**
- Anosognosia (unawareness of deficit)
- Reduced emotional awareness
- Altered sense of body ownership

**Left anterior insula lesions:**
- Speech production deficits (near Broca's area)
- Reduced disgust recognition

**Bilateral insular damage:**
- Severe autonomic dysregulation
- Altered pain perception
- Reduced emotional experience

### Psychiatric Conditions

**Anxiety Disorders:**
- Hyperactive anterior insula
- Excessive interoceptive awareness
- Amplified body signals

**Depression:**
- Altered insula-ACC connectivity
- Disrupted salience network

**Addiction:**
- Anterior insula involved in craving
- Interoceptive signals drive drug seeking
- Lesions may reduce addiction

**Eating Disorders:**
- Altered interoceptive processing
- Disrupted hunger/satiety signals

**Autism Spectrum:**
- Altered interoceptive processing
- Reduced emotional awareness

### Functional Neurological Disorders

**Altered interoception:**
- Mismatch between actual and perceived body state
- Insula dysfunction implicated

### Stroke and Cardiovascular

**Insular stroke:**
- Can cause cardiac arrhythmias
- Autonomic instability
- Insula controls autonomic outflow
