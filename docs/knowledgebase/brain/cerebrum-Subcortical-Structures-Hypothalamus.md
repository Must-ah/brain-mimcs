# Region-Centric Communication Map: Cerebrum — Subcortical Structures — Hypothalamus

This document provides a complete communication map for every major nucleus of the hypothalamus. For each nucleus, it specifies what that region communicates with at every level: within the hypothalamus, with other subcortical structures, with the brainstem, with the spinal cord, and with the cerebral cortex.

The hypothalamus is the master controller of homeostasis — the maintenance of stable internal conditions. Despite being only about 4 grams (the size of an almond), it regulates hunger, thirst, body temperature, circadian rhythms, sleep-wake cycles, sexual behavior, stress responses, and the entire endocrine system through its control of the pituitary gland. The hypothalamus integrates sensory information about internal body state with emotional and cognitive information from limbic structures and cortex.

---

## Part 1: Overview of Hypothalamic Organization

### Anatomical Location

The hypothalamus is located below the thalamus (hypo = under), forming the floor and lower walls of the third ventricle. It extends from the optic chiasm anteriorly to the mammillary bodies posteriorly. The pituitary gland hangs below it, connected by the infundibulum (pituitary stalk).

### Regional Organization

The hypothalamus is divided into three regions from anterior to posterior:

**Anterior (Supraoptic/Chiasmatic) Region:** Contains nuclei involved in circadian rhythms, temperature regulation, and hormone production.

**Middle (Tuberal) Region:** Contains nuclei involved in feeding, growth, and the connection to the pituitary.

**Posterior (Mammillary) Region:** Contains nuclei involved in memory, arousal, and temperature regulation.

The hypothalamus is also divided medial to lateral:

**Periventricular Zone:** Adjacent to the third ventricle; contains neurons that release hormones into the pituitary portal system.

**Medial Zone:** Contains most of the distinct nuclei.

**Lateral Zone:** Contains the lateral hypothalamic area; diffuse neurons involved in arousal and feeding.

### Functional Principles

**Homeostatic Control:** The hypothalamus senses internal state (via blood-borne signals, neural afferents) and initiates corrective responses (via autonomic, endocrine, and behavioral outputs).

**Endocrine Control:** The hypothalamus controls the pituitary gland through two mechanisms:
- Releasing/inhibiting hormones → anterior pituitary (via portal blood system)
- Direct axonal projection → posterior pituitary (neurosecretion)

**Integration Hub:** The hypothalamus receives input from limbic structures (amygdala, hippocampus), prefrontal cortex, brainstem, and spinal cord, integrating emotional, cognitive, and physiological information.

## Part 2: Anterior (Supraoptic) Region

### Suprachiasmatic Nucleus (SCN)

**Location and Function:** Located just above the optic chiasm. The master circadian clock — generates ~24-hour rhythms that synchronize all body functions to the day-night cycle.

**Within Hypothalamus:**
- **To other hypothalamic nuclei:** Distributes circadian timing signals
  - To paraventricular nucleus: Rhythmic hormone release
  - To lateral hypothalamic area: Sleep-wake timing
  - To preoptic area: Temperature rhythm
  - To dorsomedial nucleus: Major relay for SCN output
- **From other hypothalamic nuclei:** Feedback from arousal systems

**Within Subcortical Structures:**
- **From retina (direct):** DRIVER input via retinohypothalamic tract
  - Specialized intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs)
  - Contains melanopsin (sensitive to blue light)
  - This entrains the clock to external light-dark cycle
- **To/from thalamus (paraventricular thalamic nucleus):** Circadian modulation of arousal
- **To/from basal forebrain:** Sleep-wake regulation
- **From intergeniculate leaflet (thalamus):** Secondary light input

**To Brainstem:**
- **To raphe nuclei:** Modulates serotonin (sleep-wake)
- **To locus coeruleus:** Modulates norepinephrine (arousal rhythm)
- **To VTA:** Modulates dopamine rhythm
- **To PAG:** Modulates daily pain sensitivity

**To Spinal Cord:**
- Via relay through paraventricular nucleus → intermediolateral column
- Controls circadian rhythm of autonomic function

**To Cerebral Cortex:**
- Indirect via thalamus and basal forebrain
- No direct cortical projections

**From Cerebral Cortex:**
- Minimal direct input
- Some from infralimbic cortex (mPFC)

**Intrinsic Properties:**
- Contains ~20,000 neurons
- Neurons generate autonomous ~24-hour rhythms (even in isolation)
- Core region: Receives retinal input, light-responsive
- Shell region: Outputs to rest of hypothalamus

**Primary Inputs:**
- Retina (ipRGCs): Light entrainment
- Intergeniculate leaflet: Secondary photic input
- Raphe nuclei: Serotonergic (behavioral state)

**Primary Outputs:**
- Dorsomedial hypothalamus: Major relay
- Paraventricular nucleus: Hormone rhythms
- Lateral hypothalamic area: Arousal rhythms


### Supraoptic Nucleus (SON)

**Location and Function:** Located above the optic tract, lateral to the optic chiasm. Produces vasopressin (antidiuretic hormone, ADH) and oxytocin for release from the posterior pituitary.

**Within Hypothalamus:**
- **Adjacent to paraventricular nucleus:** Both produce vasopressin and oxytocin
- **From osmoreceptors (in organum vasculosum):** Detects blood osmolality
- **From other hypothalamic regions:** Integrated homeostatic control

**Within Subcortical Structures:**
- **From subfornical organ:** Blood-borne signals (angiotensin II for thirst)
- **From organum vasculosum of lamina terminalis (OVLT):** Osmolality sensing

**From Brainstem:**
- **From nucleus of solitary tract (NTS):** Cardiovascular information (baroreceptor, volume)
- **From parabrachial nucleus:** Visceral information
- **From locus coeruleus:** Noradrenergic modulation
- **From raphe nuclei:** Serotonergic modulation

**To Pituitary:**
- **Axons project directly to posterior pituitary (neurohypophysis):**
  - Neurosecretory neurons
  - Release vasopressin and oxytocin directly into blood
  - Vasopressin: Water retention by kidneys
  - Oxytocin: Uterine contraction, milk ejection, social bonding

**To Cerebral Cortex:**
- No direct projections

**From Cerebral Cortex:**
- Minimal direct input

**Primary Inputs:**
- Osmoreceptors: Blood concentration
- NTS: Volume status
- Circumventricular organs: Blood-borne signals

**Primary Outputs:**
- Posterior pituitary: Vasopressin, oxytocin release


### Preoptic Area (POA)

**Location and Function:** Located at the most anterior part of the hypothalamus, merging with the basal forebrain. Critical for thermoregulation, sleep initiation, and reproductive behavior. Contains several subnuclei.

#### Medial Preoptic Area (mPOA)

**Function:** Sexual behavior, parental behavior, thermoregulation

**Within Hypothalamus:**
- **To ventromedial hypothalamus:** Sexual behavior coordination
- **To arcuate nucleus:** GnRH neurons for reproductive hormones
- **From SCN:** Circadian timing of reproduction

**Within Subcortical Structures:**
- **From amygdala (medial):** Pheromone and social information
- **To/from bed nucleus of stria terminalis:** Extended amygdala, sexual behavior
- **From hippocampus:** Contextual information

**From Brainstem:**
- **From PAG:** Reproductive behavior coordination
- **To PAG:** Initiates lordosis/mounting behavior

**To Spinal Cord:**
- Via PAG relay → spinal cord: Sexual reflexes

**To Cerebral Cortex:**
- Minimal direct

**From Cerebral Cortex:**
- **From mPFC:** Cognitive modulation of sexual behavior
- **From insula:** Interoceptive information

**Functions:**
- Contains sexually dimorphic nucleus (larger in males)
- Controls male and female sexual behavior
- Parental behavior (especially maternal)
- GnRH release for reproduction

#### Ventrolateral Preoptic Area (VLPO)

**Function:** Sleep initiation — the "sleep switch"

**Within Hypothalamus:**
- **To lateral hypothalamic area (orexin neurons):** INHIBITS orexin during sleep
- **To tuberomammillary nucleus (histamine):** INHIBITS histamine during sleep
- **From SCN:** Circadian sleep timing

**Within Subcortical Structures:**
- **To basal forebrain (cholinergic):** Inhibits during sleep

**To Brainstem:**
- **To locus coeruleus:** INHIBITS norepinephrine during sleep
- **To raphe nuclei:** INHIBITS serotonin during sleep
- **To VTA:** Modulates dopamine during sleep
- **To PPT/LDT:** Modulates cholinergic arousal

**Key Function:**
- VLPO neurons are GABAergic/galaninergic
- Active during sleep, silent during waking
- Inhibit all arousal systems simultaneously
- "Flip-flop" switch with orexin neurons determines sleep vs wake

**Primary Inputs:**
- SCN: Circadian timing
- Adenosine accumulation: Sleep pressure

**Primary Outputs:**
- All arousal centers (inhibitory): Sleep initiation

#### Median Preoptic Nucleus (MnPO)

**Function:** Thermoregulation, fluid balance

**Within Hypothalamus:**
- **To lateral hypothalamic area:** Initiates drinking
- **To paraventricular nucleus:** Hormone release for fluid balance

**Within Subcortical Structures:**
- **From subfornical organ:** Thirst signals (angiotensin II)
- **From OVLT:** Osmolality, pyrogens (fever)

**To Brainstem:**
- **To rostral ventrolateral medulla:** Cardiovascular responses to temperature
- **To raphe pallidus:** Brown fat thermogenesis

**Functions:**
- Detects fever signals (prostaglandins)
- Initiates fever response
- Fluid intake regulation


### Anterior Hypothalamic Area (AHA)

**Location and Function:** Located posterior to the preoptic area. Involved in heat dissipation (cooling responses) and aggression.

**Within Hypothalamus:**
- **To posterior hypothalamus:** Opposes heat conservation
- **From preoptic area:** Temperature sensing

**To Brainstem:**
- **To rostral ventrolateral medulla:** Vasodilation for cooling
- **To salivatory nuclei:** Panting in some species

**To Spinal Cord:**
- Via autonomic pathways: Sweating, vasodilation

**Functions:**
- Heat dissipation responses
- Defensive aggression (with amygdala input)

## Part 3: Middle (Tuberal) Region

### Arcuate Nucleus (ARC)

**Location and Function:** Located at the base of the hypothalamus, surrounding the median eminence. Critical for feeding regulation and growth hormone control. Contains neurons that sense metabolic hormones.

**Within Hypothalamus:**
- **To paraventricular nucleus:** Feeding regulation, hormone release
- **To lateral hypothalamic area:** Feeding drive
- **To ventromedial hypothalamus:** Satiety signals
- **To dorsomedial hypothalamus:** Feeding and arousal integration

**Key Neuronal Populations:**
- **POMC/CART neurons:** Anorexigenic (reduce feeding)
  - Produce α-MSH → MC4 receptors → satiety
  - Activated by leptin, insulin
- **AgRP/NPY neurons:** Orexigenic (promote feeding)
  - Produce neuropeptide Y and AgRP → increase feeding
  - Inhibited by leptin, activated by ghrelin

**Within Subcortical Structures:**
- **Senses blood-borne hormones (adjacent to median eminence, reduced BBB):**
  - Leptin (from fat): Signals energy stores, activates POMC, inhibits AgRP
  - Ghrelin (from stomach): Signals hunger, activates AgRP
  - Insulin (from pancreas): Signals fed state
  - Glucose: Direct sensing
- **To/from amygdala:** Emotional aspects of feeding

**From Brainstem:**
- **From NTS:** Satiety signals from gut (CCK, GLP-1)
- **From parabrachial nucleus:** Taste, visceral information

**To Pituitary:**
- **To median eminence → anterior pituitary:**
  - GHRH (growth hormone-releasing hormone): Stimulates GH release
  - Dopamine (from tuberoinfundibular neurons): Inhibits prolactin
  - Also modulates other hypothalamic releasing hormones

**To Cerebral Cortex:**
- Indirect via other hypothalamic relays

**From Cerebral Cortex:**
- **From mPFC:** Cognitive control of feeding
- **From insula:** Interoceptive signals

**Primary Inputs:**
- Leptin, ghrelin, insulin (blood-borne): Metabolic state
- NTS: Gut satiety signals
- SCN: Circadian feeding timing

**Primary Outputs:**
- Paraventricular nucleus: CRH, TRH modulation
- Lateral hypothalamus: Feeding drive
- Median eminence: GHRH, dopamine


### Ventromedial Nucleus (VMN/VMH)

**Location and Function:** Located in the medial hypothalamus. The "satiety center" — signals fullness and stops eating. Also involved in female sexual behavior and defensive aggression.

**Within Hypothalamus:**
- **From arcuate nucleus:** POMC/α-MSH satiety signals
- **To lateral hypothalamic area:** Inhibits feeding
- **To/from preoptic area:** Sexual behavior
- **From SCN:** Circadian feeding timing

**Within Subcortical Structures:**
- **From amygdala (ventromedial):** Emotional significance of food, threats
- **Senses metabolic signals:**
  - Glucose-sensitive neurons (glucostat hypothesis)
  - Leptin receptors

**From Brainstem:**
- **From NTS:** Satiety signals
- **From parabrachial:** Taste aversion

**To Brainstem:**
- **To PAG:** Defensive behavior (aggression, freezing)
- **To reticular formation:** Arousal related to threats

**To Spinal Cord:**
- Via PAG → spinal cord: Defensive postures

**To Cerebral Cortex:**
- Indirect

**From Cerebral Cortex:**
- **From vmPFC:** Decision-making about food
- **From insula:** Interoception

**Lesion Effects:**
- Bilateral VMH lesions → hyperphagia (overeating) and obesity
- Demonstrates satiety function

**Functions:**
- Satiety signaling
- Female lordosis behavior (via PAG)
- Defensive rage/aggression

**Primary Inputs:**
- Arcuate nucleus (POMC): Satiety
- Leptin, glucose: Metabolic state
- Amygdala: Emotional significance

**Primary Outputs:**
- Lateral hypothalamus: Inhibits feeding
- PAG: Defensive behavior


### Dorsomedial Nucleus (DMH)

**Location and Function:** Located dorsal to the VMN. Integrates circadian information with feeding, arousal, and stress responses. Major relay station for SCN outputs.

**Within Hypothalamus:**
- **From SCN:** MAJOR input; primary relay for circadian signals
- **To lateral hypothalamic area (orexin neurons):** Circadian arousal
- **To paraventricular nucleus:** Circadian hormone release
- **From arcuate nucleus:** Feeding signals
- **From VMN:** Satiety information

**Within Subcortical Structures:**
- **From amygdala:** Stress and emotional information
- **To/from bed nucleus of stria terminalis:** Anxiety, stress

**To Brainstem:**
- **To locus coeruleus:** Arousal (circadian modulation)
- **To raphe nuclei:** Serotonin modulation
- **To PAG:** Defensive behavior

**To Spinal Cord:**
- Via autonomic pathways: Circadian autonomic rhythms

**Functions:**
- Circadian rhythm distribution (relay from SCN)
- Feeding regulation integration
- Stress and anxiety responses
- Thermogenesis control

**Primary Inputs:**
- SCN: Circadian timing
- Arcuate, VMN: Feeding signals
- Amygdala: Stress

**Primary Outputs:**
- Lateral hypothalamus: Arousal timing
- Paraventricular nucleus: Hormone timing
- Brainstem arousal centers: Circadian modulation


### Lateral Hypothalamic Area (LHA)

**Location and Function:** Extends throughout the lateral hypothalamus. Contains diffusely organized neurons including orexin/hypocretin neurons. Involved in feeding, arousal, reward, and motivated behavior.

**Within Hypothalamus:**
- **From arcuate nucleus (AgRP/NPY):** Hunger signals
- **From VMN:** Satiety inhibition
- **From DMH:** Circadian timing
- **From SCN (via DMH):** Sleep-wake timing
- **To all other hypothalamic nuclei:** Widespread influence

**Key Neuronal Populations:**
- **Orexin/Hypocretin neurons:**
  - Only ~70,000 neurons in humans
  - Project throughout entire CNS
  - Promote and stabilize wakefulness
  - Loss causes narcolepsy
- **MCH (melanin-concentrating hormone) neurons:**
  - Promote sleep (opposite of orexin)
  - Also involved in feeding
- **Other populations:** GABA, glutamate neurons

**Within Subcortical Structures:**
- **To/from amygdala:** Emotional aspects of feeding, reward
- **From nucleus accumbens:** Reward-related feeding
- **To VTA:** Modulates dopamine (reward)
- **To basal forebrain:** Cholinergic arousal
- **From hippocampus:** Contextual feeding

**To Brainstem:**
- **To locus coeruleus:** MAJOR orexin projection; arousal
- **To raphe nuclei:** MAJOR orexin projection; arousal
- **To PPT/LDT:** Cholinergic arousal
- **To VTA:** Reward and motivation
- **To PAG:** Defensive behavior
- **To NTS:** Feeding regulation
- **To reticular formation:** General arousal

**To Spinal Cord:**
- **Direct hypothalamospinal projections:** Sympathetic activation
- **To intermediolateral column:** Autonomic control

**To Cerebral Cortex:**
- **Orexin projections to entire cortex:**
  - Especially prefrontal cortex
  - Promotes cortical activation
  - Maintains wakefulness

**From Cerebral Cortex:**
- **From prefrontal cortex:** Cognitive control of feeding
- **From insula:** Interoception
- **From limbic cortex:** Emotional feeding

**Lesion Effects:**
- Bilateral LHA lesions → aphagia (failure to eat), adipsia (failure to drink), reduced arousal
- Demonstrates feeding/arousal function

**Orexin System:**
- Orexin-A and Orexin-B (hypocretin-1 and -2)
- Stabilize "flip-flop" sleep-wake switch
- Inhibited by VLPO during sleep
- Loss → narcolepsy (inappropriate sleep intrusions)

**Functions:**
- Feeding initiation ("hunger center")
- Arousal and wakefulness (orexin)
- Reward-related behavior
- Autonomic activation

**Primary Inputs:**
- Arcuate (AgRP): Hunger
- VMN: Satiety (inhibitory)
- VLPO: Sleep (inhibitory to orexin)
- Amygdala, NAc: Emotion, reward

**Primary Outputs:**
- Entire CNS (orexin): Arousal
- Brainstem arousal centers: Wakefulness
- Cortex: Activation


### Tuberomammillary Nucleus (TMN)

**Location and Function:** Located in the posterior lateral hypothalamus, near the mammillary bodies. Contains all brain histamine neurons. Critical for arousal and wakefulness.

**Within Hypothalamus:**
- **From orexin neurons (LHA):** Excitatory; promotes waking
- **From VLPO:** Inhibitory; allows sleep
- **From SCN (via DMH):** Circadian timing

**Within Subcortical Structures:**
- **To basal forebrain:** Histaminergic arousal
- **To striatum:** Modulates basal ganglia

**To Brainstem:**
- **To all brainstem arousal nuclei:**
  - Locus coeruleus
  - Raphe nuclei
  - PPT/LDT
- Histamine promotes wakefulness

**To Cerebral Cortex:**
- **To entire cortex:** Histamine promotes cortical activation
- Especially prefrontal cortex

**To Thalamus:**
- **To all thalamic nuclei:** Promotes tonic (relay) mode

**Functions:**
- Arousal and wakefulness
- Active during waking, silent during sleep
- Part of ascending arousal system
- Antihistamines cause drowsiness by blocking this system

**Primary Inputs:**
- Orexin (excitatory): Promotes waking
- VLPO (inhibitory): Allows sleep

**Primary Outputs:**
- Entire CNS: Histaminergic arousal

## Part 4: Posterior (Mammillary) Region

### Mammillary Bodies

**Location and Function:** Two spherical structures at the posterior base of the hypothalamus. Critical relay in the Papez circuit for memory. Contain medial and lateral mammillary nuclei.

**Within Hypothalamus:**
- **From other hypothalamic regions:** Minor

**Within Subcortical Structures:**
- **From hippocampus (via fornix):** MAJOR input
  - Subiculum → postcommissural fornix → mammillary bodies
  - Memory information from hippocampus
- **To anterior thalamic nucleus (via mammillothalamic tract):** MAJOR output
  - Part of Papez circuit
  - Memory relay to cingulate cortex

**From Brainstem:**
- **From tegmental nuclei (via mammillary peduncle):** Ascending information
- **From dorsal tegmental nucleus:** Head direction signals

**To Brainstem:**
- **To tegmental nuclei (via mammillotegmental tract):** Feedback
- **To dorsal tegmental nucleus:** Head direction circuit

**Papez Circuit:**
Hippocampus → Fornix → **Mammillary bodies** → Mammillothalamic tract → Anterior thalamus → Cingulate cortex → Cingulum → Parahippocampal cortex → Hippocampus

**Subdivisions:**
- **Medial mammillary nucleus (large):** Memory; projects to anterior thalamus (AV, AM)
- **Lateral mammillary nucleus (small):** Head direction; projects to anterodorsal thalamus (AD)

**Functions:**
- Episodic memory (Papez circuit)
- Spatial memory and navigation
- Head direction system component

**Clinical Significance:**
- Damage (often from thiamine deficiency in alcoholism) → Korsakoff's syndrome
- Characterized by anterograde amnesia and confabulation

**Primary Inputs:**
- Hippocampus (via fornix): Memory

**Primary Outputs:**
- Anterior thalamus: Memory relay to cortex


### Posterior Hypothalamic Area (PHA)

**Location and Function:** Located in the posterior hypothalamus, dorsal to mammillary bodies. Involved in heat conservation, arousal, and blood pressure regulation.

**Within Hypothalamus:**
- **From anterior hypothalamus:** Temperature regulation (opposes cooling)
- **From lateral hypothalamus:** Arousal
- **From SCN (via relays):** Circadian timing

**Within Subcortical Structures:**
- **From amygdala:** Defensive arousal

**To Brainstem:**
- **To locus coeruleus:** Arousal
- **To reticular formation:** General activation
- **To rostral ventrolateral medulla:** Blood pressure increase

**To Spinal Cord:**
- **To intermediolateral column:** Sympathetic activation
- Heat conservation: Vasoconstriction, shivering

**Functions:**
- Heat conservation (shivering, vasoconstriction)
- Arousal and wakefulness
- Blood pressure regulation
- Defensive responses

**Primary Inputs:**
- Anterior hypothalamus: Temperature
- Amygdala: Threat

**Primary Outputs:**
- Brainstem: Arousal, autonomic
- Spinal cord: Sympathetic activation

## Part 5: Paraventricular Nucleus (PVN)

The PVN deserves special attention as it is the master controller of stress responses and a major neuroendocrine output center.

**Location and Function:** Located adjacent to the third ventricle in the anterior hypothalamus. Contains multiple neuronal populations controlling stress, autonomic function, and hormone release.

### Magnocellular Division

**Function:** Produces hormones for posterior pituitary release

**Neurons:**
- Vasopressin neurons → posterior pituitary → water retention
- Oxytocin neurons → posterior pituitary → uterine contraction, milk ejection, social bonding

**Same function as supraoptic nucleus (SON)**

### Parvocellular Division

**Function:** Produces releasing hormones for anterior pituitary

**Key Neurons:**
- **CRH (corticotropin-releasing hormone) neurons:**
  - Release CRH → anterior pituitary
  - Stimulates ACTH release
  - ACTH → adrenal cortex → cortisol
  - THIS IS THE HPA AXIS (stress response)
- **TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone) neurons:**
  - Release TRH → anterior pituitary
  - Stimulates TSH release
  - TSH → thyroid → T3/T4 (metabolism)
- **Other releasing hormone neurons:**
  - Contribute to GnRH, GHRH, somatostatin regulation

### Autonomic Division

**Function:** Direct control of autonomic nervous system (descending projections)

**To Brainstem:**
- **To NTS:** Cardiovascular, respiratory regulation
- **To dorsal motor nucleus of vagus:** Parasympathetic control
- **To rostral ventrolateral medulla (RVLM):** Sympathetic control
- **To nucleus ambiguus:** Heart rate (vagal)
- **To parabrachial nucleus:** Visceral integration

**To Spinal Cord:**
- **To intermediolateral column (T1-L2):** Sympathetic preganglionic neurons
- **To sacral parasympathetic (S2-S4):** Pelvic parasympathetic

### Inputs to PVN

**Within Hypothalamus:**
- **From arcuate nucleus:** Metabolic signals
- **From SCN:** Circadian timing of HPA axis
- **From preoptic area:** Temperature
- **From VMN:** Feeding-related

**Within Subcortical Structures:**
- **From amygdala (central and medial):** MAJOR input
  - Activates stress response
  - Emotional triggers for HPA axis
- **From bed nucleus of stria terminalis:** Sustained anxiety
- **From hippocampus (via relays):** Inhibitory
  - Contextual inhibition of stress
  - Negative feedback
- **From subfornical organ, OVLT:** Blood-borne signals

**From Brainstem:**
- **From NTS:** Visceral afferents, cardiovascular
- **From locus coeruleus:** Noradrenergic activation of stress
- **From raphe nuclei:** Serotonergic modulation
- **From parabrachial nucleus:** Visceral information
- **From PAG:** Defensive stress

**From Cerebral Cortex:**
- **From medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC):**
  - Inhibitory (infralimbic cortex)
  - Regulates stress response
  - Top-down control of HPA axis
- **From insula:** Interoceptive information
- **From anterior cingulate:** Stress and pain

### HPA Axis Summary

1. Stressor detected (by amygdala, brainstem, or directly)
2. PVN activated
3. CRH released into hypophyseal portal blood
4. Anterior pituitary releases ACTH
5. ACTH travels through blood to adrenal cortex
6. Adrenal cortex releases cortisol
7. Cortisol provides negative feedback to PVN, pituitary, hippocampus

**Primary Inputs:**
- Amygdala: Emotional stress
- Brainstem (NTS, LC): Physiological stress
- mPFC: Inhibitory control
- Hippocampus: Contextual inhibition

**Primary Outputs:**
- Median eminence → anterior pituitary: CRH, TRH
- Posterior pituitary: Vasopressin, oxytocin
- Brainstem: Autonomic control
- Spinal cord: Sympathetic/parasympathetic

## Part 6: Hypothalamic Connection to Pituitary

### Anterior Pituitary (Adenohypophysis)

**Connection Type:** Hormonal via hypophyseal portal system

**Mechanism:**
1. Hypothalamic neurons release hormones into capillaries in median eminence
2. Portal blood carries hormones to anterior pituitary
3. Anterior pituitary cells respond by releasing their hormones into systemic circulation

**Hypothalamic Releasing/Inhibiting Hormones:**

| Hypothalamic Hormone | Source | Effect on Anterior Pituitary |
|---------------------|--------|------------------------------|
| CRH | PVN | Releases ACTH → cortisol |
| TRH | PVN | Releases TSH → thyroid hormones |
| GnRH | Preoptic, arcuate | Releases FSH, LH → sex hormones |
| GHRH | Arcuate | Releases GH → growth |
| Somatostatin | Periventricular | Inhibits GH |
| Dopamine | Arcuate (tuberoinfundibular) | Inhibits prolactin |

### Posterior Pituitary (Neurohypophysis)

**Connection Type:** Direct axonal projection (neurosecretion)

**Mechanism:**
1. Hypothalamic neurons (in SON and PVN) produce hormones in cell body
2. Hormones transported down axons to posterior pituitary
3. Hormones released directly into blood from axon terminals

**Hormones:**
- **Vasopressin (ADH):** Water retention, vasoconstriction
- **Oxytocin:** Uterine contraction, milk ejection, social bonding

## Part 7: Hypothalamic Communication Summary

### With Brainstem

| Hypothalamic Region | Brainstem Target | Function |
|--------------------|------------------|----------|
| PVN (autonomic) | NTS, RVLM, DMV | Autonomic control |
| LHA (orexin) | LC, raphe, PPT/LDT, VTA | Arousal |
| VLPO | LC, raphe, VTA | Sleep (inhibitory) |
| VMN, PHA | PAG | Defensive behavior |
| Arcuate, PVN | NTS | Feeding regulation |
| TMN (histamine) | All arousal centers | Wakefulness |

### With Spinal Cord

| Pathway | Origin | Target | Function |
|---------|--------|--------|----------|
| Hypothalamospinal | PVN, LHA, PHA | IML (T1-L2) | Sympathetic control |
| Hypothalamospinal | PVN | Sacral (S2-S4) | Parasympathetic (pelvic) |

### With Subcortical Structures

| Hypothalamic Region | Subcortical Structure | Function |
|--------------------|----------------------|----------|
| PVN | Amygdala (receives from) | Stress activation |
| Mammillary bodies | Hippocampus (receives), anterior thalamus (projects) | Memory |
| LHA | VTA, NAc | Reward, motivation |
| Arcuate, VMN, LHA | Amygdala (bidirectional) | Emotional feeding |
| All | Basal forebrain | Arousal, sleep |

### With Cerebral Cortex

**To Cortex:**
- Primarily indirect via thalamus
- Orexin (from LHA) → direct to cortex (arousal)
- Histamine (from TMN) → direct to cortex (arousal)

**From Cortex:**
- **mPFC (especially infralimbic):** Stress regulation
- **Insula:** Interoception
- **Anterior cingulate:** Pain, stress
- **Orbitofrontal:** Value, feeding decisions
