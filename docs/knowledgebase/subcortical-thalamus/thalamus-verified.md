## Updates Needed

### Update 1: TRN Sectoral Heterogeneity

**Current KB:** Describes TRN as single functional unit with sector organization matching thalamic nuclei.

**Research Update (eLife 2018; Eur J Neurosci 2024):**
- TRN parcellated into **at least 5 modality-specific sectors** (sensory, motor, limbic)
- Sensory TRN sectors show stronger repetitive burst discharge than non-sensory
- Difference due to **CaV3.3 (T-type Ca²⁺ channel)** expression
- Rostral (limbic) TRN: silencing elongates sleep episodes
- Caudal (sensory) TRN: silencing fragments sleep

**Suggested Addition:**
```markdown
**TRN Sectoral Heterogeneity:**
The TRN is parcellated into at least 5 modality-specific sectors based on 
innervation by corresponding dorsal thalamic nuclei:
- Sensory sectors (adjacent to LGN, MGN, VP)
- Motor sector (adjacent to VL/VA)
- Limbic/prefrontal sector (adjacent to MD, AN)

Sensory TRN neurons show stronger burst discharge due to higher CaV3.3 
(T-type Ca²⁺ channel) expression. This heterogeneity enables:
- Rostral TRN: regulates sleep episode duration
- Caudal TRN: regulates sleep fragmentation
- Differential spindle generation across cortical areas
```

---

### Update 2: Frontal L5 → TRN Direct Pathway

**Current KB:** States only Layer VI projects to TRN (via collaterals).

**Research Update (Nat Neurosci 2022):**
- **Frontal cortex Layer 5** pyramidal cells establish **direct monosynaptic connections** with TRN
- This is **region-specific** — other cortical areas do NOT have L5→TRN connections
- Allows synchronous cortical events to synchronize multiple relay nuclei
- Spike output of L5-contacted TRN cells correlates with level of cortical synchrony

**Suggested Addition:**
```markdown
**Exception — Frontal L5→TRN Pathway:**
Unlike other cortical regions, frontal cortex Layer 5 pyramidal cells 
establish direct monosynaptic connections with TRN (Hádinger et al., 2022). 
This frontal L5–TRN pathway:
- Parallels L6–TRN projection but has distinct morphological/physiological features
- Allows synchronous cortical events to synchronize multiple relay nuclei
- Provides frontal cortex with unique top-down control over thalamic gating
```

---

### Update 3: Core/Matrix Classification (Optional Note)

**Current KB:** Uses driver/modulator + first-order/higher-order framework. Does NOT mention core/matrix.

**Research Status:** Core/matrix (Jones 1998) is an alternative framework:
- **Core cells:** Parvalbumin+, project to middle layers (L4), topographic
- **Matrix cells:** Calbindin+, project to superficial layers (L1), diffuse

**However — 2024 Critique (Sherman & Usrey, J Neurosci 2024):**
> "We suggest that no clear criteria for the matrix thalamus exist that distinguishes it from the core thalamus... We suggest that the core/matrix idea has lived beyond its sell-by date and should be abandoned."

**Recommendation:** Your KB's choice to use Sherman's framework is **validated by current research**. Add only a brief note:

```markdown
**Note on Alternative Classification:**
An alternative framework (Jones, 1998) classifies thalamic cells by 
calcium-binding proteins:
- Parvalbumin+ "core" cells → middle cortical layers (L4)
- Calbindin+ "matrix" cells → superficial layers (L1)

However, this classification is now controversial. Sherman & Usrey (2024) 
argue it should be abandoned in favor of the driver/modulator and 
first-order/higher-order frameworks, which are more functionally meaningful.
```
